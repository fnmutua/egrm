# 1. INTRODUCTION
This chapter sets out the purpose, scope, and usage rules for the procurement‑ready Technical Specifications Annex and its associated Technical Compliance Matrix. The intent is to ensure that all bidders respond against the same baseline requirements and that evaluation and acceptance can be carried out in a defensible, measurable manner.
## 1.1 Purpose of this Annex
This annex consolidates the functional and non‑functional requirements for the KUSP2 electronic Grievance Redress Mechanism (e‑GRM) into a structured and testable specification. It is designed for use with Request for Quotation (RFQ) procurement and therefore emphasizes minimum mandatory requirements, objective verification methods, and clear evidence expectations.
## 1.2 Intended Use in Evaluation and Acceptance
Bidders shall respond to these specifications by completing the Technical Compliance Matrix in Chapter 7. During evaluation, the Client will determine responsiveness by checking compliance to mandatory requirements and reviewing supporting evidence. During implementation and commissioning, the same requirements will be used as the baseline for configuration verification, test planning, and acceptance decisions.
## 1.3 Reference Documents
The technical specifications in this annex are derived from, and shall be read together with, the wider KUSP2 e‑GRM procurement package. At minimum, the following documents provide authoritative context for requirements, workflows, and acceptance scenarios.
The reference documents include, but are not limited to, the items below.
KUSP2 e‑GRM Terms of Reference (ToR), including the System Requirements Specification (Software + Hardware) annex.
KUSP2 e‑GRM Business Requirements Document (BRD).
KUSP2 e‑GRM System Design & Wireframe Report (for user journeys and interface expectations).
KUSP2 e‑GRM System Operation Manual and System Maintenance Manual (for operational expectations and maintainability).
Where a requirement is expressed in more than one document, the most specific and testable formulation shall apply. Any ambiguity identified by bidders shall be raised during the clarification period and addressed through a formal addendum where applicable.
## 1.4 Priority Levels and Interpretation
To support clear evaluation, each requirement is assigned a priority level. These priorities guide how the Client will interpret responsiveness and how bidders should indicate compliance in their response.
Priority levels are interpreted as follows.
**Must**: Mandatory requirement. Non‑compliance (or an unacceptable deviation) may render the quotation non‑responsive.
**Should**: Important requirement. The system is expected to meet the requirement; justified partial compliance may be accepted depending on impact and mitigation.
**Could**: Optional or value‑adding requirement. It is desirable and may be used to differentiate solutions where consistent with evaluation criteria.
Unless explicitly stated otherwise, requirements apply to the production environment and to any enabling configurations needed to support user acceptance testing (UAT) and go‑live. Channel‑dependent requirements (e.g., USSD or hotline integration) remain applicable, but their activation may be subject to Client‑led regulatory and commercial readiness; bidders must still demonstrate technical capability and provide a realistic integration plan.
# 2. SCOPE OF SUPPLY
This chapter defines what is being procured as the e‑GRM supply package. The scope is described in output terms (modules and deliverables) to remain technology‑neutral while ensuring that bidders price and implement comparable solutions.
## 2.1 Core Software Modules
The e‑GRM solution shall be supplied as an integrated set of modules that together provide a single system of record for KUSP2 grievances across national, county and municipal levels. The minimum software modules are described below.
At minimum, the supplied solution shall include the following core modules.
Public portal for grievance submission, guidance/knowledge resources, and complainant status tracking.
Staff console for assisted intake and case management (triage, assignment, action tracking, escalation, closure, and communications history).
Administration/configuration console to manage forms, workflows, reference data, roles/permissions, templates, and system settings.
Reporting and dashboard capability for operational and program‑level monitoring, including export functions.
Audit logging and evidence features to support oversight, traceability, and compliance reviews.
The modules above may be delivered as separate user interfaces or as a unified interface with role-based access. Regardless of user interface design, all modules shall operate on a single underlying grievance register and consistent data model.
## 2.2 Services and Supporting Deliverables
In addition to software, the procurement includes implementation and commissioning services required to make the e‑GRM operational within the agreed timeline. These services are essential to achieving a working system rather than a nominal software delivery.
The supporting services and deliverables include, at minimum, the items below.
Solution configuration (and development/customization where unavoidable) to implement the agreed workflows, data fields, routing rules, roles, and reports.
Integration enablement for agreed channels and interfaces, with testing evidence for each enabled integration.
Quality assurance and testing (functional, integration, regression, security, accessibility and performance as applicable) with documented evidence packs.
Training delivery and training materials sufficient for sustainable operation by NPCT and sub-national users.
Handover documentation and operational readiness deliverables (as-built documentation, configuration registers, support procedures).
The detailed technical deliverables and evidence requirements that support these services are specified in Chapter 5. All deliverables shall be provided in the formats and language requirements set out in the procurement package.

## 3.4 Multi‑Channel Intake and Communications
KUSP2 requires a practical multi‑channel approach to grievance intake and communications. The baseline requirement is that all grievances—regardless of channel—are captured into a single grievance register with consistent timestamps, routing and auditability. Some channels (such as USSD and hotline integration) may depend on third‑party readiness; however, bidders shall still demonstrate technical capability and provide a clear plan for integration and testing.

Email is used for notifications and, where enabled, may also serve as an intake channel. Email handling shall be reliable, auditable and protected against misuse. The requirements in Table 3‑3 define minimum email capabilities.

# 6. TESTING, ACCEPTANCE AND HANDOVER
This chapter consolidates the testing and acceptance approach for the e‑GRM procurement. While each requirement above includes specific acceptance criteria, the items below define the minimum acceptance checkpoints, evidence packs, and readiness conditions that apply across the solution.
## 6.1 Acceptance Checkpoints
Acceptance for the e‑GRM should be performed in checkpoints that align to configuration completion, testing completion, go‑live readiness, stabilization, and final handover. This staged approach reduces risk by ensuring that defects and gaps are identified early and resolved before production deployment.
The minimum acceptance checkpoints are described below.
Inception and requirements confirmation checkpoint: approval of implementation plan, validated requirements traceability, and confirmed hosting/integration dependencies.
Configured solution checkpoint: demonstration of configured workflows, forms, routing rules, roles/permissions, and draft reports in a non‑production environment.
Test completion checkpoint: completion of functional/integration/regression testing, with security and accessibility verification evidence proportionate to scope.
UAT checkpoint: Client-led UAT completed with defect log and sign‑off (or conditional sign‑off) recorded.
Go‑live readiness checkpoint: completion of go‑live checklist, monitoring and backup readiness, and cutover plan approval.
Hypercare exit checkpoint: stabilization period completed, critical incidents resolved, and transition to steady‑state support confirmed.
Final handover checkpoint: delivery and acceptance of the full handover package, including documentation, configuration registers, and data export capability.
Bidders shall confirm that their implementation approach supports these checkpoints and shall indicate any items they propose to treat as conditional due to Client or third‑party dependencies (e.g., USSD short code provisioning).
## 6.2 Minimum Evidence Pack at Acceptance
To ensure acceptance decisions are measurable and auditable, the Supplier shall provide an evidence pack. The evidence pack supplements demonstrations by providing durable records of configuration, testing, and compliance controls.
At minimum, the evidence pack shall include the elements below (tailored to the selected deployment model).
Architecture and environment inventory (logical and deployment diagrams).
Configuration register/workbook covering workflows, routing rules, roles, templates, SLAs and reference data.
Functional and integration test evidence (including channel tests where enabled).
Security configuration summary and vulnerability remediation summary.
Accessibility test summary for key public and staff journeys.
Backup configuration and restore test record.
Performance test summary (where required/appropriate).
UAT scripts, defect log, and UAT sign‑off record.
Go‑live readiness checklist and cutover plan.
Handover package and support model documentation.
The Client may request additional evidence where required for internal approvals, audits, or legal/regulatory compliance. The Supplier shall cooperate in providing such evidence, provided it is proportionate and within the agreed scope.
## 6.3 Acceptance Method and Verification Types
Each requirement is intended to be objectively verifiable. Verification may include (i) demonstration of working functionality, (ii) inspection of configuration settings, (iii) execution of tests (functional, integration, performance, security or accessibility), and (iv) document review of delivered evidence packs. Bidders shall indicate in their response whether compliance is achieved through configuration, customization, or third‑party components.
