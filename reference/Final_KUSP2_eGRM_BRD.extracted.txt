# 1. Introduction and Executive Summary
## 1.1 Background and Purpose of the e-GRM Project
The Second Kenya Urban Support Program (KUSP2) is a five-year Government of Kenya programme (2024–2028) implemented through the State Department for Housing and Urban Development (SDHUD) and its National Programme Coordination Team (NPCT). KUSP2 aims to strengthen urban institutions and improve service delivery across Kenya’s counties and municipalities. A robust Grievance Redress Mechanism (GRM) is therefore a core accountability and service-improvement tool under KUSP2, supporting transparency, citizen engagement and timely redress for project-affected persons and other stakeholders.
For the purposes of KUSP2, a “grievance” should be understood broadly as a concern, suggestion, request for information, allegation, or feedback related to KUSP2 activities and service delivery, submitted through any approved channel, including by a complainant who may choose to remain anonymous. This broader definition is important because the e-GRM must support not only complaint resolution, but also structured feedback loops and information responses that reduce recurring grievances and strengthen trust in public institutions.
KUSP2 is implementing an electronic Grievance Redress Mechanism (e-GRM) to complement existing county and municipal GRM structures. The e-GRM will provide a unified platform for grievance intake, tracking, assignment, resolution, and reporting, supporting multi-channel access (digital and assisted) and enabling program-wide monitoring.
In practical terms, the e-GRM is intended to function as a single system of record for KUSP2 grievances, while still enabling decentralized handling at county/municipal level through defined roles, routing, and escalation pathways. This means:
Complainants can submit grievances through appropriate channels (online or assisted), receive a reference number, and track progress where applicable.
County and municipal GRM focal persons/committees can register and process cases received through in-person or phone channels via assisted intake, ensuring that all grievances—digital or non-digital—are captured consistently in the same register.
NPCT/SDHUD can monitor case volumes, timeliness, and outcomes across jurisdictions, identify systemic issues, and meet program accountability and safeguards reporting obligations using standardized data and dashboards.
A key driver for the e-GRM is to reduce fragmentation and variability across counties/municipalities by introducing consistent workflows, service-level expectations, and minimum data standards, while protecting confidentiality—especially for sensitive grievances. In addition, the e-GRM supports KUSP2 commitments under the World Bank Environmental and Social Framework (including ESS10 on stakeholder engagement and disclosure) by enabling an accessible, transparent, and responsive grievance process.
## 1.2 Document Objective and Scope (Phase 2 Implementation)
This Business Requirements Document (BRD) defines the business, functional, non-functional, data, integration, and compliance requirements for the KUSP2 e-GRM for the Phase 2 implementation period (3 months). It is intended to be included with the Request for Quotations (RFQ) to inform qualified bidders of what must be delivered.
The BRD is written from the perspective of the Implementing Firm (the Supplier) and focuses on implementation requirements only. It defines required outcomes, capabilities and deliverables without prescribing specific technology platforms, implementation methodologies, tools, or solution architectures. This technology and platform neutrality is a core procurement principle: bidders may propose solutions and approaches, but they must meet the defined outcomes and acceptance expectations.
Accordingly, this BRD should be used by bidders to:
Understand the minimum mandatory (Must) outcomes for Phase 2 delivery, and the implementation services expected (configuration, integrations, testing, training, deployment, and handover).
Design an implementation plan and resourcing model that meets the three-month delivery window and supports successful adoption across national and sub-national users.
Prepare an evidence-based delivery approach, including testing artifacts, demonstrations, and documentation needed for acceptance and handover.
The Implementing Firm shall deliver a working, secure, accessible and operational e-GRM solution, including configuration, required development/customization, channel integrations, testing, training, deployment, and handover within the Phase 2 timeline. “Operational” in this context means that the solution is production-deployed in an approved environment, user access is provisioned, priority workflows and reports are functioning, and the Client has received sufficient documentation and knowledge transfer to sustain operations after go-live.
Where implementation dependencies exist—particularly for telecom-enabled channels such as SMS/USSD and toll-free hotline—the Implementing Firm must design integration and operations with practical fallbacks (e.g., assisted intake and alternative notification mechanisms), given that channel availability may depend on third-party providers and coverage.
## 1.3 Project Phases Overview (Phase 1: Design & Procurement; Phase 2: Implementation)
KUSP2 e-GRM activities are structured in two phases:
Phase 1 – Design and procurement preparation: definition of business requirements, workflows, standard data collection, stakeholder engagement planning, and preparation of procurement documentation and implementation inputs.
Phase 2 – Implementation and commissioning (this BRD’s focus): build/configure the e-GRM, enable required multi-channel interfaces, conduct testing and user acceptance, train users, deploy to the approved production environment, and complete handover for sustained operations.
Phase 1 outputs are formal implementation inputs. In particular, Phase 1 has produced (among others) validated requirements and workflows, a standardized intake/data collection form and data standards, reporting/KPI expectations, and key risk and compliance considerations that must be reflected in Phase 2 delivery planning and configuration.
Therefore, during Phase 2 mobilization, the Implementing Firm shall:
Review Phase 1 outputs as baseline inputs and confirm traceability between (a) the agreed requirements and workflows and (b) the system configuration, forms, queues, categories, SLAs, templates, and reports being delivered.
Validate practical implementation constraints early (e.g., hosting approvals, channel readiness, stakeholder availability for UAT and training), and incorporate mitigations into the implementation plan and delivery schedule.
Ensure the delivered system supports decentralized operations (county/municipal handling) while enabling national oversight, aggregation, and reporting through standardised data and governance controls.
Phase 1 activities themselves are not part of the Supplier’s scope; however, the Supplier is expected to implement the solution without redefining the agreed functional intent, using those Phase 1 outputs as binding inputs for configuration and delivery.
## 1.4 Executive Summary of Key Requirements and Deliverables
The Phase 2 implementation shall deliver, at minimum:
a) Multi-channel grievance intake and acknowledgement (web portal and assisted intake; with enabled integrations for email, SMS/USSD and hotline where applicable and available).
All channels must feed into a unified grievance register with consistent reference numbering and source/channel tagging to support operational routing and reporting.
Acknowledgement must be supported in a practical, channel-appropriate manner (e.g., confirmation page on web, scripted confirmation for assisted intake, and notifications where enabled), recognising that some channels depend on third-party readiness.
b) End-to-end grievance case management workflows (triage, assignment, investigation, resolution actions, escalation, closure and feedback).
Workflows must support multi-level handling (municipal/county/national) with clear routing and escalation triggers aligned to agreed governance roles.
The solution must preserve a complete and auditable case history from intake through closure, including actions taken and outcomes recorded.
c) Role-based access controls and audit logging to support confidentiality (including special handling for sensitive cases such as SEA/SH).
Access must be enforced on a least-privilege basis, and audit logs must enable accountability for key case and administrative actions.
Sensitive grievance handling must apply stricter confidentiality controls and safe workflow behaviours to reduce risk of inappropriate disclosure.
d) Standardized grievance intake data capture aligned to the Revised Standard Grievance Intake/Data Collection Form (KUSP2 e-GRM).
The Implementing Firm must implement the standardized data fields, validations, and reference data controls so that cases are comparable across counties/municipalities and suitable for aggregation and safeguards reporting.
Data quality controls should be practical for each channel (e.g., minimal mandatory fields for USSD while still meeting the standard dataset through staged capture in assisted follow-up, if required by the agreed design).
e) Dashboards and reports for operational management and compliance reporting (national and sub-national levels).
Reporting must support oversight of volumes, categories, timeliness/SLAs, escalation rates, and outcomes by geography and channel, enabling both local operational management and national-level monitoring.
Export capability is required for downstream analysis and programme reporting processes.
f) Administration tools for configuring categories, SLAs, notifications, user roles, reference data lists and content without software development.
Configuration must support sustainable operations beyond go-live (e.g., changing categories/help topics, adjusting SLA settings, updating templates, updating county/municipality reference lists) without dependence on the supplier for routine changes.
g) Compliance controls aligned to Kenya legal requirements (data protection, records retention, telecom) and World Bank safeguards.
The system must embed privacy-by-design principles appropriate to handling personal data and sensitive grievances, including consent-aware data capture and strict confidentiality controls where required.
The e-GRM must support the GRM’s credibility and accessibility obligations (including safeguards-related requirements for stakeholder engagement and responsiveness).
h) Implementation deliverables, training, documentation, testing evidence, go-live support and handover within 3 months.
The Supplier must plan and execute implementation with sufficient quality assurance and documentation to enable Client acceptance, UAT sign-off, and sustainable operations after commissioning.
Acceptance is evidence-driven: the Supplier is expected to provide demonstrations and testing evidence covering the implemented workflows and controls, consistent with the delivery expectations for Phase 2.
# 2. Business Context
## 2.1 Program Overview and GRM Context
KUSP2 supports urban development and institutional strengthening across Kenya, including performance-based intergovernmental transfers and technical assistance to counties and municipalities.
As a program with broad stakeholder interfaces and decentralized implementation, KUSP2 requires a GRM that is accessible, predictable, transparent, and credible across all participating jurisdictions and stakeholder groups.
Within this operating environment, grievances may arise from multiple program touchpoints, including (but not limited to): planning and prioritization processes, implementation of urban investments and services, contractor and service-provider performance, environmental and social risk management, citizen engagement processes, and integrity/accountability concerns. A robust GRM therefore functions as both:
A service channel for complainants to lodge concerns and receive timely redress; and
A management tool for program leadership to detect recurring issues, guide corrective actions, and strengthen institutional performance.
To serve this role effectively, the GRM must be designed to work across multiple levels of the program delivery chain—municipal, county, and national—with clear escalation pathways and standardized data capture to enable aggregation. In practice, this means the GRM must support:
Decentralized handling (most grievances should be resolved at the closest appropriate level);
Escalation and oversight (cases that breach timelines, require higher authority, or present heightened sensitivity must move to the right level promptly and with full traceability); and
Consistency across locations (common categories, minimum data standards, common workflow states, and shared reporting outputs).
The e-GRM will support KUSP2 commitments under the World Bank Environmental and Social Framework (including ESS10 on stakeholder engagement and information disclosure) and Government of Kenya requirements for public participation, accountability and access to information. This includes ensuring that stakeholders are informed about:
How to lodge a grievance and through which channels;
What timelines and service standards apply (acknowledgement, response, escalation, and closure expectations);
What confidentiality and non-retaliation protections are in place; and
How outcomes are communicated, including the right to appeal or escalate where applicable.
In addition, the e-GRM is intended to operate as a program-wide system of record that strengthens governance by ensuring grievances are: (i) logged consistently, (ii) assigned and actioned with accountability, and (iii) reportable in ways that support oversight and compliance requirements.
## 2.2 Business Need and Current Challenges
Existing grievance handling practices across counties and municipalities can be fragmented, largely paper-based or spread across informal channels. This creates challenges including:
Limited visibility of grievance status for complainants and program leadership.
In many contexts, complainants are unable to reliably confirm whether their grievance has been received, assigned, investigated, or resolved—especially when it is lodged through an informal channel (e.g., verbal reports, personal phone calls, ad hoc messages, or walk-ins without a formal register entry). From a program leadership perspective, limited visibility makes it difficult to:
Monitor backlog and timeliness;
Identify geographic or thematic hotspots;
Verify whether escalated cases are being actioned; and
Demonstrate accountability through auditable evidence.
Inconsistent data capture and classification, hindering aggregation and analysis across locations and themes.
Where counties and municipalities use different registers, templates, or categorization approaches, data cannot be reliably aggregated for program-wide reporting. Common issues include:
Missing key fields (location, category, channel, assignment, dates);
Non-standard categories (free-text classifications that cannot be analyzed consistently);
Duplicate records for the same issue when multiple channels are used; and
Limited ability to disaggregate (e.g., by county/municipality, intake channel, grievance type, or outcome).
This weakens KUSP2’s ability to use grievance data for learning and continuous improvement.
Delays in triage, assignment, escalation and closure, with limited service level monitoring.
Paper-based or informal handling often lacks systematic service-level target tracking, resulting in delayed acknowledgement, slow routing to responsible officers, and limited enforcement of escalation protocols when timelines are breached. Without automated or semi-automated controls (queues, reminders, overdue flags, escalation triggers), delays can accumulate and create:
Increased complainant dissatisfaction;
Higher reputational and political risk; and
Reduced confidence in program accountability processes.
Barriers to access for stakeholders with different levels of connectivity, literacy, language and disability.
A GRM that depends on a single channel (e.g., an online form only, or in-person only) risks excluding significant stakeholder segments. Barriers may include:
Limited internet access or device constraints (feature phones vs smartphones);
Digital literacy differences;
Language constraints for user-facing content; and
Accessibility gaps for persons with disabilities if interfaces are not designed to recognized accessibility standards.
These barriers can reduce uptake, increase inequity, and undermine KUSP2’s inclusion commitments.
Increased risk of confidentiality breaches for sensitive grievances if handled through non-secure channels.
Informal handling (e.g., uncontrolled email forwarding, shared messaging platforms, or open-access paper registers) increases the risk that personal data or sensitive grievances are seen by unauthorized persons. This is particularly critical for SEA/SH and other sensitive cases, where confidentiality must be enforced through need-to-know access, controlled notifications, and careful handling of narrative details. Privacy risk is also heightened when attachments and evidence are exchanged without proper controls.
These challenges collectively justify the business need for a unified e-GRM that standardizes intake, workflows, roles, auditability, reporting, and privacy controls—while still supporting assisted intake and operational realities at county and municipal levels. They also align with known delivery and operational risks that require proactive mitigation during Phase 2 implementation (e.g., adoption, telecom dependencies, privacy compliance, and capacity constraints).
## 2.3 Objectives of the KUSP2 e-GRM
Provide a unified, trackable grievance management platform from intake to closure across KUSP2 implementation levels.
The e-GRM is intended to serve as the single system of record for KUSP2 grievances, ensuring every grievance—regardless of channel—results in a logged case with:
A unique reference number for tracking;
Clear ownership (assigned officer/unit/team);
Standard workflow states and timestamps;
A documented action history (notes, tasks, decisions, and communications); and
A closure record that captures outcomes and (where applicable) complainant feedback.
This unification also supports structured escalation across municipal → county → national levels with traceability.
Enable multi-channel access and assisted intake so that all stakeholders can lodge grievances without cost barriers.
The e-GRM must be inclusive by design, supporting both digital and assisted access. In operational terms, this includes:
A public web portal for self-service submission and status tracking;
Assisted intake for grievances received via phone calls, in-person visits, letters, or other locally used channels; and
Enabled integrations for email, SMS/USSD and hotline where applicable and available, ensuring these channels feed into the same unified register.
Assisted intake is not a fallback; it is a core mechanism to ensure equitable access in contexts with limited connectivity or literacy constraints.
Support timely response and resolution aligned to KUSP2 service standards (including acknowledgement on submission, response within program timelines, and resolution targets where feasible).
The e-GRM should operationalize service standards by embedding timeliness controls into workflows, including:
Automated or prompt-based acknowledgement mechanisms;
Configurable service-level targets (e.g., acknowledgement, response, resolution) tied to timestamps;
Overdue tracking and escalation triggers; and
Management dashboards that highlight bottlenecks, backlogs, and repeated breaches.
This ensures that timeliness is not only a policy statement but a measurable operational output.
Provide reliable, disaggregable data for management decisions, safeguards monitoring and reporting.
The e-GRM should produce consistent data suitable for operational and compliance reporting, including disaggregation by:
Geography (county, municipality, and other configured levels);
Channel/source of intake;
Category/type (including safeguards-linked categories where relevant);
Status/outcome; and
Timeliness (acknowledgement, response, resolution, overdue).
This supports evidence-based management, trend analysis, and corrective action planning at both national and sub-national levels.
Ensure compliance with Kenya legal requirements (data protection, access to information, records management) and World Bank safeguards, including special handling for SEA/SH and other sensitive grievances.
The e-GRM must implement privacy- and security-by-design controls appropriate for handling personal data, including:
Role-based access control and audit logging;
Consent capture where personal data is collected;
Controlled handling of attachments and evidence;
Configurable retention/archiving aligned to applicable requirements; and
“Need-to-know” workflows and restricted reporting for sensitive cases (including SEA/SH) to prevent unauthorized access and inappropriate disclosure.
Compliance is not limited to technical controls; it also depends on clear procedures, user training, and operational governance.
Collectively, these objectives frame the e-GRM as both a service delivery mechanism for complainants and a program management instrument that strengthens accountability, safeguards compliance, and institutional performance.
## 2.4 Expected Benefits and Outcomes
Improved transparency and accountability through consistent tracking, auditability and reporting.
With standardized registration, reference numbering, and audit logs, stakeholders can trace: who received a grievance, what actions were taken, when decisions were made, and what outcome was communicated. This strengthens internal accountability and improves the credibility of the GRM as a trusted mechanism.
Better complainant experience through clear acknowledgement, status updates and predictable workflows.
Complainants benefit from:
Confirmation that their grievance has been received;
A reference number for follow-up;
A clear understanding of expected steps and timelines; and
More consistent communication throughout the lifecycle (request for additional information, resolution notification, closure confirmation, and satisfaction capture where applicable).
Predictability and responsiveness are essential to sustaining trust and encouraging appropriate use of the GRM.
Improved management oversight through dashboards, SLA monitoring and escalation triggers.
Supervisors and program leadership gain a real-time (or near real-time) view of:
Volumes by county/municipality and category;
Backlogs and overdue cases;
Workload distribution by unit/assignee;
Escalations and reopened cases; and
Timeliness performance against configured service levels.
This enables early intervention, targeted capacity support, and evidence-driven management decisions.
Reduced operational burden through configurable templates, knowledge resources and automation of routine communications.
Standardized forms, routing rules, and reusable templates reduce repetitive work and improve consistency. Knowledge resources (FAQs and guidance content) help prevent avoidable grievances and reduce inbound inquiries by addressing common questions proactively. Automation—used appropriately and with privacy safeguards—supports timely acknowledgements, reminders, and workload management.
Stronger compliance posture through privacy controls, access management, audit logs and retention policies.
By embedding privacy and security controls into day-to-day operations, the e-GRM reduces exposure to data breaches and confidentiality failures, especially for sensitive cases. Structured retention/archiving settings and export capabilities also strengthen compliance with records management and lawful reporting requirements, while ensuring the Client retains ownership and control of grievance data.
Overall, the expected outcome is a functioning e-GRM that improves access, timeliness, and accountability at scale—while remaining practical for decentralized use and sustainable under the operational capacities of national, county, and municipal stakeholders.
# 3. Stakeholder Profiles

The e-GRM serves multiple stakeholder groups spanning national, county/municipal and community levels. Stakeholder engagement and clear role definition are critical to successful Phase 2 implementation and adoption. The system must be implemented in a way that preserves existing GRM institutional arrangements while providing a unified digital “system of record” for KUSP2 grievances, end-to-end traceability, and program-wide reporting.
For Phase 2 implementation, stakeholder profiling is not only a communications activity; it is an operational and governance requirement. The Implementing Firm shall use stakeholder profiles to:
Confirm who will use the system, how they will use it, and what authorizations they require (role-based access control, segregation of duties, confidentiality).
Map key workflows and escalation pathways across implementation levels (municipality → county → national), including “handoffs” and points where delays typically occur (triage, assignment, investigation coordination, approvals, closure).
Plan training, onboarding, and user acceptance testing (UAT) using representative users from each stakeholder group, including contexts with connectivity constraints and vulnerable groups.
Ensure that compliance obligations (data protection, recordkeeping, accessibility, sensitive case handling) are operationalized through both configuration and standard operating procedures (SOPs), not left to informal practice.

## 3.2 National-Level Stakeholders
National stakeholders include SDHUD/NPCT leadership, national GRM coordination staff, safeguards specialists, ICT and data protection focal persons, monitoring and evaluation (M&E) staff, and communications teams. These stakeholders define program-wide standards, oversee aggregated reporting, and manage escalated grievances.
To enable consistent national governance and reduce operational ambiguity during Phase 2, national stakeholder roles should be reflected in the e-GRM through distinct role profiles and responsibilities, including at minimum:
a) Governance and policy ownership (NPCT leadership / GRM coordination):
Define and approve national GRM operational standards that the e-GRM enforces through configuration (e.g., standard statuses, escalation levels, service level targets, closure requirements).
Approve the standardized classification scheme (categories/help topics and definitions) and ensure consistent use across counties/municipalities.
Define escalation thresholds and receiving authorities at national level (e.g., cases escalated due to time breach, complexity, integrity concerns, or multi-jurisdictional issues).
Oversee implementation governance decisions, including configuration change approvals where changes affect national comparability of data.
b) Safeguards and sensitive-case oversight (E&S specialists and designated focal persons):
Ensure the e-GRM configuration supports safeguards-aligned workflows, including confidential handling and restricted access for sensitive grievances (including SEA/SH).
Validate that reporting outputs for sensitive cases are aggregated and privacy-preserving by default, with exports restricted to explicitly authorized users.
Support training content related to ethical handling, confidentiality, and “need-to-know” access.
c) M&E and reporting stakeholders:
Define national dashboards and reporting packs needed for performance monitoring (e.g., volumes, timeliness, overdue rates, resolution outcomes, escalations, geographic distribution).
Validate data disaggregation requirements (county, municipality, intake channel, category/type, status) and confirm export formats required for program reporting and audits.
Use the e-GRM outputs to identify systemic issues, recurring themes, and capacity gaps requiring management action.
d) ICT, security, and data protection stakeholders:
Provide (or approve) hosting environment requirements and access arrangements; confirm operational readiness expectations (backup, restore, monitoring, admin access).
Approve security configurations (RBAC structures, MFA/2FA settings where enabled, audit log access and retention settings) and verify evidence of security testing.
Confirm that consent capture and privacy notice content are implemented on public and assisted intake channels, and that the system supports secure data handling practices (including controlled attachment access).
e) Communications stakeholders:
Ensure public-facing guidance content is clear, consistent, and aligned to KUSP2 GRM messaging (how to submit, what to expect, timelines in general terms, confidentiality and non-retaliation assurances, and status tracking using reference numbers).
Coordinate awareness activities, including pre-launch messaging and post go-live sensitization, ensuring the e-GRM complements rather than confuses existing GRM channels.
Implementation requirement for Phase 2: The Implementing Firm shall ensure national users are represented in UAT and that national reporting dashboards, audit log access, and administrative configuration functions are demonstrably usable by designated NPCT roles prior to go-live.
## 3.3 County and Municipal Stakeholders
County and municipal stakeholders include County Programme Coordination Teams (CPCTs), municipal boards and designated GRM focal persons/committees. These stakeholders are primary users of the case management functions, and are responsible for intake (including assisted intake), investigation, resolution actions, and closure within their jurisdictions.
Given the decentralized operating model, sub-national stakeholders will interact with the e-GRM in roles that typically include:
a) GRM focal persons / grievance handlers (operational users):
Receive grievances through the e-GRM queues and/or register grievances received through assisted channels (in-person, phone) into the system using the standardized dataset.
Conduct triage (validate completeness, confirm location, classify grievance category/type, identify responsible unit), and assign to appropriate handler(s) or teams.
Record investigation actions, communications, referrals, and evidence within the case record (including structured action tracking where configured).
Draft responses and closure summaries using approved templates and appropriate language, ensuring complainant communications are consistent and privacy-aware.
b) Supervisors / municipal or county GRM committee members (oversight users):
Monitor operational queues (open, overdue, escalated), ensure timely action, and address bottlenecks.
Validate resolution adequacy and documentation completeness prior to closure (e.g., mandatory fields populated, outcome recorded, complainant informed where possible).
Manage escalations within county/municipality (e.g., transfer between departments, elevation to senior authority) and approve escalation to national where required by workflow rules or program protocols.
c) County/Municipal technical and administrative support users:
Support user onboarding, access provisioning, and basic troubleshooting as part of local adoption.
Coordinate with NPCT and the Implementing Firm during rollout for training schedules, UAT execution, and feedback loops.
d) Operational constraints that must be accommodated:
Variability in connectivity, devices, staffing levels, and prior experience with digital systems.
Need for assisted intake processes for community members who cannot submit digitally, ensuring that assisted intake does not reduce accessibility or confidentiality.
Need for consistent categorization and data quality to support county-level and national-level reporting.
Phase 2 implementation expectations:
The Implementing Firm shall onboard and train a representative set of county/municipal users (including GRM focal persons and supervisors) and support them through pilot/UAT activities.
The e-GRM configuration shall support sub-national workload management (queues, assignment rules, due dates, overdue visibility) and facilitate routine reporting for local management, not only national reporting.
## 3.4 Community/Public Stakeholders and Inclusion Considerations
The e-GRM must be usable by diverse community members, including those with limited digital access or literacy. The stakeholder engagement plan requires multiple access channels and inclusive communications. The system and implementation activities shall support:
Language accessibility (at minimum English and Kiswahili for user-facing components).
Accessibility for persons with disabilities (e.g., compatibility with assistive technologies and accessible design).
Options for anonymous or confidential reporting, particularly for sensitive grievances.
Assisted intake processes for in-person or telephone grievance lodging where digital access is limited.
To operationalize inclusion in Phase 2 (beyond stating requirements), the Implementing Firm shall ensure that inclusion considerations are reflected in design choices, configuration decisions, content, and training, including:
a) Channel strategy for equitable access (digital and assisted):
Ensure the web portal is mobile-friendly and usable on low-bandwidth connections (simple layouts, minimal page weight, clear form validation).
Ensure assisted intake screens mirror the standardized dataset so that grievances received by phone or in-person are registered consistently and are not treated as “second-class” entries.
Where USSD/hotline/SMS integrations are enabled and available, ensure they support short, clear prompts and minimal data capture appropriate to those channels while preserving required fields through follow-up where necessary.
b) Practical accessibility measures:
Ensure public-facing journeys (submit grievance, check status, view process overview) are navigable with keyboard-only operation and compatible with assistive technologies.
Ensure error messages, form labels, and required field indicators are clear and do not rely only on colour or complex wording.
Ensure downloadable/printable guidance exists for offline dissemination (e.g., posters, one-page “how to submit” guides) to support those without continuous internet access.
c) Safety, confidentiality, and trust-building:
Provide clear public messaging on confidentiality expectations, non-retaliation assurances, and the right to seek other remedies, in plain language.
Implement anonymous submission options and configure communications so that no sensitive information is exposed in insecure channels by default.
Ensure sensitive grievance handling workflows restrict access and avoid unnecessary narrative capture in free text, supporting safe referral pathways through structured fields and designated focal persons.
d) Community engagement linkage (implementation support):
The Implementing Firm shall support NPCT communications and sensitization by providing demo scripts and role-based “walkthroughs” that can be used in community-facing sessions.
The Implementing Firm shall incorporate inclusion-focused test participants (where feasible) into accessibility testing and UAT validation, so issues are identified before go-live.
These inclusion requirements align with known adoption and equity risks for GRM systems (e.g., exclusion of vulnerable groups, low uptake where access barriers exist) and therefore should be treated as delivery-critical, not optional enhancements.

# 4. System Scope
## 4.1 In-Scope Functionality and Processes
Phase 2 scope covers implementation and commissioning of the KUSP2 e-GRM to support end-to-end grievance handling across the program. The Phase 2 scope is defined around delivery of an operational “single system of record” for KUSP2 grievances, capable of receiving grievances from approved channels, routing them to the correct handling level (municipal/county/national), tracking actions and timelines, protecting confidentiality, and producing auditable reporting outputs.
In scope:
A public-facing interface for grievance submission, information access and status tracking.
The Implementing Firm shall provide a public-facing interface that enables KUSP2 stakeholders to:
Lodge new grievances through a guided and user-friendly process (with clear field prompts and validation).
Receive immediate confirmation that the grievance has been received, including a unique reference number and clear next steps.
Access GRM information resources (e.g., process overview, FAQs/knowledge resources, contact details, and key assurances such as confidentiality and non-retaliation).
Track grievance status using a grievance reference number and an appropriate verification factor (e.g., email/phone or other agreed credential), without exposing personal data to unauthorized users.
Complete submissions in a manner that supports inclusion, including mobile-friendly layouts and clear language.
Capture consent to personal data use where personal data is provided, and present an approved privacy notice (content supplied/approved by the Client).
Assisted grievance intake for grievances received via phone, in-person, and other assisted channels.
The e-GRM shall support staff-assisted lodging of grievances on behalf of complainants, including:
A staff intake screen mirroring the standardized intake form fields, including mandatory/optional fields and validation rules.
Ability to register grievances from walk-in complainants, call-based complainants, letters, and other assisted mechanisms, with a clear source/channel attribute recorded for each case.
Support for anonymous submissions via assisted intake (i.e., complainants may opt not to provide identifying information).
Practical operational controls, such as scripts or prompts for call-handlers/intake officers (to be included in training/job aids), and clear guidance on what information should not be captured in free text for sensitive cases.
Ability to generate and communicate the grievance reference number to the complainant during assisted intake (verbally, via SMS/email where enabled, or through printed acknowledgement where applicable).
Grievance case management workflows: triage, assignment, investigation, action tracking, escalation, closure and reopening/appeal handling.
The Implementing Firm shall configure workflows that support consistent handling across counties/municipalities while allowing controlled operational flexibility, including:
Triage and classification: capture/confirm category, location, priority/severity, sensitivity flag (where applicable), and assignment routing attributes immediately after intake.
Assignment and accountability: assignment to a responsible unit (municipal/county/national) and/or individual/team, with traceable ownership and ability to transfer/reassign with audit trail.
Investigation and action tracking: capability to record actions taken, investigation notes (with internal-only visibility where applicable), tasks linked to the grievance, and supporting evidence/attachments.
Escalation: escalation pathways across levels (municipal → county → national) based on configured triggers such as overdue timelines, grievance severity, inability to resolve at current level, or complainant dissatisfaction.
Resolution and closure: structured capture of resolution outcome/summary, dates, communication of outcome to complainant (or reason not communicated, e.g., anonymous/unreachable), and complainant satisfaction (where available).
Reopening/appeals: controlled reopening or appeal workflow when a complainant is not satisfied, including re-assignment to a review authority and documentation of review decisions.
Auditability of lifecycle: every key state change (created, assigned, escalated, resolved, closed, reopened) must be logged with actor and timestamp.
Configurable service level targets (e.g., acknowledgement, response and resolution timelines) with overdue monitoring and escalation.
The Implementing Firm shall configure service level targets that support KUSP2 service standards and reporting needs, including:
Separate targets for acknowledgement, first response, and resolution/closure (where feasible and defined by the program).
Working schedules (business hours, weekends/holidays) where required to compute due dates consistently.
Visual overdue indicators and operational queues (e.g., “Overdue”, “Due soon”) for staff.
Configurable escalation triggers that notify supervisors and/or move cases to escalation queues where targets are breached.
Notifications to complainants and staff via configured channels (email/SMS where enabled).
The system shall support automated and manual communications that are:
Template-based and configurable by administrators (including English/Kiswahili versions for public-facing templates, as applicable).
Respectful of complainant preferences and consent (e.g., preferred contact method where provided).
“Privacy-safe” by design: messages via SMS/email should avoid sensitive personal data by default and include only minimum necessary information (e.g., reference number, general status, next action).
Logged against the grievance record (what was sent, to whom, when, by which channel), supporting transparency and audit.
Role-based access control and auditability, including privacy controls for sensitive grievances (e.g., SEA/SH).
In scope are confidentiality and governance controls commensurate with handling personal data and sensitive grievances, including:
Role-based permissions aligned to national/county/municipal operating structures (least-privilege access).
Record-level access control (e.g., users view only cases within their jurisdiction/assignment and permitted categories).
Enhanced confidentiality handling for sensitive cases (including SEA/SH), such as restricted visibility to designated roles, suppression/redaction of notifications, and controlled reporting outputs.
Comprehensive audit logs capturing access and key actions (including administrative changes), with export capability for compliance and internal oversight.
Dashboards and reports at national and sub-national levels; export capability for analysis and reporting.
The Implementing Firm shall deliver reporting outputs that support:
Operational management: workload, open vs closed, due/overdue, assignment distribution, and queue health.
Program monitoring and safeguards reporting: volumes and trends by category, geography, channel, timeliness metrics, escalation rates, and outcomes.
Disaggregation: ability to filter and group by county/municipality and other configured location levels, grievance category/type, channel/source, status, and time period.
Exports: non-proprietary exports (e.g., CSV/XLSX/PDF as appropriate) suitable for analysis and formal reporting, governed by access controls (especially for sensitive cases).
Administration/configuration tools, including configuration of categories, reference data lists, templates, users/roles, and reporting without software development.
Phase 2 includes provision and configuration of administrative capabilities enabling Client teams to sustain operations without ongoing vendor dependence, including:
Management of grievance categories/help topics and mapping to routing rules.
Management of reference data lists (e.g., counties/municipalities, project contexts, status codes, escalation levels).
Management of notification templates and public portal content pages.
User management, role definition, team structures, and permission assignment.
Configuration of SLA plans, schedules, escalation rules, queues, and dashboards.
Configuration change logging/versioning (at minimum via audit logs and maintained configuration records).
Implementation services: configuration/development, integrations, testing, training, deployment, documentation and handover.
Phase 2 includes all professional services required to deliver a working system within three months, including:
Mobilization, requirements confirmation against Phase 1 inputs, and implementation planning.
Environment setup (as approved) and solution configuration/customization necessary to meet “Must” requirements.
Channel integrations that are enabled and available during Phase 2 (and technical readiness for those dependent on Client-led commercial/regulatory arrangements).
Testing (functional, integration, regression, performance, security, accessibility) with evidence packs.
User training and change support (NPCT and selected county/municipal users), plus administrator training for sustainment.
Deployment to approved production environment, cutover support, stabilization/hypercare, and formal handover of documentation and artifacts.

# 5. Functional Requirements
This section defines the required functional capabilities of the e-GRM for Phase 2 implementation and commissioning. Requirements are organized by major functional components that collectively enable a complete grievance lifecycle from intake to closure, with monitoring and reporting for operational control and compliance.
Requirement priorities are indicated as Must / Should / Could. The Implementing Firm shall implement all Must requirements within Phase 2, and shall demonstrate compliance through configuration evidence, test results, and user acceptance outcomes.
General implementation principles applicable across all functional requirements (applies to Section 5 as a whole):
Single system of record: All enabled channels (web, assisted intake, email, USSD, hotline) shall create or update records within one unified grievance register, using a consistent reference numbering approach and a consistent lifecycle status model.
Data consistency and validation: Mandatory fields and validation rules (as defined in Section 7) shall be applied consistently, with channel-appropriate adaptations (e.g., USSD minimal fields). Data quality controls shall include required-field enforcement, controlled vocabulary lists (reference data), and structured classification for routing/reporting.
Privacy-by-design and confidentiality: Functional design shall minimize unnecessary personal data capture, protect confidentiality via access controls and audit logs, and avoid disclosure of sensitive information through insecure channels (e.g., SMS). Sensitive grievances require enhanced handling and restricted workflows.
Configuration-first delivery: Where the BRD requires configurability, the Implementing Firm shall implement via administrative configuration rather than custom code, unless customization is unavoidable to meet a Must requirement.
Evidence-based acceptance: All Must requirements shall be verifiable through clear acceptance criteria, test scripts, and demonstration scenarios agreed with NPCT and selected county/municipal users.
## 5.1 Multi-Channel Grievance Intake and Lodging
The e-GRM shall support multiple grievance intake channels to ensure accessibility for diverse stakeholders, including those with limited connectivity or literacy, and those requiring assisted intake.
All intake channels shall result in:
Creation (or update) of a grievance record with a unique reference number;
Capture of mandatory data fields appropriate to that channel (per Section 7);
An acknowledgement to the complainant via the originating channel or an agreed alternative channel (respecting consent and contact preferences); and
Clear identification of source channel, intake date/time, and responsible intake office/user for auditability.
Operational guidance (non-normative): Intake processes should reduce duplicate submissions by (a) enabling staff to search for existing grievances by complainant contact details and keywords, and (b) using messaging that discourages repeated submissions while status tracking is available.
### 5.1.1 Web Portal Grievance Submission and Tracking
The public portal shall provide self-service access for grievance submission, access to information resources, and status tracking using a grievance reference number. # 6. Non-Functional Requirements
Non-functional requirements define the quality attributes and operational constraints that the KUSP2 e-GRM must satisfy in addition to the functional capabilities described in Section 5. These requirements ensure that the system is usable and inclusive for the public, reliable and efficient for staff, secure for personal and sensitive data, and sustainable for long-term program operations.
The Implementing Firm shall ensure that the delivered e-GRM meets the non-functional requirements in this section, and shall provide objective evidence of compliance as part of acceptance (including test plans, test results, and configuration evidence as applicable). These requirements apply across all implemented channels (web portal, assisted intake, email, and enabled integrations such as SMS/USSD/hotline) and across all environments used in Phase 2 (test/staging/production as approved).
## 6.1 Usability and Accessibility
The e-GRM user experience shall support diverse users, including: (i) members of the public lodging grievances through self-service channels, (ii) staff users handling cases at municipal, county and national levels, and (iii) administrators responsible for configuration and reporting. The user experience must be designed to reduce barriers to access, ensure consistency in data capture, and enable efficient processing within defined timelines.
Key usability and accessibility considerations that shall be implemented and validated include:
Public-facing simplicity and clarity
Public-facing pages shall use plain-language content and clear prompts that explain the purpose of the GRM, how submissions are handled, expected timelines, and how to track status using a reference number.
Forms shall be designed to minimize cognitive load (e.g., logical grouping of fields, progressive disclosure where appropriate, clear required/optional indicators).
Error handling shall be user-friendly and actionable (i.e., users are clearly told what to correct and how).
Mobile-first and low-bandwidth usability
The public portal shall be responsive and usable on common smartphones and feature-phone-adjacent browsers.
Page weight should be minimized (avoid unnecessary heavy scripts/media); the portal should remain usable under intermittent connectivity, with clear messaging if a submission fails.
Inclusive access
User-facing components shall support, at minimum, English and Kiswahili, with the ability to add additional languages in future via configuration/translation workflows.
The system shall support accessible content patterns (readable font sizes, headings, descriptive labels, accessible validation messages, and non-color-only status indicators).
Assistive technology compatibility
Forms, navigation, and key workflows shall be compatible with screen readers and support keyboard-only navigation.
Focus order, skip links (where applicable), and semantic structure shall enable predictable navigation for users with disabilities.
Operational usability for staff
Staff consoles shall support efficient handling at volume through well-designed queues, filters, search, and workflow prompts.
The system should reduce manual repetition via configurable templates, configurable default values, and automation (e.g., acknowledgements, routing rules) while maintaining safeguards for sensitive cases.
The Implementing Firm shall include usability and accessibility validation as part of the testing approach and UAT support, including the remediation of critical accessibility barriers before go-live.
## 6.2 Performance and Scalability
The e-GRM shall perform reliably under expected load across national and sub-national users, and shall be scalable as usage expands across participating counties/municipalities and as grievance volumes grow.
Performance and scalability requirements shall be implemented with attention to:
Operational peak periods and reporting cycles
The system must remain responsive during periods of high intake (e.g., when community sensitization drives increased submissions) and during reporting cycles when dashboards/exports may be heavily used.
Search and reporting performance
Search, filtering, and dashboard generation shall be designed to remain responsive under realistic data volumes (including growth over time), and avoid “time-out” conditions that would undermine adoption.
Scalable storage
The system shall support growth in attachments and case history without degrading core performance. Storage and database growth shall be managed through scalable architecture and governance controls (e.g., retention/archiving as defined in data requirements).
Capacity planning evidence
The Implementing Firm shall document baseline sizing assumptions (concurrent users, expected monthly grievance volumes, attachment volumes, and growth expectations) and demonstrate—through testing and/or architecture evidence—that the solution can sustain those assumptions in the approved hosting environment.
## 6.3 Security and Privacy
The e-GRM will handle personally identifiable information (PII) and may handle sensitive grievances (including SEA/SH). Accordingly, the solution shall implement security controls commensurate with risk, incorporating “privacy-by-design” and “security-by-design” principles across the application, infrastructure, integrations, and operational procedures.
Minimum expectations include:
Identity and access management
Strong authentication for staff users (with optional/enabled MFA/2FA as required).
Role-based access control (RBAC) implementing least privilege, including separation between day-to-day grievance handling and administrative functions.
Record-level access restrictions based on department/team/assignment and restricted workflows for sensitive cases.
Secure communications and data protection
Encryption of data in transit (TLS/HTTPS) and appropriate protection for stored data and attachments (including controlled access, audit logging, and secure storage practices).
Controlled handling of sensitive content in notifications, ensuring that unprotected channels (e.g., SMS) do not expose sensitive details.
Auditability and monitoring
Logging of key events and actions (including administrative changes and sensitive record access) to support accountability and investigations.
Security monitoring readiness (e.g., ability to identify failed login attempts, unusual access patterns, and export/download events).
Incident readiness
Operational procedures and documentation to support incident response, including roles, escalation pathways, and evidence availability (logs, configuration records, and deployment records).
## 6.4 Availability, Backup and Disaster Recovery
The e-GRM is a public-facing accountability channel and must be available and resilient. The Implementing Firm shall implement availability and continuity controls appropriate for the approved hosting model.
Minimum expectations include:
Availability targets and maintenance controls
Availability targets (including planned maintenance windows) shall be clearly stated and aligned with the program’s service needs.
Offline/maintenance mode functionality shall provide a clear public message when the system is unavailable, including alternative instructions where applicable.
Backup and restore
Regular backups of application data and configuration shall be implemented.
Restoration procedures shall be documented and tested (at least once prior to go-live or during hypercare), with evidence provided.
Disaster recovery planning
A proportionate DR approach shall be defined based on hosting arrangements (government data centre and/or approved cloud), including agreed Recovery Time Objective (RTO) and Recovery Point Objective (RPO) targets and practical steps to execute recovery.
## 6.5 Maintainability and Extensibility
The solution shall be maintainable by designated administrators and support teams after Phase 2 handover. The system should be extensible to add channels, modify forms and categories, and evolve reporting needs without major redevelopment.
Maintainability and extensibility requirements include:
Configuration over customization
Routine changes (forms, reference lists, routing rules, notification templates, SLA schedules) should be possible via administrative configuration.
Where custom development is required, it must be documented, testable, and maintainable, with handover artifacts provided as per Phase 2 deliverables.
Change control and safe updates
Configuration changes should be auditable and support rollback where feasible (or clearly documented recovery steps where rollback is not supported).
The Implementing Firm shall structure environments and release management to reduce the risk of production disruption (e.g., testing in staging before production updates).
Operational documentation
The Implementing Firm shall provide administrator-oriented documentation sufficient for sustained operations (user management, configuration, reporting, backup/restore, and common troubleshooting scenarios).

# 7. Data Requirements
This section defines data requirements for the e-GRM, including core entities, required data fields for grievance intake, classification schemes, and data retention/migration considerations. Data requirements shall be implemented in a manner consistent with data protection principles (data minimization, purpose limitation, and confidentiality).
In Phase 2, the Implementing Firm shall implement a coherent data model that supports (i) consistent capture across all intake channels, (ii) end-to-end case management and auditability, and (iii) reliable reporting at national and sub-national levels. Data design shall prioritize the following cross-cutting principles:
Single system of record: all grievances and related actions (messages, assignments, escalations, tasks, attachments) are linked to one authoritative grievance record.
Traceability by design: each change to key fields (status, assignee, category, SLA/due date) is time-stamped, attributable to a user/role, and reflected in the audit trail.
Channel consistency: data captured via web, assisted intake, email, USSD, and hotline (where enabled) must map to the same standardized dataset, even if some channels capture a reduced “minimum dataset” and are completed later by staff.
Privacy-by-design: only the minimum personal data required for processing is collected and retained, sensitive details are minimized and protected, and access is restricted to a need-to-know basis.
## 7.1 Core Data Entities and Structure
At minimum, the e-GRM shall support the core data entities below to enable end-to-end grievance processing and reporting.
In implementing these entities, the Implementing Firm shall ensure:
Stable identifiers: each entity shall have a stable internal identifier (system ID) in addition to any human-readable number (e.g., grievance reference number). Human-readable numbers should be unique and configurable, while internal IDs should remain immutable.
Standard timestamps: entities shall consistently store created_at, updated_at, and (where relevant) closed_at, resolved_at, archived_at, and deleted_at/anonymized_at timestamps, including timezone context per system configuration.
Actor attribution: key operational actions (create, update, assign, transfer, close, reopen, export) shall record the initiating user/role and (where available) source information (channel, IP address for web/admin actions, integration identifier for system-to-system events).
Field governance and history: where reference data (e.g., categories, locations) changes over time, the system shall preserve historical selections for existing grievances to ensure reporting consistency.

## 7.2 Complainant Information (Personal Data and Consent)
The system shall support complainant information capture where provided, while also allowing anonymous submission. Personal data shall be collected only as necessary for grievance handling, with explicit consent where required, and protected through access controls and audit logging.
To operationalize these requirements, the Implementing Firm shall ensure the e-GRM supports:
Anonymous reporting as a first-class pathway: anonymous grievances shall be registerable and processable without forcing entry of personal identifiers. The system shall clearly reflect the constraints of anonymous cases (e.g., limited ability to provide updates) and support staff workflows accordingly.
Consent capture and evidence: for grievances where personal data is provided, the system shall store a clear record of consent that includes (at minimum) timestamp, channel, the consent statement/version displayed, and the user action that indicated consent (e.g., checkbox, recorded assent during assisted intake).
Representative submissions: where a representative submits on behalf of another person/group, the system shall capture representative details and authorization indicators (including “consent of affected person” where applicable), and shall ensure that communications are aligned with the agreed contact person and contact method.
Contact preference enforcement: where a preferred method of contact is captured, the system should support aligning outbound communications to that preference, subject to channel availability and privacy controls (e.g., avoid sensitive details via SMS).
Access restriction for personal data: staff visibility of complainant personal data shall be role-based and consistent with confidentiality needs, particularly for sensitive grievances and for cases where the complainant requests confidentiality.

## 7.3 Grievance Details Data Requirements
The e-GRM shall capture sufficient information to understand, categorize and address the grievance, including location, issue description, affected program component, and any supporting evidence/attachments. Field-level requirements are defined in the standardized intake dataset (Section 7.2.1).
To improve the usefulness of grievance detail data for triage, resolution, and reporting, the Implementing Firm shall ensure:
Issue summary vs detailed narrative: the system supports a short “summary/subject” field for queue visibility and dashboards, while retaining a longer description for full context.
Structured location capture: county and municipality are mandatory as per the standardized dataset; the system should encourage additional location specificity (site/area) through guided prompts and consistent text entry, while avoiding excessive complexity for public users.
Evidence handling: attachments should be linked clearly to the grievance and, where applicable, to a specific thread entry or task; staff should be able to describe the relevance of attachments through notes without duplicating sensitive content.
Linkage to project context: the “project context” field shall support classification by KUSP2 component/location/context and enable program-wide aggregation; where project context values change over time, historical values already used in grievances should remain reportable.
## 7.4 Classification Scheme and Taxonomy
The system shall support configurable classification of grievances to enable routing and reporting. Classification schemes shall include, at minimum:
Geography: county, municipality, and other relevant location levels captured in the intake form.
Channel/source of grievance (web, hotline/phone, email, USSD, in-person, letter/other).
Grievance type/category aligned to KUSP2 components and safeguards categories (configurable).
Priority/severity indicators (configurable).
Sensitive flag/category for SEA/SH or other confidential cases (special controls apply).
In addition, the Implementing Firm shall implement taxonomy governance features that make classification reliable in practice:
Controlled lists and versioning: category lists, help topics, and status codes should be configurable, but changes should be auditable and should not overwrite historical values already recorded in grievance records.
Multi-dimensional classification: classification should allow multiple dimensions to coexist (e.g., a grievance may be categorized as “Project Implementation” and also flagged “Sensitive” if applicable), with reporting able to filter/group across dimensions.
Routing alignment: classification values must align with routing rules (departments/queues/teams) to reduce manual triage and ensure timely assignment.
Consistency across languages: where public-facing categories are displayed in English and Kiswahili, the underlying coded value should remain consistent to avoid split reporting (i.e., language labels should not create distinct categories in analytics).
## 7.5 Resolution and Outcome Data
The e-GRM shall capture resolution-related data to support monitoring and reporting, including actions taken, responsible staff, key dates (assignment, response, resolution), outcome status, closure reason, and complainant satisfaction (where recorded).
In Phase 2 implementation, resolution/outcome data shall be structured so that it supports both operational learning and compliance reporting. The Implementing Firm shall ensure:
Key dates are captured automatically where possible: status change timestamps, assignment timestamps, first response timestamps, and closure timestamps should be system-generated to enable accurate SLA calculations.
Outcome capture is standardized: “Resolution Outcome/Summary” and “Closure reason” (where implemented) should support consistent reporting (e.g., resolved with corrective action; resolved with information provided; referred to another body; invalid/duplicate; withdrawn).
Satisfaction is reportable: satisfaction outcomes (“Satisfied”, “Not satisfied”, “N/A”) must be captured in a structured form and linked to the closure event, with ability to trigger reopening/escalation workflows where “Not satisfied” is recorded (as defined in functional requirements).
Escalations/referrals are traceable: referral/escalation events should be identifiable in the record and audit trail, including the level and receiving unit, without requiring sensitive details in free text.
## 7.6 Reference Data Lists
The system shall support administration of reference data lists, including but not limited to: categories, status codes, departments/queues, escalation levels, notification templates, and SLA parameters. Reference data shall be configurable without development.
To maintain data quality and reporting integrity, reference data management should include:
Ownership and update controls: designated administrators should manage reference data; updates should be logged and, where supported, reviewed/approved before becoming active.
Active/inactive lifecycle: list items should support deactivation rather than deletion so historical records remain interpretable (e.g., a municipality name change should not break older records).
Dependency awareness: where lists are used in routing rules, forms, and reports, changes should be validated to avoid creating orphan routing configurations or invalid form options.
Language labels: reference data should support bilingual labels (English/Kiswahili) for public-facing lists while retaining a stable underlying value/code for analytics.
## 7.7 Data Volume and Scaling Considerations
The system shall support national rollout across all participating counties and municipalities, with capacity to scale as usage grows. The Implementing Firm shall size the solution (within the approved hosting environment) to support concurrent users and anticipated growth, and provide sizing assumptions in the Implementation Plan.
At minimum, sizing assumptions and scaling approach should address:
Transaction volumes: anticipated grievance submissions per day/week/month (including spikes during sensitization campaigns or incidents).
Concurrent usage: staff users across national/county/municipal levels and public portal access patterns.
Attachment storage: expected attachment volume and average file size, including impact of retention periods and backup strategy.
Reporting load: expected frequency and complexity of dashboard/report generation (including national aggregation and exports).
Growth trajectory: ability to add additional counties/municipalities, departments, categories, and channels without data model redesign.
## 7.8 Data Retention, Archiving and Deletion
The e-GRM shall support retention and archiving policies aligned to legal and program requirements. The system shall support:
Configurable retention periods for grievance records and attachments (by category where necessary).
Archiving mechanisms that preserve audit trails and support retrieval for authorized users.
Controlled deletion/anonymization where legally required (e.g., upon expiry of retention period or for specific categories), subject to authorization and audit logging.
Export of records for statutory reporting, audits or legal proceedings where authorized.
For implementation clarity, the Implementing Firm shall ensure:
Retention rules can differ by sensitivity: sensitive grievances (e.g., SEA/SH) may require distinct retention, stricter access, and carefully controlled export/anonymization behavior as per agreed policy.
Archiving preserves integrity: archiving should remove records from routine operational queues while keeping them retrievable for authorized roles; archived records must retain full audit history.
Deletion/anonymization is governed: deletion or anonymization actions should require elevated permissions, record the authorizing user, and be fully auditable. Where anonymization is applied, it should remove or mask personal identifiers while preserving aggregate/statistical value where appropriate.
Legal hold and audit readiness (where applicable): the system should support preventing deletion/anonymization for records under review/audit (e.g., by flagging a record for restricted disposal), consistent with Client governance procedures.
## 7.9 Data Migration Requirements (if applicable)
If the Client requires migration of existing grievance records (e.g., from legacy registers or spreadsheets) into the e-GRM, the Implementing Firm shall provide a migration approach including mapping, cleansing support, migration scripts/tools, validation, and evidence of completed migration. Migration requirements and scope (datasets, history depth) will be confirmed during mobilization.
The migration approach should include, at minimum:
Source inventory and profiling: confirm source formats (paper registers, spreadsheets), field completeness, duplicates, and sensitive data presence.
Mapping to the standardized dataset: define a mapping matrix from source fields to e-GRM fields, including rules for missing data (e.g., use “Unknown”/blank where allowed; flag records requiring review).
Data cleansing and de-duplication rules: propose practical de-duplication logic (e.g., by reference number, date, complainant contact, similarity of description) while ensuring legitimate distinct grievances are not merged incorrectly.
Privacy controls during migration: ensure migration files are handled securely, access is limited, and any sensitive records are migrated with appropriate confidentiality flags and restricted visibility from day one.
Validation and sign-off: provide validation outputs (record counts, exception lists, sample record verification, field-level completeness checks) and obtain Client confirmation that migrated data is accurate and usable.
Rollback/contingency plan: define how the migration can be re-run or rolled back safely in the event of defects, without creating duplicate records or breaking auditability.
# 8. Integration and Interface Requirements
This section defines required integrations and interfaces for the e-GRM. The Implementing Firm may propose any suitable technical approach, provided the delivered solution meets the interface requirements and uses secure, standards-based mechanisms.
Across all interfaces and integrations, the Implementing Firm shall ensure:
A single system of record: All enabled channels (web, assisted intake, email, SMS/USSD, hotline) must create or update the same underlying grievance record and preserve consistent reference numbering and status tracking.
Security-by-design: Integrations shall use authenticated access, encrypted transport, secure secret management, and least-privilege controls. Integration endpoints, keys/tokens, and service accounts shall be environment-specific (dev/test/staging/production) and revocable.
Privacy and confidentiality: Interface designs must minimize exposure of personally identifiable information (PII) and sensitive content. In particular, public channels (SMS/USSD) shall not expose sensitive details and must use “minimum necessary” notifications.
Reliability and auditability: Integrations must include error handling, retries (where appropriate), reconciliation procedures, and auditable logs showing what was sent/received, when, and with which system identity.
Operational supportability: The Implementing Firm shall provide interface specifications, configuration documentation, and administrator runbooks describing how to operate, monitor, troubleshoot, and rotate credentials for each enabled integration.
## 8.1 Integration with KUSP2 Website and Digital Properties
The e-GRM public portal interfaces shall be accessible from the official KUSP2 website and/or approved digital properties. At minimum, the grievance submission and tracking interfaces shall be linkable or embeddable within the KUSP2 website.
To ensure consistent public access and trust, the Implementing Firm shall support one or more approved integration patterns (as directed by the Client), such as:
Direct linking: The KUSP2 website links users to the e-GRM portal pages (landing page, submission form, status tracking page).
Embedding/iframe integration (where approved): The KUSP2 website embeds key portal pages in a controlled frame, with explicit controls to prevent clickjacking, cross-site scripting risk, and unauthorized embedding by unapproved domains.
Subdomain or branded URL approach (where feasible): The e-GRM public portal is accessible using an approved, recognizable URL pattern to enhance public confidence, while still maintaining separation between public portal and staff console.
Integration with digital properties shall also ensure:
Consistent public messaging: The privacy notice, “free of charge” notice, confidentiality assurances, and process overview are visible and consistent regardless of whether a user enters via KUSP2 site navigation, direct link, or embedded portal view.
Mobile and low-bandwidth considerations: The embedded or linked experience must remain usable on common mobile devices and low-bandwidth connections, without requiring heavy page assets to perform basic actions such as submitting a grievance or checking status.
Availability handling: When the e-GRM is offline (planned maintenance or unplanned outage), the public entry points from KUSP2 digital properties shall direct users to an “offline/maintenance” page with clear alternate instructions (e.g., assisted intake options) as approved by the Client.
Environment separation: Any “test” links, demo environments, or staging URLs must not be exposed on public-facing pages.

## 8.2 Toll-Free Hotline Integration (where enabled)
Where the Client enables a toll-free hotline, the e-GRM shall support assisted intake and (where feasible) integration with call center workflows. The Implementing Firm shall provide integration specifications and implement the agreed integration within Phase 2.

Optional call-centre system integration (where approved and feasible): Where a call centre platform exists and the Client approves integration, the Implementing Firm should support linkage of non-sensitive call metadata (e.g., call date/time, call queue/line, agent identifier) to the grievance record, subject to privacy safeguards.

## 8.3 USSD Integration (where enabled)
Where the Client enables a USSD channel through a telecom operator or aggregator, the Implementing Firm shall integrate the USSD channel to create grievances and/or perform status enquiries. Integration shall be secure and reliable, with monitoring and error handling.
The USSD integration must be designed with the practical constraints of USSD sessions, including short session timeouts and limited text display. Therefore, the Implementing Firm shall ensure:
Short, optimized menu flows: Capture only the minimum required data for USSD as defined in the standardized dataset, and guide users toward assisted channels where more detail is needed.
Language prompts and clarity: Prompts must be available at minimum in English and Kiswahili, using simple wording suitable for broad public audiences.
Safe handling of partial sessions: If a session drops before completion, the system should not create incomplete records unless explicitly configured (and if configured, such partial records must be clearly flagged for triage).
Security controls: USSD gateway integrations must be protected via authenticated mechanisms and network controls (e.g., IP allowlisting and secure transport to the extent supported by the gateway), and must avoid exposing sensitive case details in USSD responses.
Operational monitoring: Administrators should be able to view basic operational indicators for USSD transactions (success/failure counts and error codes where available) to identify issues such as gateway downtime or misconfiguration.

Consent and contact preferences: Communications should respect complainant preferences captured during intake (where provided) and avoid sending messages to contacts when consent or authorization is not present.

## 9.1 Kenya Data Protection Act (2019) and Privacy Requirements
The system shall implement privacy-by-design controls consistent with Kenya’s data protection requirements, including:
Lawful basis and consent capture where required (including recording of consent and privacy notice version).
The e-GRM shall support explicit consent at point of submission where personal data is captured and store verifiable consent records. At minimum, the system shall:
Record consent as a structured event (checkbox/affirmative action), including timestamp, channel, and form/notice version.
Support differentiated consent where needed (e.g., consent to be contacted via SMS vs. email).
Allow administrators to update the privacy notice content while preserving version history for audit purposes (so that historical cases retain the notice version presented at the time of submission).
Support the ability to record consent for assisted intake where the complainant is not using the portal (e.g., a call-handler records consent based on a script), including the staff user identity and intake channel.
Data minimization: collect only necessary data fields; support anonymous grievances.
The system shall enable anonymous submission as per functional requirements, and ensure that:
Contact details are optional unless required for a specific process step, and the system does not force collection of phone/email for non-sensitive general grievances.
Channel-specific constraints (e.g., USSD typically captures phone number implicitly) are addressed by limiting collection to minimum viable data and applying controls that treat phone number as personal data.
Free-text fields are governed with guidance text and staff training to avoid unnecessary inclusion of personal or sensitive information in narrative fields.
Access controls and least-privilege permissions, including segregation for sensitive grievances.
The system shall implement a layered privacy access model:
Role-based access control (RBAC) at module/function level (e.g., who can export data, who can view audit logs, who can configure retention).
Record-level restrictions by department, jurisdiction (county/municipality), assignment, and sensitive classification flags.
Field/attachment protection so that specific data elements (e.g., complainant contact details, certain attachments) can be masked or restricted to authorized roles.
Need-to-know handling for sensitive cases (SEA/SH and other confidential grievances), including restricted visibility, restricted exports, and redacted notifications.
Security controls (encryption in transit; secure storage; audit logs).
The Implementing Firm shall implement and document controls that protect personal data throughout the processing lifecycle:
Transport encryption (HTTPS/TLS), secure cookies/sessions, and protection from common web threats.
Secure storage controls for attachments and structured data, including access-controlled storage paths and authentication-gated downloads.
Comprehensive audit logging (who accessed what, when, and from where) for privileged operations and access to sensitive records.
Support for data subject rights requests (access, correction, deletion/anonymization where applicable) through controlled administrative processes and audit trails.
While the Client remains responsible for responding to data subject requests, the system shall support operational capability to comply. Minimum system enablement includes:
Ability to search and retrieve all grievances linked to a complainant (including across channels).
Controlled update mechanisms for correcting personal information (with audit trail of what changed).
Controlled anonymization/deletion actions consistent with retention policy and lawful requests (including approvals and audit logging).
Ability to place a “legal hold” / retention hold (where supported) to prevent deletion of records needed for investigations or ongoing proceedings.

Consent capture records demonstrated (including privacy notice versioning).
Access control matrix (roles × permissions) including sensitive-case roles.
Sample audit logs demonstrating access/event traceability.

## 9.7 World Bank Environmental and Social Safeguards (ESS10 and Related Requirements)
The e-GRM shall support KUSP2 safeguards requirements by ensuring accessible grievance channels, timely processing, transparent tracking, and protection of complainants. The system shall support reporting required for safeguards monitoring.
In practical terms, Phase 2 implementation shall ensure the e-GRM enables:
Accessible and inclusive intake:
Multiple channels (web + assisted intake as baseline, and enabled telecom/email channels where available).
Anonymous reporting capability.
Clear public guidance (how to lodge, what to expect, free-of-charge statement, confidentiality and non-retaliation assurances).
Predictable and timely processing:
Configurable SLAs for acknowledgement/response/resolution, aligned to KUSP2 service standards.
Overdue monitoring and escalation rules supporting municipal → county → national workflows.
Traceability and transparency:
Status tracking for complainants (via reference number and agreed authentication checks).
Auditable lifecycle events (triage, assignment, investigation actions, outcomes, closure, satisfaction).
Safeguards reporting outputs:
Reporting and disaggregation by geography, category, channel, and status.
Trend analysis capability to identify systemic issues and support corrective actions.
Sensitive-case reporting in aggregate form only, consistent with confidentiality requirements.
Implementation evidence expectations (minimum):
Demonstration of end-to-end grievance lifecycle scenarios aligned to safeguards needs.
Sample safeguards dashboards/exports showing required disaggregation fields.
SLA configuration and overdue/escalation demonstration.
## 9.8 SEA/SH Grievance Handling and Survivor-Centered Approaches
SEA/SH grievances require special confidentiality, restricted access, and appropriate referral pathways. The system shall support sensitive case workflows and ensure communications do not expose survivors or sensitive details.
Phase 2 implementation shall operationalize SEA/SH handling through both system controls and safe operational procedures:
Confidential intake and classification:
Ability to classify a grievance as sensitive (SEA/SH and other confidential categories).
Clear guidance for staff to avoid entering unnecessary personal details into free-text fields.
Restricted access and safe routing:
Sensitive cases shall be automatically routed to designated focal person(s)/queues with restricted visibility.
Access attempts and access grants shall be logged.
Staff outside authorized roles must not be able to view sensitive case content or attachments.
Safe communications and notification suppression:
Default notifications for sensitive cases should be suppressed or strictly minimized to avoid inadvertent disclosure via SMS/email.
Where communication is necessary, templates must be redacted and generic, and align with survivor-centered principles and agreed protocols.
Referral tracking without over-collection:
The system shall support structured referral/action tracking (e.g., “referred to designated service provider / focal point” with dates) without requiring narrative detail.
Any reporting on sensitive cases shall be aggregate-only by default.
Training and controlled operationalization:
SEA/SH handling procedures must be reinforced through training materials and role-based training (what to do, what not to record, who to escalate to, how to communicate safely).
Implementation evidence expectations (minimum):
Sensitive case workflow demonstration (classification → restricted routing → restricted visibility → safe outcome recording).
Permissions evidence for SEA/SH roles (access control matrix).
Notification template review evidence (showing suppression/redaction).
Aggregate-only report example for sensitive categories.

# 10. Assumptions and Constraints
This section documents the planning assumptions and practical constraints that govern Phase 2 delivery (3 months). These items are intended to (i) set shared expectations between the Client and the Implementing Firm, (ii) clarify what conditions must be in place for timely delivery and acceptance, and (iii) highlight key risks requiring proactive mitigation through the Implementation Plan (Deliverable D1), risk register, and delivery governance.
Where any key assumption is not met, the Implementing Firm shall (a) promptly log the issue as a dependency or risk, (b) propose feasible mitigation and sequencing options that preserve delivery of Must requirements, and (c) submit any required change requests through the agreed change control process (without delaying critical-path work unnecessarily).
## 10.1 Key Assumptions
a) Phase 1 outputs (requirements, standardized intake form, stakeholder engagement requirements, workflow scenarios) are available and approved for use during Phase 2.
The Phase 2 implementation assumes that Phase 1 deliverables are provided to the Implementing Firm during Week 1 mobilization in complete and usable form, including (as applicable):
Validated business and functional requirements and workflow scenarios for key grievance journeys (intake → triage → assignment → investigation/action → resolution → closure → satisfaction/appeal).
The finalized standardized grievance intake dataset (mandatory/optional fields, validation rules, classification taxonomy, and channel-specific minimum fields).
Reporting and KPI expectations (including disaggregation dimensions such as geography, channel, category/type, status, and SLA metrics).
Compliance and risk inputs (e.g., privacy and sensitive-case handling expectations, records retention assumptions, and accessibility expectations).
“Approved for use” means the Client confirms these are baseline inputs for configuration and testing, with any material changes managed through agreed change control to protect the 3month timeline.
b) The Client will provide timely access to required environments, credentials, and integration endpoints (or approve the Implementing Firm’s proposed environments where applicable).
Phase 2 assumes the Client will enable practical access for implementation and testing by:
Confirming the approved hosting model for production (and whether dev/test/staging are separate) and providing environment owners/approvers.
Providing required credentials, network access (including VPN/IP allowlisting where applicable), DNS/SSL certificate arrangements, and any government security approvals needed to deploy and test.
Ensuring any Client-side technical dependencies (e.g., website embedding/linking approvals, email domain/SPF/DKIM where required) are actioned within the delivery window.
c) The Client will lead commercial/regulatory arrangements for telecom services (USSD short codes, toll-free hotline, SMS bundles) and provide timely integration parameters.
This BRD assumes that where telecom channels are enabled, the Client will:
Secure and maintain the relevant commercial arrangements and regulatory approvals with telecom operators/aggregators and hotline providers.
Provide the Implementing Firm with technical integration parameters (e.g., gateway endpoints, credentials, whitelisted IPs, callback URLs/webhooks, sender IDs/short codes, and test numbers) early enough to meet Release 2 and UAT timelines.
The Implementing Firm’s responsibility is to implement and test the technical integration using secure methods and provide monitoring/error-handling and fallback procedures.
d) Key NPCT and selected county/municipal stakeholders will be available for demonstrations, training and UAT within the 3-month timeline.
Successful adoption and acceptance requires active participation from stakeholders in:
Requirements confirmation and configuration validation (Weeks 1–3).
Sprint demos and scenario walk-throughs (at minimum fortnightly, and before UAT).
UAT execution and sign-off (Weeks 9–11), including participation by a representative mix of users (national, county/municipal focal persons, safeguards, M&E/reporting users, and administrators).
Training attendance and practical exercises using agreed scenarios and sample data.
e) The Implementing Firm will be able to deploy the solution to an environment approved for production use, compliant with data residency and security requirements.
Phase 2 assumes the final production deployment target is confirmed early enough to support:
Security hardening and configuration (role/permission model, encryption, audit logs, retention settings).
Performance readiness checks appropriate to expected loads.
Go-live cutover planning, backup/restore readiness, and hypercare support.
If the approved production environment is delayed, the Implementing Firm shall propose an interim path (e.g., staging-to-production promotion plan) that preserves compliance requirements and does not compromise data protection.
## 10.2 Constraints
a) Phase 2 delivery timeline is fixed at three (3) months; scope prioritization may be required for Should/Could requirements if dependencies delay delivery.
The Implementing Firm shall plan delivery around the fixed milestone cadence and deliver all Must requirements. Where dependencies (e.g., telecom provisioning, hosting approvals) threaten timeline, the Implementing Firm shall:
Preserve delivery of core intake + case management + reporting + compliance controls.
Sequence optional channels/features as “enabled when available” without blocking go-live (using assisted intake and alternative notification paths where needed).
Maintain a transparent backlog showing Must/Should/Could status, delivery sequencing, and dependency impacts.
b) Technology and platform neutrality is required; this BRD defines outcomes and capabilities, not specific products or architectures.
The Implementing Firm may propose any suitable solution approach provided it:
Meets all functional and non-functional requirements, including privacy, auditability, accessibility, and interoperability.
Avoids unjustified vendor lock-in and supports practical handover, administration, and data export.
Enables configuration-based changes for common updates (forms, lists, templates, routing, SLAs) without requiring custom development for routine administration.
c) Automated chatbots shall not be used for grievance intake.
The system must rely on guided forms and assisted intake processes for grievance submission. Informational features (FAQs/knowledge resources) may be used to reduce repeat queries, but cannot replace clear and safe intake mechanisms—especially for sensitive cases.
d) Compliance with data protection and confidentiality requirements may restrict certain data capture and reporting approaches (e.g., for sensitive grievances).
Constraints include the need to:
Minimize collection of personal data to what is necessary for handling the grievance (and support anonymous reporting).
Prevent exposure of sensitive information through unsecured channels (e.g., SMS/email templates should avoid sensitive details).
Apply need-to-know access, restricted visibility, and controlled exports for sensitive cases (e.g., SEA/SH), including aggregate-only reporting by default.
e) Availability and reliability of telecom channels (USSD/SMS/hotline) may depend on third-party providers and coverage; the system must support operational fallbacks (assisted intake, alternative notifications).
The e-GRM must remain operational even when specific channels are delayed or unavailable, including:
Assisted intake by staff (phone/in-person) with consistent reference numbering and standardized data capture.
Alternative contact methods based on complainant preference and feasibility (e.g., phone call where SMS delivery fails).
Logging of failed notification attempts to preserve an auditable record and prompt follow-up actions.

# 11. Acceptance Criteria (Enhanced)
Acceptance criteria define how the Client will verify that Phase 2 implementation outputs meet requirements and are fit for operational use under KUSP2. Acceptance will be evidence-based and will rely on a combination of demonstrations, documented test results, configuration evidence, training completion records, and handover artifacts. The Implementing Firm shall plan for acceptance from the start of Phase 2, embedding testability, traceability, and evidence capture into each sprint/release and deliverable.
Unless otherwise agreed during mobilization (Deliverable D1), acceptance will be assessed at three complementary levels:
Deliverable acceptance (D1–D9): confirmation that each contractual output is complete, submitted in the agreed format(s), and meets the “Minimum Contents” and quality requirements.
System acceptance: confirmation that the implemented e-GRM meets Must functional requirements and non-functional/compliance requirements through testing and UAT.
Operational acceptance/go-live readiness: confirmation that the system can be operated safely and sustainably (users trained, roles configured, support and backup procedures ready), and that residual risks are documented and manageable.
The Implementing Firm shall maintain an Acceptance Evidence Pack throughout implementation, comprising (at minimum): a requirements-to-test traceability matrix, test scripts and results, defect logs and resolutions, configuration screenshots/exports, training materials and attendance, UAT sign-off evidence, and go-live/hypercare records.
## 11.1 Functional Acceptance
Functional acceptance requires that all Must functional requirements (Section 5) are implemented and verified through demonstrations and documented test cases, including end-to-end workflow scenarios across intake channels, governance levels, and grievance types (including sensitive cases). Functional acceptance is not limited to UI checks; it includes workflow correctness, data validation, auditability, and role-based access behaviour.
Minimum functional acceptance requirements include:
All Must functional requirements are implemented and verified (including multi-channel intake, case management workflows, notifications, reporting, and admin configuration).
Standardized grievance intake data fields and validations are implemented across relevant channels (web, assisted intake, and enabled integrations such as email, USSD, hotline).
Sensitive grievance handling controls are implemented and verified, including restricted access and safe communications.
Audit trail is available and complete for key actions and status transitions.
Data exports and reports produce correct and complete outputs aligned to configured fields and filters.
To strengthen repeatability and reduce ambiguity, the Implementing Firm shall support functional acceptance using the following minimum verification approach:
a) Requirement Traceability and Coverage
Maintain a Requirements Traceability Matrix (RTM) mapping each Must requirement ID (e.g., FR-PUB-01) to one or more test cases/scripts, expected results, and evidence location (screenshots/log extracts/export files).
Ensure coverage includes:
Public portal journeys (submit grievance, receive reference number, status check, knowledge resources).
Assisted intake journeys (staff creates grievance on behalf of complainant; source recorded correctly).
Staff case handling journeys (triage, assignment, internal notes, external replies, escalation, closure).
Administration/configuration journeys (update categories, SLAs, templates, routing rules without code).
Reporting and exports (dashboards, disaggregation filters, SLA metrics, audit log exports).
Attachment handling journeys (upload, access control inheritance, export/print case file).
b) End-to-End Acceptance Scenarios
The Implementing Firm shall demonstrate and test a minimum set of end-to-end scenarios, agreed during mobilization, that reflect real KUSP2 operating contexts. Scenarios shall include, at minimum:
Scenario 1: Web submission → acknowledgement → assignment → response → resolution → closure
Verifies reference numbering, mandatory fields, consent capture, acknowledgement/notification, workflow transitions, and closure controls.
Scenario 2: Assisted intake (phone/in-person) → follow-up communications → closure
Verifies staff intake, source attribution, complainant directory handling, and documentation of actions taken.
Scenario 3: Overdue grievance → escalation rule triggers → escalated queue visibility
Verifies SLA calculations, overdue flagging, escalation routing, and audit logs for escalation events.
Scenario 4: Reopen/appeal due to dissatisfaction → reassignment/escalation → outcome recording
Verifies complainant satisfaction capture, reopening pathway, and decision trail.
Scenario 5: Sensitive grievance classification → restricted access → safe notifications suppressed/redacted
Verifies sensitive classification controls, need-to-know access, restricted reporting/export, and safe handling of communications.
Scenario 6 (if enabled): Email/USSD/hotline integration paths
Verifies that enabled channels create/append to the correct grievance record, acknowledge appropriately, and do not expose sensitive content.
c) Data Validation and Business Rules
Functional acceptance must confirm that standardized data capture (Section 7) is implemented consistently, including:
Mandatory fields are enforced per channel constraints (e.g., USSD short flow vs. full web form).
Conditional fields behave correctly (e.g., representative fields become required when lodging on behalf).
Controlled vocabularies/reference lists are used where specified (county/municipality, categories, statuses).
Consent capture is recorded with time/date and (where implemented) privacy notice version or reference.
Anonymous grievances can be recorded without forcing personal identifiers while still capturing actionable grievance details.
d) Defect Classification and Acceptance Gates
Unless otherwise agreed in D1/D7, functional acceptance will apply the following minimum defect gating principles:
Severity 1 (Critical): blocks core workflow, creates data integrity risk, enables unauthorized access, breaks sensitive case confidentiality, or prevents go-live → must be fixed before UAT sign-off/go-live.
Severity 2 (High): materially affects usability or compliance but has a documented workaround → must be fixed before go-live unless a Client-approved mitigation exists.
Severity 3 (Medium/Low): minor defect or cosmetic issue → may be scheduled post go-live with a tracked resolution plan, subject to Client approval and risk assessment.
## 11.2 Performance and Availability Acceptance
Performance and availability acceptance verifies that the e-GRM meets Non-Functional Requirements (Section 6) in an environment representative of intended operations and within constraints of the approved hosting environment.
a) Performance Acceptance
Performance acceptance requires verification against the NFR performance benchmarks through documented performance testing and/or monitored demonstrations, including:
User interface response times for key workflows (opening queues, loading grievance detail, search/filter, posting a reply, saving a draft).
Reporting responsiveness for standard dashboards, SLA monitoring views, and exports.
Integration responsiveness (where applicable) such as inbound email processing latency and outbound notification dispatch time.
Minimum performance acceptance approach:
Define test approach and assumptions in D1/D5 (e.g., number of concurrent staff users, expected daily/weekly volume, attachment sizes).
Conduct performance tests on staging (or equivalent) with test data volumes sufficient to reflect realistic usage (including closed-case history and attachments).
Provide evidence that performance meets the thresholds in Section 6 (e.g., 95% page renders within defined limits under expected peak load), noting any exclusions (e.g., large downloads).
b) Availability and Operational Continuity Acceptance
Availability acceptance requires verification of continuity controls appropriate to a public service channel, including:
Maintenance/offline mode: ability to display a configured offline page and safely prevent new submissions where required during maintenance, while preserving data integrity.
Monitoring readiness: basic operational visibility into system status (uptime, errors, integration failures), and an agreed escalation route for incidents during hypercare.
Backup/restore readiness:
Document backup schedules, retention, and storage location(s) for databases and attachments.
Demonstrate at least one successful restore test (or an equivalent verification mechanism) prior to go-live, with evidence (logs, timestamps, validation of restored records).
Confirm who has access to backup tooling, and how access is governed/audited.
Where RTO/RPO targets are required, they shall be documented and agreed in D1/D2 based on hosting constraints and program criticality, and verified to the extent feasible during Phase 2.
## 11.3 Security, Privacy and Compliance Acceptance
Security, privacy, and compliance acceptance confirms that the e-GRM is configured and tested to protect personal data, prevent unauthorized access, and support legal/safeguards obligations (Section 9). Acceptance is evidence-driven: the Implementing Firm must provide configuration documentation, test results, and verification artifacts.
Minimum acceptance requirements include:
a) Identity and Access Controls
Role-based access controls (RBAC) are configured and tested for:
Separation of standard user vs. administrator privileges.
Department/team scope and record-level visibility rules.
Attachment access inheritance and “login required” behaviour where enabled.
Sensitive grievance restricted visibility and audited access.
Authentication controls are verified:
Password policy and account lifecycle controls (create/disable).
MFA/2FA enablement and enforcement according to policy.
Session management controls (timeout/invalid session handling) as supported by the solution.
b) Data Protection and Privacy Controls
Public portal privacy notice is displayed and consent is captured (where required) with timestamp and stored as part of the grievance record metadata.
Anonymous submission is supported without forcing personal identifiers, while still enabling processing.
Data minimization practices are enforced in channel designs (e.g., USSD flow does not collect unnecessary sensitive data).
Reporting outputs and exports default to privacy-preserving behaviour for sensitive cases (aggregate outputs; restricted access to PII exports).
c) Encryption, Secure Configuration, and Logging
HTTPS/TLS is enforced for all web access and tested.
Audit logs are enabled and verified for:
Key operational actions (create / edit / status change / assignment / close / reopen).
Administrative/configuration changes.
Access attempts to sensitive grievances and protected attachments.
Logging and retention controls are configured according to Section 6/9, including log retention and controlled deletion (where applicable).
d) Security Testing Evidence
The Implementing Firm shall provide agreed security testing evidence proportionate to risk and timeline, such as:
Vulnerability scanning results (application and/or infrastructure scope as agreed), including remediation records for high/critical issues.
Verification of permission boundaries (attempted unauthorized access tests) and evidence of denial/logging.
Review of integration security controls (API key controls, IP allowlisting, secure secret handling).
e) Compliance Evidence Pack
The Implementing Firm shall provide a concise, structured compliance evidence pack aligned to Section 9.9, including (as applicable):
Access control matrix and role catalogue (with sensitive case roles explicitly identified).
Data flow/integration overview and data residency statement for the deployed environment.
Retention/archiving configuration notes and operational procedures.
Safe messaging template review outputs (ensuring sensitive content is excluded from SMS/email by default).

## 12.2 Revised Standard Grievance Intake/Data Collection Form (Template)
This appendix summarizes the standardized grievance intake data collection template to be implemented in the e-GRM. The Implementing Firm shall ensure the public portal and assisted intake screens capture these fields with the required validation rules. Full reference form text and guidance is provided in the supporting annex.
A. Standardized intake fields and minimum validation rules
| Section | Field | Mandatory | Type | Notes / Rules |
| A | Complainant Name | Optional | Text | Allow blank for anonymous submission. If provided, enforce reasonable length limits and character safety (no script injection). |
| A | Contact Phone Number | Optional | Telephone | Recommended for follow-up; store with consent basis as applicable. Validate format; normalize (e.g., country code) where configured. |
| A | Email Address | Optional | Email | Store with consent. Validate format; apply domain allow/block rules if configured. |
| A | Postal/Physical Address | Optional | Text | For correspondence where applicable; may be captured as free-text with optional structured components (county/sub-county/ward) if configured. |
| A | Preferred Method of Contact | Optional | Select | Phone, Email, SMS, Post, In-person. If provided, should guide notification choices and staff follow-up actions. |
| A | Representative’s Name | Conditional | Text | Required where a grievance is submitted on behalf of another person/group. |
| A | Relationship/Organization | Conditional | Text | Describe relationship to affected person/group (e.g., family member, community leader, CSO). |
| A | Representative’s Contact | Conditional | Telephone/Email | Required where representative is used and differs from complainant contact. Validate as above. |
| A | Consent of Affected Person | Conditional | Checkbox | Record authorization to lodge on behalf of another person/group; store timestamp and, where used, method of confirmation. |
| A | Consent to Personal Data Use | Required | Checkbox | Required when personal data is provided/processed. Record consent timestamp and privacy notice version displayed at time of consent. |
| B | Date of Submission | Optional | Date | Receiving officer may record if blank. System shall also record system “created date/time” automatically for audit purposes. |
| B | Grievance Reference Number | System | ID | System-generated unique reference. Reference number format shall support operational usability (readable, unique, non-guessable if required). |
| B | Submission Channel | Required | Select | In-person, Phone Call, Email, Online Form, Letter, etc. Source/channel should be captured consistently for reporting and SLA measurement. |
| B | County | Required | Select | Select county where issue is relevant. Must be from controlled list to support disaggregation. |
| B | City/Municipality | Required | Select | Select city/municipality where issue is relevant. Must be from controlled list and mapped to county for data quality checks where feasible. |
| B | Location/Site of Concern | Optional | Text | Specific location details (neighborhood, project site). Guidance text should encourage non-sensitive location description where confidentiality is requested. |
| B | Grievance Category | Required | Multi-select | Environmental; Social; Gender-Based (GBV/SEAH); Corruption/Fraud; Project Implementation; Institutional/Policy; Other (specify). Categories must be configurable. |
| B | Description of Grievance | Required | Long text | Detailed description; allow attachments. For sensitive cases, implement guidance text and controls to minimize unnecessary sensitive narrative in free text. |
| B | Evidence/Supporting Documents | Optional | Attachment(s) | Allow upload/list; guidance on limits. Apply file type/size restrictions and access control inheritance. |
| B | Reported Elsewhere? | Optional | Yes/No + text | If yes, capture details and any reference number (e.g., police OB number, other GRM reference) where voluntarily provided and appropriate. |
| C | Expected Outcome | Optional | Multi-select + text | Information/Explanation; Corrective Action; Compensation/Remedy; Accountability/Disciplinary Action; Other (specify). Used to guide triage and resolution planning. |
| D | Date Received | Required | Date | For official use. For web submissions, the system may auto-populate from submission timestamp while allowing staff verification/adjustment per policy. |
| D | Received By (Name & Office) | Required | Text | Officer/office that received/logged. For web submissions, system may record “e-GRM portal” and assign receiving office based on routing rules. |
| D | Assigned To (Officer/Unit) | Required | Text/Select | Responsible handler/unit. Must support assignment to named officer or team/unit, consistent with permissions and routing configuration. |
| D | Actions Taken / Investigation Notes | Optional | Long text | Record key steps and findings. This should be internal-only by default; visibility must be controlled for confidentiality. |
| D | Grievance Status | Required | Select | Open/Pending; Resolved; Referred; Escalated (Municipality/County/National); Closed. Status values may be extended with sub-statuses, but core values shall remain reportable. |
| D | Resolution Outcome/Summary | Conditional | Long text | Mandatory on resolution/closure. Should support structured outcome codes and a narrative summary. |
| D | Date of Resolution | Conditional | Date | Required when status moves to Resolved/Closed (as applicable). |
| D | Complainant Informed of Outcome | Conditional | Yes/No + date | If anonymous/unreachable mark accordingly. System should record method of communication and timestamp where feasible. |
| D | Complainant Response | Conditional | Select | Satisfied; Not satisfied; N/A (anonymous). If “Not satisfied”, enable reopen/appeal/escalation workflow per BRD. |
| D | Referral/Escalation Details | Conditional | Long text | If referred/escalated capture details (to whom, why, date, and reference identifiers if any). |

B. Channel alignment and practical implementation guidance (minimum)
To ensure consistent implementation across channels, the Implementing Firm shall operationalize the standardized fields as follows:
Web portal submissions
Shall capture all required public-facing fields with clear labels, tooltips, and validation.
Shall explicitly capture Consent to Personal Data Use (and record the privacy notice version presented).
Shall allow anonymous submission with clear messaging on limitations (e.g., reduced ability to provide updates).
Shall provide structured selection lists for County and City/Municipality and prevent free-text substitutes for these controlled fields.
Assisted intake (phone/in-person/letter)
Staff intake screens shall mirror the standardized dataset and guide intake officers to capture mandatory fields reliably.
The system shall support “unknown/not provided” values (or controlled null handling) for optional fields to reduce forced fabrication of data.
For letter-based grievances, the system should support attaching scanned copies (subject to security controls) and recording the physical filing reference where used.
USSD intake (where enabled)
Shall capture a minimal mandatory subset suitable for short sessions (e.g., county, municipality, category/help topic, short description/summary, consent where phone number is treated as personal data, and a callback preference if captured).
Shall avoid collecting sensitive personal details within the USSD flow.
Shall ensure the resulting record is flagged as USSD-origin and is available for staff follow-up and completion, without creating “partial records” unless explicitly configured.
Hotline intake (where enabled)
Call handlers shall have guided scripts aligned to the standardized dataset and key policy statements, including confidentiality and non-retaliation assurances.
Where call metadata capture is enabled, it shall be stored as internal-only fields linked to the grievance record and protected as operational data.
C. Key intake policy statements to be displayed/communicated (minimum)
The system (public portal pages, assisted intake scripts, and where feasible USSD prompts) shall communicate the following statements in a clear, language-appropriate manner:
The GRM is free of charge; no fees shall be requested from complainants.
Confidentiality will be maintained and retaliation against complainants is prohibited.
KUSP2 aims to resolve grievances within the program’s target timelines where feasible, and will provide updates through agreed channels.
Automated chatbots shall not be used for grievance intake.

## 12.4 Technology Options Analysis (For Information Only)
A technology options analysis was completed during Phase 1 to inform procurement planning. This BRD does not prescribe a specific technology platform. Bidders shall propose an implementation approach that satisfies all requirements and constraints, including maintainability, scalability, accessibility, interoperability, and compliance.
To preserve technology neutrality while enabling fair and comparable proposals, bidders are expected (as part of their technical proposal) to clearly describe:
How the proposed solution meets the Must requirements in Sections 5–9, including multi-channel intake, standardized data capture, workflows, reporting, and administration.
Configuration vs. custom development split, including which features are out-of-the-box, configured, customized, or built, and the implications for timeline and long-term maintenance.
Hosting approach aligned to Client approvals, data residency expectations, and operational constraints; and how environments (dev/test/staging/production) will be managed in Phase 2.
Integration approach (email, SMS/USSD, hotline, website embedding, API), including security, monitoring, and fallback arrangements.
Licensing implications, including all third-party components, subscription or recurring costs, and any constraints on government usage.
Exit/handover provisions, including full data export, configuration export, documentation, and (where applicable) source code/build artifacts to avoid vendor lock-in.
Any proposed proprietary components must be disclosed together with licensing implications and exit/handover provisions.
## 12.5 Stakeholder Consultation Summary (Phase 1 Inputs)
Stakeholder consultations during Phase 1 informed the e-GRM requirements, including the need for multi-channel access, standardized data capture, clear escalation pathways, and usability for sub-national users. The Stakeholder Engagement Plan provides detailed engagement requirements and should be used as an input to Phase 2 communications, training, and rollout planning.
Key consolidated themes (non-exhaustive) that the Implementing Firm shall consider during Phase 2 implementation include:
Accessibility and inclusion: strong preference for multiple access channels (web + assisted intake) to avoid excluding stakeholders with limited internet access, low digital literacy, or disabilities; requirement for language-appropriate interfaces (English and Kiswahili minimum) and inclusive design practices.
Operational practicality at county/municipal level: need for simple, fast workflows and clear role-based queues; emphasis on local GRM focal persons being able to register cases quickly and track assignments and due dates without complex reporting tools.
Consistency and data quality: demand for standardized categories, controlled lists for location fields, and mandatory minimum datasets so that NPCT can reliably aggregate across counties/municipalities and meet safeguards reporting needs.
Predictable escalation and accountability: stakeholders requested clear escalation triggers and transparent status progression so that cases do not “stall” at local level without visibility.
Confidentiality and sensitive grievances: strong concern about safe handling of SEA/SH and other sensitive cases, including restricted access and communications that do not expose complainants.
Training and adoption: need for role-based training, quick reference guides, and practical scenario-based exercises (including assisted intake) to build confidence and ensure consistent use post go-live.

WF-02 Staff-created grievance (intake from phone/in-person)
| Step | Actor | Action | System Outcome / Data |
