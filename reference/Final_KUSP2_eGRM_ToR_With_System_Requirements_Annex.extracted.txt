# 1. BACKGROUND & CONTEXT
This chapter sets out the operational and strategic context for the KUSP2 electronic Grievance Redress Mechanism (eGRM). It clarifies (i) how the eGRM fits within KUSP2’s accountability architecture; (ii) why KUSP1 experience necessitated a standardized, auditable digital approach; (iii) the core legal, policy and institutional parameters that must shape the solution; and (iv) how eGRM performance information supports program monitoring and decision-making across national, county and municipal levels.
## 1.1 Program Overview and Strategic Alignment
KUSP2 is a Government of Kenya program supported by development partners to strengthen urban institutions and improve delivery of urban infrastructure and services. The program requires an electronic grievance redress mechanism (e-GRM) to provide a structured, inclusive and transparent way to receive, process, resolve and monitor grievances linked to KUSP2 activities across participating counties and municipalities.
KUSP2 is a fiveyear Government program (2024–2028) implemented through the State Department for Housing and Urban Development (SDHUD) and its National Program Coordination Team (NPCT), with implementation responsibilities distributed across 45 counties and 79 municipalities. It builds on KUSP1 (2017–2023) and continues to support urban planning, infrastructure, climate resilience, governance and inclusive service delivery—areas where citizen feedback and grievance handling are essential to maintaining trust and ensuring responsive public service delivery.
Within this program context, the eGRM is positioned as a core accountability and service-improvement instrument. It is expected to:
Complement existing county and municipal GRM structures (rather than replace them) by providing a program-wide “system of record” for grievances and actions taken;
Strengthen transparency and responsiveness through trackable cases, auditable processing, and timely feedback to complainants; and
Enable program-wide insight through standardized analytics and reporting (e.g., volumes, categories, resolution timelines, escalations), supporting management decisions and corrective actions.
Strategically, the eGRM aligns with Kenya’s governance and participation expectations—counties are required to facilitate public participation and establish ICT-based participation platforms—making a digital GRM an appropriate and scalable channel for petitioning, feedback and grievance redress across dispersed municipalities.
The eGRM is also explicitly aligned to development partner standards, including the World Bank’s requirements on stakeholder engagement and accessible grievance mechanisms, which emphasize responsiveness, inclusivity, transparency, confidentiality and protection from retaliation. These principles shape both the public-facing user experience (how people submit and track grievances) and the internal case management controls (how data is accessed, shared and acted on).
Finally, this procurement sits within a two-phase approach to the eGRM initiative, where Phase 1 deliverables (requirements, workflows, data standards, compliance mapping and procurement documentation) are provided as implementation inputs for Phase 2. Phase 2 focuses on implementing and commissioning a working solution within a three-month implementation period, without redefining the agreed functional intent.
## 1.2 Lessons from KUSP1 and Rationale for the e-GRM
Experience from prior implementation highlighted inconsistent and weakly documented handling of grievances across counties and municipalities. The e-GRM is intended to standardize grievance handling, improve responsiveness and accountability, and generate reliable management information for monitoring performance and informing corrective actions.
KUSP1 experience (as reflected in stakeholder consultations and documentation reviews) highlighted recurring, practical weaknesses that the eGRM is intended to address, including:
Non-standard practices and uneven capacity: grievance handling approaches varied widely by county/municipality, with differences in intake channels, escalation practices, and documentation discipline;
Paper-based or poorly structured recordkeeping: inconsistent and poorly documented grievance records reduced traceability, made follow-up difficult, and limited learning; and
Limited management visibility: fragmented data meant the program could not reliably aggregate trends or monitor timeliness and outcomes across the portfolio.
These issues are not merely administrative—they directly affect trust, reputational risk and program performance. KUSP2’s risk context and financing arrangements place strong emphasis on having a functional complaints handling system early in implementation, reinforcing the need for a mechanism that can demonstrate both existence (a working GRM) and performance (timely and fair handling with evidence).
The eGRM rationale is therefore to establish a unified, program-wide capability that:
Standardizes grievance lifecycle management from intake through closure using configurable workflows (including assignment, investigation notes, responses, escalation and closure);
Enforces consistent data capture aligned to a standardized intake/data collection form (so that all counties and municipalities collect comparable information);
Improves responsiveness by supporting clear service standards and automated prompts (acknowledgements, reminders, escalation triggers); and
Provides actionable, reliable reporting to support governance, supervision and continuous improvement.
From a service-standard perspective, KUSP2 requires both timely engagement and timely outcomes. Program commitments include providing a response to grievances within defined timelines (including a requirement to respond within 14 days), while GRM guidance to complainants emphasizes an aim to resolve grievances within 30 days where feasible. The eGRM is expected to make these expectations operational by enabling date stamping, due-date tracking, escalation and performance reporting.
The rationale also includes strengthening safe and appropriate handling of different grievance types—including environmental, social, integrity-related issues and other sensitive matters—through controlled workflows, referral pathways and restricted access where required. For example, sensitive cases (including SEA/SH-related complaints) require a confidential pathway with restricted access and minimal sensitive data capture, reinforcing the need for a digital solution that can enforce differentiated controls by category.
## 1.3 Legal, Policy and Institutional Framework
The e-GRM must operate within the Government of Kenya legal and policy framework, including data protection and access to information obligations, county governance and public participation requirements, and relevant ICT and accessibility standards. The solution must also support KUSP2’s commitments on stakeholder engagement, confidentiality, inclusion, and non-retaliation.
a) Core legal and policy drivers
The eGRM operates in a governance environment where public participation, access to information and citizen petitioning are legal expectations. Relevant Kenyan requirements include, among others:
County Governments Act (2012): establishes principles of public participation, the right to petition county government, and county duties to respond expeditiously; it also reinforces the establishment of ICT-based platforms for citizen participation;
Access to Information Act (2016): supports citizens’ rights to access information held by public entities and reinforces openness principles; and
Related governance frameworks referenced in KUSP2 stakeholder engagement planning, including constitutional principles on transparency and accountability and urban governance requirements.
The eGRM also collects and processes personal data, making Kenya’s Data Protection Act (2019) central to system design and operations. This requires a privacy-by-design approach, including data minimization, purpose limitation and storage limitation, and influences hosting decisions, retention configurations, and user access controls. Hosting is expected to prioritize government-approved infrastructure and compliant data localization approaches.
In addition, the legal and compliance context for eGRM includes:
Telecommunications compliance under Kenya Information and Communications Act (KICA) and Communications Authority regulations, which is particularly relevant for any SMS/USSD short codes and toll-free channels;
Accessibility obligations, including Persons with Disabilities Act and national accessibility standards for public digital systems; and
Relevant labor, OSH, and environmental requirements that shape the need to receive and route specific categories (e.g., worker issues, environmental issues) to the appropriate responsible units and authorities.
b) Implications for eGRM design and operation
These legal and policy drivers translate into concrete system and operational requirements, including:
Privacy and confidentiality controls: role-based access controls, audit logging, and controlled sharing of case information—particularly for sensitive categories—so that only authorized users can view or act on certain grievances;
Data governance and lifecycle management: configurable retention and storage limitation aligned to program needs; secure hosting consistent with government and data protection expectations; and user-facing privacy notices/consent processes as applicable;
Accessibility and inclusion by design: ensuring the eGRM is usable by persons with disabilities and those with limited digital access, through accessible web design (aligned to national accessibility standards that mirror WCAG) and provision of alternative channels (e.g., toll-free phone line, assisted intake at offices); and
Regulatory readiness for channels: recognizing that telecom channels require licensing/approvals and consumer protection compliance, and may involve client-led commercial arrangements with vendor technical integration support.
KUSP2’s commitments also include operational safeguards that must be embedded as guiding principles of the mechanism—namely that the GRM be accessible, free to use, transparent, confidential, and protective against retaliation—which should be reinforced both in system design (e.g., user journey, information disclosure) and in procedures/training for users handling grievances.
c) Institutional arrangements and accountability
Institutionally, the eGRM must support a distributed operating model:
SDHUD/NPCT provides national oversight, system stewardship, consolidation of reporting and coordination with development partners and oversight bodies;
County and municipal structures (including designated GRM officers/focal points and relevant committees) are responsible for day-to-day grievance intake (including assisted intake), investigation/response, documentation of actions and closure at the appropriate level; and
Referral and escalation pathways are required for grievances that must be handled by specialized institutions (e.g., integrity/corruption allegations, certain environmental matters, sensitive safeguarding matters), with controlled information sharing and traceable handoff.
Given this model, the Implementing Firm should treat the eGRM not only as a software build, but as an enabling platform for governance and compliance: the system must embed workflow discipline, produce evidence, and support supervision, while remaining usable under real-world conditions (varying connectivity, variable capacity, multiple languages, and diverse user profiles).
## 1.4 Alignment with KUSP2 Results Areas and Disbursement Architecture
The e-GRM supports KUSP2 by enabling consistent tracking and reporting of grievance handling performance (e.g., volumes, categories, resolution timeliness, escalations). These outputs contribute to strengthened accountability and service delivery at municipal and county levels.
KUSP2’s financing and performance environment places strong emphasis on measurable governance and accountability. Under the World Bank Program-for-Results instrument, KUSP2’s overall risk profile was assessed as substantial, and a functional complaints handling system is required early in implementation—making GRM performance data a practical input into program oversight and risk management.
In this context, the eGRM supports results areas and disbursement architecture by producing reliable, standardized management information that can be used at multiple levels:
Operational level (municipality/county): backlog management, workload balancing, timeliness tracking, escalation support, and documentation of actions/outcomes;
Program coordination level (NPCT/SDHUD): consolidated dashboards and trend analysis to identify systemic issues, guide technical support, and prioritize corrective actions; and
Compliance/reporting level (development partners and oversight bodies): generation of standardized reporting outputs required for entities such as CAJ (Ombudsman), NEMA, the World Bank and others as applicable.
To support these uses, the eGRM should enable monitoring of a practical performance “minimum set” of indicators, such as:
Intake volumes by time period, county, municipality, and channel (web, email, phone-assisted, etc.);
Categorization trends (e.g., environmental, social, integrity/fraud, service delivery, project implementation);
Timeliness metrics (e.g., acknowledgement, first response, resolution/closure), including compliance reporting against required service standards (e.g., response within 14 days) and “target” resolution timelines (e.g., aim for resolution within 30 days where feasible);
Escalations/referrals (counts and destinations), including the ability to track “hand-offs” while maintaining confidentiality; and
Outcome/satisfaction signals where captured (e.g., complainant satisfied/not satisfied, reopened cases), to support continuous improvement.
The eGRM therefore enables KUSP2 to move from ad hoc grievance recording toward a consistent performance management approach, where grievance handling becomes a measurable part of strengthening municipal and county accountability and improving service delivery across the program footprint.

# 2. OBJECTIVES OF THE ASSIGNMENT
## 2.1 Overall Objective
To implement, configure, deploy and commission a technology-neutral e-GRM solution within a three-month implementation period, in accordance with the agreed requirements, workflows, data collection standards, and compliance obligations.
In this ToR, “implement and commission” means the Supplier shall deliver a working, secure and operational e-GRM that is ready for sustained use by national, county and municipal stakeholders. At minimum, commissioning shall entail: (i) a functioning public-facing grievance submission and status-tracking experience and an internal staff console for processing cases; (ii) configured workflows, routing, escalation and service-level monitoring aligned to the agreed GRM process; (iii) enabled intake channels as agreed for Phase 2 (with web and assisted intake as the baseline and other channels enabled where applicable/available); (iv) implemented data model and standardized intake dataset with appropriate validation; (v) configured privacy, security and accessibility controls; (vi) completed testing (including UAT readiness), production deployment, and post go-live stabilization; and (vii) delivery of documentation, training and handover artifacts so that the Client can operate the system sustainably beyond the implementation window.
## 2.2 Specific Objectives
The Supplier shall deliver the following specific objectives, each of which is required to achieve the overall objective within the three-month period:
a) Configure and operationalize end-to-end grievance lifecycle management across multiple intake channels.
The Supplier shall configure the e-GRM to support the full grievance lifecycle from intake to closure, including (as applicable to the agreed workflows):
Registration and acknowledgement: creation of a single grievance record per submission, allocation of a unique reference number, and acknowledgement through the originating or agreed channel.
Triage and classification: initial screening, categorization, prioritization, and determination of confidentiality/sensitivity requirements (including special handling pathways where applicable).
Assignment and workload management: routing to responsible national/county/municipal units and assignees based on configured rules (e.g., geography, category/help topic, responsible department), supported by queues, filters, and search.
Investigation and action tracking: structured capture of actions taken, internal notes, attachments/evidence, and linked tasks/reminders to support timely handling and traceability.
Escalation and referral: escalation to higher levels (municipal → county → national or other agreed escalation levels) based on elapsed time, severity, unresolved status, or other configured rules; and referral handling where matters must be redirected to an appropriate authority consistent with the GRM procedures.
Resolution, closure, and re-opening/appeal: capture of resolution summaries and closure conditions, recording complainant satisfaction where applicable, and enabling controlled re-opening or appeal workflows where complainants are dissatisfied or where further review is warranted.
These capabilities shall be implemented so that all enabled channels feed a unified case register with consistent numbering, status tracking, and auditability across the system.
b) Implement standardized grievance intake data fields and data quality controls as defined in the agreed intake/data collection form.
The Supplier shall implement the standardized intake dataset and associated processing fields as the authoritative data standard for the e-GRM, ensuring that data captured through all supported channels is consistent and fit-for-purpose for monitoring, reporting and audits. This shall include, at minimum:
Field implementation and validation: correct configuration of mandatory/optional/conditional fields; input validation (e.g., formats, completeness checks); and configurable lists/reference data (e.g., counties/municipalities, categories/help topics, status codes).
Controlled vocabularies and disaggregation: implementation of category/type taxonomies and geography fields so grievances can be consistently filtered, routed and reported at national and sub-national levels.
Consent and anonymity handling: ability to capture consent for personal data processing where personal data is provided, and ability to lodge grievances anonymously without preventing processing (subject to agreed procedures for follow-up limitations).
Attachment handling: capture and secure storage of supporting evidence/attachments with configurable file type and size limits, and access controls aligned to grievance sensitivity and user permissions.
Data quality monitoring: configuration of data completeness checks and operational prompts to reduce incomplete records, and enable reliable exports for analysis and statutory/program reporting.
Implementation shall align to the agreed standardized form and data dictionary provided as Phase 1 inputs, and shall be reflected consistently in both public-facing and assisted intake workflows.
c) Enable role-based access, auditability, and secure handling of personal and sensitive grievance data in line with applicable legal and policy requirements.
The Supplier shall implement privacy-by-design and security-by-design controls proportionate to the sensitivity of grievance information. This shall include:
Role-based access control (RBAC) and least privilege: role and permission structures for national, county and municipal users, limiting visibility and actions to what is required for each role.
Sensitive grievance controls: restricted visibility, controlled routing and appropriate notification suppression/redaction for sensitive grievance types, with enhanced logging of access attempts and actions on such cases.
Audit trails and traceability: immutable or tamper-evident logs of key actions (e.g., create/view/update, assignment, status changes, communications, configuration changes) with timestamps and user identifiers to support oversight and investigations.
Secure transmission and storage: secure configurations for data in transit and at rest, and protection of attachments/evidence consistent with access controls.
Retention and archiving controls: configuration options to support retention, archiving, and controlled disposal/anonymization where required by the agreed policy and lawful requirements.
Operational security readiness: security and compliance documentation and testing evidence to demonstrate that required controls are implemented and functioning as configured.
These controls shall support the e-GRM’s compliance posture and protect complainants, staff, and the integrity of the GRM process.
d) Deliver dashboards, reports, and exports required for operational monitoring and programme reporting.
The Supplier shall configure dashboards and reporting outputs that enable both day-to-day operational management and consolidated programme reporting across counties and municipalities. This shall include:
Operational dashboards for volumes, status distribution, overdue/at-risk cases, workload by unit/assignee, and pipeline views (received → in progress → resolved/closed).
Performance/timeliness reporting (including configurable service level monitoring and escalation triggers) to support management follow-up and performance reviews.
Disaggregation and filtering by time period, county, municipality, intake channel, category/help topic, priority, and other agreed attributes.
Exports in common formats (e.g., CSV/Excel) to support analysis, audits, and integration into programme reporting workflows, including production of aggregate/anonymized outputs suitable for disclosure where required.
Audit-ready outputs, including the ability to export a complete grievance case file (subject to permissions) and export activity logs for compliance verification.
e) Deliver training, user support materials, and handover artifacts to enable sustainable operation by national, county and municipal users.
The Supplier shall implement a structured training and knowledge transfer package to ensure that the Client can operate, administer and extend the e-GRM after commissioning. This shall include:
Role-based training for (i) system administrators; (ii) supervisors/managers; (iii) grievance handlers; and (iv) reporting users, including practical workflows and confidentiality expectations.
Training materials and job aids (user guides, administrator guides, quick reference guides, SOPs/scripts for assisted intake) in formats suitable for reuse in ongoing onboarding.
Configuration and “as-built” documentation describing key configurations (forms, routing rules, roles, templates, SLAs, integrations), including version control and a practical operating model for routine administration.
Handover artifacts sufficient to support continuity (including configuration snapshots and documented procedures for data export/backup, user management, and routine operational monitoring).
Training and materials shall be suitable for use across dispersed locations and shall support sustainable operation by both NPCT and sub-national teams.
f) Achieve go-live readiness, complete user acceptance testing, and provide post go-live stabilization (hypercare) within the three-month window.
The Supplier shall drive implementation to verified operational readiness, including:
Testing readiness and evidence: functional, integration, regression, performance, security, and accessibility testing proportionate to the implementation scope, with documented test artifacts and defect remediation tracking.
UAT support: preparation of UAT scripts mapped to agreed workflow scenarios; support to Client-led UAT execution; and delivery of a UAT results/sign-off pack (or agreed conditional sign-off plan where residual risks are documented and accepted).
Go-live and cutover: production deployment, cutover planning, and operational readiness checks (user access, monitoring, backup/restore readiness, configuration freeze/versioning as applicable).
Hypercare stabilization: post go-live support focused on rapid issue resolution, monitoring of early operational performance, and completion of final handover deliverables within the implementation period.
## 2.3 Expected Outcomes and Benefits
Expected outcomes and benefits include:
A single, standardized system of record for KUSP2 grievances across participating counties and municipalities, consolidating multi-channel intake into one register and reducing fragmentation, duplication, and inconsistent documentation.
Improved accessibility through multiple channels and disability-inclusive design, including a baseline of web and assisted intake and the ability to enable additional channels (e.g., email, SMS/USSD, hotline) where applicable/available, complemented by accessible user interfaces and inclusive user guidance.
Improved timeliness and consistency of grievance processing through configurable workflows, service-level monitoring, escalation triggers, reminders, and task/action tracking—reducing reliance on ad-hoc follow-up and improving predictability for complainants and managers.
Improved transparency and accountability through structured acknowledgements, status tracking, communications history, and auditable action logs that clearly show who did what, when, and why, while safeguarding confidentiality where required.
Improved management information for decision-making, compliance monitoring, and continuous improvement, including dashboards and disaggregated reports to identify grievance trends, bottlenecks, recurring issues, and systemic corrective actions required at municipal, county, or national level.
Collectively, these outcomes strengthen stakeholder trust, support programme accountability, and improve feedback loops between service providers and communities participating in or affected by KUSP2 activities.
## 2.4 Phase Structure and Intended Results
This procurement relates to Phase 2 (Implementation, 3 months). Phase 1 outputs (requirements, workflows, forms, reporting needs, risk and compliance matrices, and procurement documentation) will be made available to the Implementing Firm as implementation inputs. The Implementing Firm shall validate these inputs during mobilization and implement the solution accordingly, without redefining the agreed functional intent.
To operationalize this requirement in Phase 2, the Supplier is expected to:
Conduct a structured requirements confirmation and gap analysis during mobilization, translating Phase 1 outputs into an implementation/configuration backlog, an agreed delivery plan, and an evidence-based testing and training approach.
Prioritize configuration-first delivery (consistent with technology neutrality), demonstrating early working increments of the core intake and case management capabilities, then extending to additional channels, reporting, and compliance controls as agreed.
Apply disciplined change control so that any proposed changes to agreed requirements are documented, assessed for schedule/compliance impact, and approved through the Client’s governance arrangements before implementation.
Deliver commissioning outcomes within the three-month window, including go-live readiness, UAT completion, hypercare stabilization, and a complete handover package for sustained operations by NPCT and sub-national users.
# 3. SCOPE OF WORK
## 3.1 Phase 1: Design and Procurement Preparation (Completed – Provided as Inputs)
Phase 1 activities are not part of this Supplier’s scope. However, Phase 1 outputs constitute the baseline implementation package for Phase 2 and shall be treated as the authoritative reference for functional intent, standardized datasets, reporting requirements, and compliance expectations. # 4. FUNCTIONAL SCOPE OF THE E-GRM
This section defines the minimum functional capabilities that the e-GRM shall provide as a programme-wide system of record for grievances linked to KUSP2. The intent is to ensure consistent end-to-end grievance handling across participating counties and municipalities while enabling decentralized operations (municipal/county handling with national oversight), multi-channel access, and robust auditability. The functional framework below shall be implemented in a technology-neutral manner, prioritizing configuration over customization where feasible, while still meeting all required outcomes and controls.
## 4.1 Case Intake and Registration
The solution shall support the registration of grievances from multiple channels and create a single system record per grievance. Each registered grievance shall receive a unique reference number used for tracking across all channels.
In addition to the requirements listed below, intake and registration functions shall be designed to: (i) minimize barriers to access (including for low-literacy and low-connectivity users), (ii) standardize data capture to enable consistent reporting and escalation, and (iii) prevent loss of grievances submitted outside the web channel by ensuring staff-assisted workflows are equally robust and auditable.
a) Intake channels: at minimum, web form and staff-assisted entry; and where enabled by Client arrangements, email, toll-free phone intake, and SMS/USSD intake.
The solution shall ensure that all enabled channels feed into one unified grievance register (single system of record), with consistent numbering, categorization, timestamps, and status tracking.
Staff-assisted entry shall support grievances received through in-person walk-ins, community meetings, letters, or other offline channels by enabling authorized users to register these grievances in the system using the same standardized intake fields and validation rules.
For SMS/USSD and phone-based intake (where enabled), the solution shall support “minimum viable intake” (short, structured capture) and a follow-on workflow to complete additional details through staff follow-up, while still generating a reference number immediately and recording the intake channel.
The solution shall avoid use of automated chatbots for grievance intake; user support may be provided via guided forms, tooltips, and FAQs/knowledge resources that help users submit complete grievances without replacing human-assisted channels where needed.
b) Ability to submit grievances anonymously, without preventing processing; where contact details are provided, they shall be captured with consent and according to the complainant’s preferred method of contact.
The solution shall allow a complainant to lodge a grievance without providing personally identifying details, while still capturing sufficient information to enable triage, assignment and action.
For anonymous grievances, the system shall still provide the unique reference number and enable status tracking through agreed mechanisms (e.g., reference number plus a secure verifier such as an email/phone where provided, or other agreed approach that does not expose personal data).
The system shall clearly distinguish anonymous grievances in staff views and apply appropriate rules (e.g., complainant notifications not possible unless a contact method exists).
Where contact details are provided, the solution shall support capture of preferred contact method (e.g., SMS, email, phone, post, in-person), and enable channel-specific acknowledgement and follow-up consistent with the complainant’s preference and consent.
c) Ability for a representative to submit on behalf of another person or group, including capture of representative details and confirmation of authorization.
The solution shall support recording whether the grievance is submitted by: (i) the affected person, (ii) a representative, or (iii) a group/organization.
Where a representative submits, the system shall capture representative identity and contact details, relationship/role (e.g., family member, community leader, CSO), and a confirmation/attestation of authorization (as per the standardized intake form).
The solution shall support recording whether the complainant is an individual or group, and—where applicable—capture basic group identifiers (e.g., group name) without over-collecting personal data.
d) Ability to capture and store supporting evidence/attachments, with configurable limits and accepted file types.
The solution shall support attaching one or more files at intake and during processing (e.g., photos, letters, PDFs), while enforcing configurable controls (file size limits, permitted file types, maximum number of files).
Attachment handling shall inherit access controls from the grievance record (i.e., restricted grievances must also restrict their attachments).
The solution shall support capturing basic attachment metadata (upload time, uploader role, filename/type) to support auditability and chain-of-custody for sensitive evidence.
Where feasible in the hosting environment, the solution should support malware scanning and safe handling of uploaded files to reduce security risk.
e) Data validation and completeness checks consistent with the standardized grievance intake form (see Appendix A).
The solution shall enforce mandatory fields and conditional requirements (e.g., representative fields required if “submitted by representative” is selected).
Validation shall include format checks (e.g., email/phone formats), controlled lists (e.g., counties/municipalities), and minimum content rules for narrative fields to reduce unusable submissions.
The system shall support “save-as-draft” or staged completion for staff-assisted intakes where information is collected over multiple touchpoints, while maintaining audit trails of changes.
The solution shall support basic duplicate detection and handling (e.g., flag potential duplicates based on contact details, location, date, and similar subject text) and allow authorized users to link related grievances rather than losing visibility through manual deletion.
f) Automatic capture of date/time, channel, location fields (county, city/municipality, site), and categorization fields.
The solution shall automatically record intake timestamp, intake channel/source, and the user/agent (for staff-created records).
Location fields shall be standardized using reference lists (counties/municipalities) that can be updated administratively over time.
The solution shall support capturing additional location detail (e.g., ward, neighbourhood, project site name/identifier) where required by the agreed data standards, while keeping optionality where not available.
Categorization shall align to agreed help topics/categories and support multi-select where applicable (e.g., a grievance that has both service delivery and environmental concerns), subject to agreed classification rules.
g) Explicit statement and capture of consent to personal data use at the point of intake, aligned to the privacy notice.
Where personal data is collected, the solution shall present a privacy notice and capture explicit consent (including timestamp and channel).
The solution shall support differentiated consent handling where feasible (e.g., consent to be contacted via SMS/email, consent to store personal data for follow-up), aligned to applicable requirements and Client policy.
Where consent is not provided, the system shall support lawful alternatives consistent with the programme approach (e.g., allowing anonymous submission or limiting data capture to non-identifying content).
h) Ability to record additional contacts/collaborators for a grievance (e.g., copied stakeholders), with controlled visibility and consent considerations.
The solution shall support adding additional participants/collaborators (internal or external) where needed for coordination, while allowing administrators to control whether collaborators can view the full grievance record or only receive notifications.
Any external collaborator contact details shall be captured and used in line with consent and data minimization principles.
The system shall provide clear separation between “complainant contacts” and “internal stakeholders copied for action,” to avoid accidental disclosure of complainant identity.
## 4.2 Case Processing, Assignment and Escalation
The solution shall support an end-to-end workflow from receipt to closure, including assignment to responsible units and escalation where timelines are breached or where a higher level of decision-making is required.
This workflow shall be designed to support decentralized handling (municipal/county resolution) with clear escalation pathways and auditable decision-making at each stage, including referrals to specialized entities where appropriate.
a) Configurable workflow statuses aligned to agreed GRM practice (e.g., Open/Pending, Resolved, Referred, Escalated, Closed) and the ability to add sub-statuses where required.
The solution shall allow the Client to configure: status names, allowable transitions (e.g., Open → Resolved → Closed; Open → Referred; Open → Escalated), and sub-statuses that improve operational clarity (e.g., “Awaiting Complainant Information,” “Under Investigation,” “Action in Progress”).
The system shall support reopening (or equivalent) where a grievance is closed prematurely or an appeal is raised, with full audit trail and reason capture.
Status change actions shall require appropriate permissions and shall be recorded in the case history with user, date/time, and rationale (where configured as mandatory).
b) Assignment rules based on county/municipality, grievance category/help topic, and responsible department/unit; ability for authorized users to reassign.
The solution shall support automated routing to the appropriate queue/department/unit using configurable rules based on geography, category, and other intake attributes.
Authorized users shall be able to reassign grievances (e.g., if misrouted), with mandatory reason capture where configured, and notification to the receiving unit as appropriate.
The system shall support assignment to individuals and/or teams, enabling workload balancing and continuity during leave/turnover.
The solution shall support escalation/transfer between municipal, county and national levels using clearly defined routing logic and visibility controls.
c) Priority levels (e.g., Low/Normal/High/Emergency) and due dates/target timelines; visual indicators for overdue grievances and escalation triggers.
The solution shall support configurable priority/severity levels and associated handling rules (e.g., shorter target timelines for urgent cases).
The system shall calculate and display due dates based on configured service standards (acknowledgement, response, resolution) and working calendars (business hours, weekends/holidays where applicable).
The solution shall provide dashboards/queues for “due soon,” “overdue,” and “escalation triggered,” and shall support automated reminders and escalations when thresholds are reached.
Escalation rules shall support both time-based triggers (e.g., overdue) and category-based triggers (e.g., sensitive cases routed to specialized focal persons).
e) Internal action log and notes for investigation and follow-up steps; ability to capture referrals and escalation decisions with dates and reasons.
The system shall maintain a chronological case history capturing all key actions (assignment, status changes, communications, referrals, attachments, approvals).
Internal notes shall be distinguishable from external communications and visible only to authorized users.
The solution shall support structured capture of investigation activities and outcomes (e.g., actions taken, site visit conducted, evidence reviewed), including attachments and links to referenced documents.
For referrals/escalations, the system shall capture: referral target (unit/level/agency), referral reason, date, and any reference numbers used by external bodies, to ensure traceability.
f) Support for confidential handling of sensitive grievances (including SEA/SH) through restricted visibility and controlled routing.
The solution shall support classification/flagging of sensitive cases and automatically apply enhanced confidentiality controls (restricted queues, limited roles able to view, masked fields where appropriate).
The system shall support survivor-centred handling principles by minimizing capture of sensitive narrative in free-text fields and enabling structured referral tracking where required by agreed protocols.
Notifications for sensitive cases shall be configurable to avoid disclosure through insecure channels (e.g., SMS messages should not include sensitive content).
The solution shall ensure all access to sensitive records is logged and reviewable for compliance and accountability.
g) Ability to flag, tag, or mark grievances for follow-up and to schedule reminders/tasks linked to grievance handling timelines.
The solution shall enable staff to apply tags/flags (e.g., “requires site visit,” “legal review,” “pending contractor response”) to support prioritization and management oversight.
The system shall support task/reminder functionality linked to grievances (e.g., follow-up call date, inspection date), with due dates and assignment to responsible staff, and visibility aligned to permissions.
The solution shall support linking related grievances (and where supported, merging duplicates) to improve trend analysis and reduce fragmented handling.
## 4.3 Communications and Notifications
The solution shall support structured communications with complainants and internal stakeholders, while protecting confidentiality and adhering to consent and contact preferences.
Communications functions shall support predictable, documented interaction with complainants, reduce ad hoc follow-ups, and create a complete case record of what was communicated, when, and by whom.
a) Acknowledgement of receipt with reference number and date, generated automatically where possible and otherwise recordable as a manual action.
The solution shall support automatic acknowledgements for digital channels (e.g., web form, email) and a “record acknowledgement” action for staff-assisted channels (e.g., in-person, phone) to ensure consistent documentation.
Acknowledgements shall include the grievance reference number, basic next steps, and where appropriate a link or instructions for status tracking—without exposing sensitive personal data.
The system shall support acknowledgement templates per channel and per language (at minimum English and Kiswahili for user-facing content).
b) Two-way communication log per grievance (messages, responses, call notes, letters), with distinction between internal notes and external communications.
The solution shall maintain a structured thread that captures complainant messages and staff responses, and separately captures internal notes and deliberations.
Call notes (hotline follow-ups), meeting notes, and outgoing letters shall be recordable with timestamps and responsible staff identifiers.
Where communications include attachments, these shall be stored securely with the grievance record and protected by the same access controls.
c) Notification rules and templates for key events (registration, assignment, request for additional information, resolution, closure, appeal), delivered through selected channels (SMS, email, phone scripts, printable letters) subject to consent and channel availability.
The system shall support configurable event-based notifications to: (i) complainants, (ii) assigned staff/teams, and (iii) supervisors/managers for escalations and overdue cases.
Templates shall support variable substitution (e.g., reference number, status, next steps) while allowing administrators to restrict/disable variables that would expose sensitive information.
The solution shall support channel fallbacks (e.g., if SMS delivery fails, switch to email where available) and recording of delivery failures to support follow-up.
Phone scripts and printable letter templates shall be available for offline communications, ensuring standard messaging and documentation.
d) Message history retained in the grievance record with timestamps and sender/recipient metadata.
The system shall record the date/time, sender identity (user/role), recipient (complainant/contact method), channel used, and message status (sent/failed/manual) where applicable.
The solution shall support audit-ready extraction of communication history as part of a grievance case file for internal review and lawful requests.
e) Ability to withhold complainant identity from downstream actors where confidentiality is requested, while still enabling investigation and resolution.
The solution shall support configurable data masking and role-based field visibility so that units handling actions can access necessary issue details without automatically receiving identifying complainant data.
The system shall support controlled sharing of identity only where required for resolution, and only to roles authorized under agreed procedures, with all access logged.
## 4.4 Appeals and Closure
The solution shall support recording complainant satisfaction and managing appeals/escalations for grievances where the complainant is not satisfied with the resolution.
Closure and appeal handling shall ensure decisions are well documented, consistently communicated, and traceable, while supporting escalation pathways and continuous improvement.
a) Capture of resolution summary, resolution date, and evidence of communication of the outcome to the complainant.
The system shall support structured capture of resolution details, including: summary outcome, key actions taken, responsible unit/approver (where applicable), and resolution date.
Evidence of communication shall include a record of the channel used, message content (or reference to template), and date/time; where physical letters are used, the system shall allow uploading/scanning proof of dispatch if available.
The solution shall support categorization of closure reason/outcome codes (e.g., resolved, referred to another authority, insufficient information, duplicate/merged) to improve reporting and learning.
b) Capture of complainant response (Satisfied / Not satisfied / Not applicable for anonymous).
The solution shall record complainant satisfaction status and (where feasible) optional feedback comments.
For anonymous grievances, the system shall allow closure without satisfaction capture while maintaining clear reporting separation for “not applicable due to anonymity/unreachable.”
The system shall support linking satisfaction capture to the closure workflow (e.g., prompt to record satisfaction before final closure, where appropriate).
c) Ability to trigger an appeal/escalation workflow where the complainant is not satisfied, including re-assignment to the next level.
The solution shall support appeal initiation through both complainant channels (where enabled) and staff-initiated escalation.
Appeal handling shall capture: appeal reason, date, receiving level/unit, and the outcome of the appeal decision, with corresponding communications logged.
The system shall support escalation between levels (municipality → county → national) consistent with agreed governance and decision-making structures.
d) Closure controls requiring mandatory completion of key fields and documentation before final closure.
The solution shall support a closure checklist approach, enforcing completion of mandatory fields (e.g., status, resolution summary, dates, responsible unit, communication record) before allowing closure.
The system shall support supervisory review/approval for closure of specific categories (e.g., high priority, escalated, sensitive) where required by agreed procedures.
The solution shall support preventing closure when critical linked tasks remain open, where task management is enabled.
e) Archiving and retention controls for closed grievances consistent with the agreed retention policy.
The system shall support archiving of closed grievances, preserving full audit trails and attachments while limiting access according to role and need.
Retention controls shall support retention periods by grievance category where applicable (e.g., different handling for sensitive cases), and shall allow controlled anonymization/deletion in line with policy and applicable requirements.
The solution shall support “legal hold” or equivalent preservation controls for cases under investigation or audit, preventing deletion or alteration while the hold is active.
## 4.5 Reporting, Dashboards and Analytics
The solution shall provide operational dashboards and reporting outputs to support day-to-day management and programme reporting, without exposing personal data inappropriately.
Reporting shall serve both operational management (workload, overdue cases, escalation) and programme-level accountability (trend analysis, compliance monitoring, performance reporting), while enabling safe aggregation and disclosure where required.
a) Dashboards for volumes, categories, status distribution, resolution timelines, overdue grievances, and workload by unit/assignee.
Dashboards shall support national, county and municipal views consistent with permissions and data segmentation.
Core metrics should include: total received, open/backlog, resolved/closed, overdue, escalated, referred, and average/median handling times (acknowledgement, response, resolution as configured).
Workload views shall support queue-level and assignee-level monitoring to identify bottlenecks and support resource planning.
b) Filtering by time period, county, city/municipality, channel, category/help topic, priority, and other agreed attributes.
The solution shall support interactive filtering and drill-down from national to county/municipal levels where permitted.
Users shall be able to save commonly used filters/views (e.g., “My open grievances,” “Overdue in my municipality,” “SEA/SH aggregate view”) subject to role permissions.
The system shall support search and filtering across key fields, including reference number and structured taxonomy fields.
c) Standard reports and exports (e.g., CSV/Excel/PDF) for periodic reporting and audits, including aggregate/anonymized outputs suitable for public disclosure where required.
The solution shall support export of operational lists and summary tables in open, commonly used formats, with configurable selection of fields (and default exclusion of personal data where not needed).
The system shall support generating printable reports for management meetings and audit reviews, and exporting data for external analysis (e.g., M&E).
Where public disclosure is required, the system shall support anonymized/aggregated reporting outputs that remove identifying details and suppress small-number disclosure where appropriate.
e) Ability to generate reports aligned to KUSP2 reporting requirements (e.g., resolution within target timelines such as 30 days where feasible) and to support analysis of trends over time.
The solution shall support SLA/timeliness reporting aligned to configured service standards (including acknowledgement/response/resolution targets as agreed), and allow these standards to be updated over time without redevelopment.
Trend analytics shall support identifying recurrent issues by category, location, and implementing unit, enabling corrective action planning and learning loops.
Reports shall support tracking of escalations and referrals to inform governance performance and system improvements.
f) Audit-ready logs of actions and status changes, and the ability to export a complete grievance case file for internal reviews or lawful external requests.
The solution shall maintain auditable case histories showing “who did what, when, and why” for all key case actions (assignment, status change, communications, edits, access where applicable).
The system shall support exporting a complete case file (including metadata, communications log, action history, and attachments where authorized) in a structured format suitable for audits, investigations, and lawful information requests.
Access to audit logs and full case file exports shall be permission-controlled and recorded in administrative logs.

Accordingly, the Supplier shall:
Implement integrations using documented interfaces (e.g., RESTful APIs, webhooks, standard mail protocols) and avoid hard-coded dependencies on proprietary connectors that cannot be maintained by the Client or future suppliers.
Provide export capability sufficient for operational continuity and compliance, including:
Routine reports and extracts (CSV/Excel/PDF or equivalent common formats).
Full data export capability for transition (including metadata, audit logs, reference data, and configuration settings as applicable).
Ensure the system remains configurable rather than code-dependent for routine program changes, including categories/help topics, organizational units, SLAs, templates, routing rules, and reporting views—so that the Client can update the system without repeated vendor re-development.
Document interfaces and configurations sufficiently to support maintainability and future competition for support/enhancements.
## 6.6 Intellectual Property and Licensing
The Supplier shall clearly describe licensing terms for any third-party components and ensure the Client has the rights needed to operate the system sustainably. Ownership and handover of system artifacts (configuration, documentation, and where applicable source code) shall be defined contractually.
At minimum, the Supplier shall:

Ensure the Client has legally sufficient rights to:
Use the system for the duration required (including beyond the Supplier’s engagement).
Access and use documentation and configuration artifacts.
Obtain necessary administrator-level access to operate the system (including user management, configuration updates, and report generation).
Where custom development is undertaken, define and deliver:
Ownership/rights and handover arrangements for source code and build artifacts (where applicable under the contract).
As-built configuration documentation and environment-specific deployment notes.
Avoid licensing constructs that undermine sustainability, such as:
Mandatory ongoing subscription to access core records,
Restrictions that prevent exporting data/configuration,
“Black box” components without documentation or supportability.
## 6.7 Environmental and Social Safeguards Alignment
The solution shall support KUSP2’s environmental and social commitments, including inclusive access, confidentiality, non-retaliation, and survivor-centred approaches for SEA/SH-related grievances.
Accordingly, the Supplier shall ensure the e-GRM:
Enables safe and inclusive access through multiple channels, including assisted intake pathways and designs suitable for low-connectivity settings.
Supports confidential and sensitive-case handling, including:
Configurable sensitive flag/classification,
Restricted visibility and routing to designated focal persons,
Suppression/redaction controls for notifications and exports,
Structured referral tracking that minimizes storage of highly sensitive narrative.
Embeds non-retaliation and confidentiality messaging in public-facing guidance and staff training materials, aligned to Client-approved content.
Supports monitoring and reporting that enables safeguards oversight while preventing inappropriate disclosure of personal data (e.g., aggregate reporting for sensitive cases by default; export restrictions and audit logs for PII access).
The Supplier shall integrate these safeguards requirements into:
System configuration (roles, workflows, templates, routing rules),
Training and SOPs (including staff handling protocols),
Testing and acceptance evidence (including validation of restricted access and safe-notification behavior).
# 7. GOVERNANCE, REPORTING & COORDINATION
## 7.1 Client Oversight and Decision-Making Structure
The Client (SDHUD/NPCT) is the system owner and will provide strategic oversight, approve key deliverables, and lead acceptance decisions throughout Phase 2. Governance for Phase 2 shall be structured to enable (i) timely decision-making, (ii) strong quality assurance and compliance oversight, and (iii) practical coordination with counties and municipalities to support adoption.
a) Core oversight principles
Single ownership and accountability: SDHUD/NPCT retains ownership of the eGRM, including ownership of grievance data, program-facing performance reporting, and decisions on go-live readiness and acceptance.
Decentralized participation with centralized standards: County Program Coordination Teams (CPCTs) and municipal GRM focal points participate in validation, testing, and training, while NPCT ensures consistent national standards (workflows, data fields, reporting outputs, confidentiality controls).
Evidence-based approvals: Approvals shall be based on documented evidence (demonstrations, test results, training completion records, security/accessibility checks, and deliverable acceptance criteria).
Technology-neutral oversight: Governance focuses on outcomes, controls, and deliverables rather than mandating a specific vendor platform or architecture.
b) Governance layers and working arrangements (minimum)
To support implementation within three months, the following practical governance arrangements shall be applied (and refined during mobilization):
Steering / Management Oversight (NPCT-led): Provides direction, resolves escalations, and approves key stage gates (Implementation Plan, UAT sign-off, go-live, handover).
Implementation Working Group (NPCT + Supplier core team): Manages day-to-day execution, delivery planning, backlog prioritization, dependency tracking, and resolution of implementation blockers.
County/Municipal User Group (representative CPCTs and municipal focal points): Provides user validation input, participates in sprint reviews/demos, supports UAT execution, and validates field usability and operational practicality (including low-bandwidth and assisted intake realities).
Compliance and Safeguards Oversight (NPCT + designated focal persons): Confirms privacy-by-design, confidentiality arrangements (including sensitive cases), accessibility requirements, and adherence to relevant legal/policy obligations.
c) Decision rights and approval authority
NPCT as acceptance authority: NPCT is the final authority for formal acceptance of deliverables and go-live readiness. Counties/municipalities provide operational validation and user acceptance participation, but NPCT issues final sign-off.
Supplier execution responsibility: The Supplier is responsible for executing agreed activities, producing deliverables and evidence packs, and proposing mitigation actions where risks or dependencies threaten delivery.
Structured escalation pathway: Issues that cannot be resolved at working level within agreed timelines shall be escalated to NPCT leadership per the escalation mechanism defined in the Implementation Plan.
d) Documentation discipline and record of decisions
To ensure traceability and auditability, the Supplier shall maintain and share with the Client, at minimum:
A Decision Log (unique ID, decision description, options considered, decision owner, decision date, implications).
An Action Tracker (assigned actions, owners, due dates, status).
A Deliverables Register (deliverable IDs, versions, submission dates, review comments, acceptance status).
A Central Repository (secure, access-controlled) for project artifacts, configuration workbooks, test evidence, training materials, and handover items.

Weekly status updates shall be concise (ideally 1–2 pages) and include:

Planned next period activities: What will be delivered next week and which stakeholders are required (e.g., review sessions, UAT participation).

Channel readiness status (web, email, hotline, SMS/USSD—where enabled) including failure rates and mitigation
Updated risk register aligned to the agreed risk matrix, including trend analysis (new risks, closed risks, risks escalated)
Summary of change requests raised/approved/rejected and their impact

Unique ID, title, description, category (technical / compliance / stakeholder / dependency / operational)
Severity/priority, owner, target resolution date, current status

Resolution notes and evidence of closure (e.g., retest evidence)

Time-bound escalation for blockers unresolved within an agreed window (e.g., 48–72 hours for critical path items).
Escalation pathway from Working Group → NPCT accountable lead → Steering/Management review, with decisions recorded in the decision log.

The Supplier shall maintain a risk register and update it at least weekly, ensuring alignment to the agreed risk matrix and mitigation expectations.

## 7.5 Change Control and Configuration Management
Any changes to agreed requirements or scope shall follow a documented change control process. The Supplier shall maintain configuration management for forms, workflows, templates and integrations, including versioning and rollback procedures where applicable. The approach shall ensure that rapid implementation does not undermine auditability, data quality, or compliance controls.
a) Change control process (minimum steps)
Change Request (CR) submission: Any stakeholder may propose a change, but it shall be logged formally with rationale.
Impact assessment: The Supplier assesses impact on timeline, cost (if applicable), quality, compliance, training, and supportability.
Classification: CRs categorized (e.g., defect fix vs enhancement vs compliance requirement vs operational preference).
Approval / rejection: NPCT approves/rejects CRs based on program priorities and phase constraints; approvals recorded in the decision log.
Implementation and testing: Approved CRs implemented through controlled configuration/development, with regression testing evidence.
Documentation update: Update configuration workbook, release notes, training materials, and any affected SOPs.
Deployment and communication: Changes deployed via agreed release procedures and communicated to relevant users/stakeholders.
b) Configuration management (forms, workflows, templates, routing rules)
The Supplier shall:
Maintain a configuration baseline (what is configured, where, when, and why), including forms/data fields, workflow statuses, routing rules, SLA settings, notification templates, and role definitions.
Apply version control to configuration artifacts and documentation (version numbers, dates, authors, change summaries).
Ensure traceability from agreed requirements to configured solution components and test cases (e.g., a requirements traceability mapping embedded in the configuration workbook or test pack).
Implement role-based control over who can modify configuration in each environment, with configuration changes logged and auditable.
c) Environment and release controls
The Supplier shall define rules for promotion between environments (e.g., dev → test → staging → production) and ensure that production changes are controlled and approved.
Releases shall include release notes identifying changes, resolved issues, known limitations, and any user-impacting updates (including updated templates or workflow steps).
Where feasible, configuration changes shall support rollback (restore prior configuration snapshot) to reduce downtime and mitigate implementation risk.
d) Data and reporting integrity under change
Any changes affecting data fields, taxonomies, or reporting outputs shall be assessed for downstream impacts, including:
Continuity of KPI definitions and report comparability over time
Data quality validation rules and mandatory field compliance
Access controls and confidentiality impacts (especially for sensitive grievance handling)
Export formats and program reporting needs at national and sub-national levels

Use dashboards and reports effectively for operational monitoring, programme reporting, and compliance oversight without inappropriate exposure of personal data.
### 12.1.2 Training approach and learning principles
The Supplier shall implement a blended, role-based approach designed for a decentralized operating model and varying levels of ICT familiarity:
Role-based tracks with targeted curriculum depth (handlers vs supervisors vs administrators).
Hands-on, scenario-driven training using realistic end-to-end case simulations (intake → triage → assignment → action logging → resolution → closure/appeal), including common and edge cases (anonymous grievance, representative submission, missing data, attachment upload, escalation trigger, complainant not reachable).
Progressive delivery aligned to system readiness, so training is delivered against the configured system (or a stable training/staging environment) and reinforced during pilot/UAT and hypercare.
Accessibility-conscious training delivery, ensuring materials and delivery methods are usable by participants with disabilities (e.g., screen-reader-friendly documents, clear fonts/structure, captioned recordings where produced).
Bilingual delivery at minimum in English and Kiswahili for user-facing training materials and key job aids, with provision to support further translation where directed by the Client.
### 12.1.3 Training needs confirmation and participant profiling

Target counties/municipalities participating in training (and pilot/UAT) and their nominated participants.
Participant role profiles (administrator, supervisor, handler, reporting user, call intake agent, safeguards focal person).
Digital readiness constraints (device availability, connectivity, ability to join virtual sessions).
Language considerations and any accommodation needs for participants with disabilities.
Outputs of this readiness check shall inform the training schedule, session sizes, and modality mix.
### 12.1.4 Role-based training tracks (minimum set)
The Supplier shall provide training tracks that, at minimum, cover the following groups (roles may overlap in smaller municipalities, but competencies must still be addressed):
Track A – System Administrators (NPCT and designated administrators): configuration, security controls, user and role management, audit logs, backup/restore procedures, configuration change control, template management, reference data updates (counties/municipalities/categories), and basic operational monitoring.
Track B – Supervisors/Managers (NPCT/CPCT/municipal leadership): workload monitoring, SLA/overdue management, escalation oversight, approval/closure checks, reporting and trend analysis, and governance responsibilities.
Track C – GRM Officers/Handlers (municipal/county/national case handlers): intake review, triage, categorization, assignment, investigation/action logging, communications, referrals, resolution documentation, closure controls, satisfaction capture, and appeals escalation.
Track D – Assisted Intake Agents (front office, hotline/call-handlers where applicable): assisted intake procedures, consent capture, confidentiality, reference number issuance, minimum data capture for low-bandwidth/phone contexts, scripts and escalation triggers.
Track E – M&E and Reporting Users: dashboards, standard reports, export procedures, anonymized/aggregated reporting principles, and interpretation of KPIs and trends for management action.
Track F – Safeguards / Sensitive Case Focal Persons (including SEA/SH focal persons): confidential intake options, restricted access workflows, safe referral recording, “minimum necessary data” discipline, and prevention of inappropriate notifications or disclosures.
### 12.1.5 Curriculum content (minimum modules)
Across the training tracks above, the Supplier shall cover the following modules at appropriate depth:
GRM fundamentals and KUSP2 context
GRM purpose, principles (accessibility, predictability, transparency, fairness), and link to accountability/service delivery.
Institutional roles across NPCT/CPCT/municipal levels, escalation principles, and non-retaliation commitments.
System overview and user journeys
Public-facing journey (where applicable): submitting a grievance, receiving acknowledgement/reference number, status tracking.
Staff journey: queues, assignment, action logging, communications, closure, reporting.
Standardized intake and data quality
Mandatory vs optional fields; representative submissions; anonymous submissions; consent fields and privacy notice.
Common data quality errors and how to avoid them (misclassification, missing location, inconsistent narratives).
Attachment handling rules and evidence management (file types/size limits, naming conventions, secure viewing).
Triage, categorization, assignment and escalation
Applying categories/help topics consistently; routing logic; reassignment controls.
Priority levels and how they relate to due dates/target timelines.
SLA concepts (acknowledgement/response/resolution targets) and escalation triggers (overdue rules, governance escalations).
Case handling and documentation discipline
Action logs: what must be recorded, minimum documentation standards, timestamps, and evidence of decisions.
Distinguishing internal notes vs external communications.
Closure controls: required fields, resolution summary quality, supporting documentation.
Communications and confidentiality controls
Consent-aware communications (preferred channel), template usage, message logging.
Avoiding disclosure of personal/sensitive data in insecure channels (e.g., minimizing sensitive content in SMS).
Withholding complainant identity where confidentiality is requested.
Sensitive grievance handling (SEA/SH and other confidential cases)
Restricted visibility workflows and “need-to-know” routing.
Survivor-centred approach: safe handling, referral workflow recording, and minimizing capture of sensitive narrative in free-text fields where procedures require structured referral recording.
Do’s and don’ts for system users to prevent accidental exposure (attachments, notifications, screenshots, exports).
Reporting and dashboards
Operational dashboards: volumes, categories, status distribution, overdue queues, workloads.
Programme reporting: disaggregation by geography/channel/category, trend analysis, escalation patterns.
Export procedures (CSV/Excel/PDF), anonymization principles, and audit-ready exports.
Operational continuity and incident-aware behaviors (user-level)
What to do during downtime or channel unavailability (temporary manual intake and later entry, consistent reference handling).
Recognizing and escalating suspected security/privacy incidents (who to contact, immediate steps).
### 12.1.6 Training assessment and verification (minimum)

Short pre/post checks (knowledge quizzes) for key tracks (handlers and administrators).
Practical competency checks using defined scenarios (e.g., each handler completes a case lifecycle exercise; administrators complete a configuration change exercise and demonstrate rollback/traceability).
Training feedback collection to identify gaps requiring targeted refresher sessions during hypercare.

Integration availability expectations (e.g., email/SMS/USSD gateway reachability, message delivery confirmation where supported), including handling of partial outages.
Define support service levels that clearly distinguish between:

Resolution time (time to implement fix or workaround), and
Restore time (time to restore service during an outage).

Configure and report on GRM operational KPIs that support day-to-day management and programme oversight, including at minimum:

Clear ownership of KPI review (NPCT, CPCT, municipal focal points) and the decision-making actions expected from KPI review (e.g., targeted coaching for low-performing units, configuration tuning for routing/escalation).

A “major incident” communication protocol (who is notified, how often updates are issued, and how resolution and closure are communicated).
Describe support channels and tooling including:
How users will log issues (e.g., ticketing system, email address, phone bridge) and minimum information required (reference number, screenshots, time of occurrence, user role, affected municipality, and steps to reproduce).
Ticket classification (incident vs service request vs change request) and routing rules.
Expectations for remote support versus on-site support (where required and feasible), and how such support will be scheduled and approved.
Specify third-party dependency escalation for telecoms and other providers (where channels are enabled), including how the Supplier will coordinate with Client-led commercial/regulatory arrangements while ensuring technical issue isolation, evidence collection, and tracking of third-party tickets.

# 14. RISK MANAGEMENT
Effective risk management is essential to delivering a production-ready e-GRM within the three-month Phase 2 window, while meeting governance, compliance, inclusion, and operational performance expectations. The e-GRM is being implemented in a context where broader programme risks (including institutional capacity and safeguards-related risks) are inherently elevated; therefore, risks must be managed proactively and transparently throughout implementation and early operations.
The Supplier shall apply active risk management as a core delivery discipline (not an administrative afterthought), integrating risk controls into planning, configuration/build, testing, training, go-live readiness, and hypercare. Risks and dependencies that could prevent delivery of the Phase 2 milestones must be surfaced early, with clear ownership and actionable mitigations agreed with the Client.
## 14.1 Risk Categories and Register
The Supplier shall maintain a living risk register covering technical, schedule, stakeholder, governance, compliance, and external dependency risks. The register shall be reviewed in weekly status meetings and escalated when required.
### 14.1.1 Baseline inputs and alignment
The Supplier shall use the Phase 1 risk matrix/risk register as the baseline, and update it for Phase 2 implementation realities (e.g., confirmed hosting model, integration endpoints, telecom readiness, rollout sequencing, and staffing availability).
The Supplier shall ensure risk management aligns with the agreed implementation governance and reporting cadence (weekly working meetings; monthly management reviews as applicable).
### 14.1.2 Minimum structure of the risk register
At minimum, the risk register shall be maintained in an Excel-ready format that can be shared and updated collaboratively, and shall include:
Risk ID and Risk Title
Risk description (cause → event → consequence), including what “failure” looks like in operational terms
Category (technical, stakeholder, governance, compliance, schedule, external)
Likelihood and Impact rating, and an overall Risk Rating
Mitigation measures (preventative and detective controls), with actions, due dates, and responsible parties
Contingency measures (what will be done if the risk materializes)
Risk Owner (NPCT / Supplier / County partners / joint / third party, as applicable)
Status (e.g., Open / In progress / Mitigated / Closed) and review date/notes
### 14.1.3 Risk rating approach and review discipline
The Supplier shall apply a consistent likelihood/impact assessment approach (e.g., Low/Medium/High for each dimension), combining these into an overall rating (e.g., High/Moderate/Low) to support prioritization and escalation decisions.
High-rated risks shall have explicit contingency plans and measurable early-warning indicators, and shall be reviewed as standing agenda items in weekly meetings until materially reduced.
### 14.1.4 Relationship to issue management and change control
The Supplier shall maintain a clear distinction between:
Risks (potential future events), and
Issues (risks that have already materialized or current blockers),
while ensuring traceability between the risk register, issue log, and decision log.
Material scope/configuration changes arising from risk responses shall follow the agreed change control and configuration management approach (including versioning and rollback where applicable).
## 14.2 Mitigation Measures and Owners
Key risks already identified for the e-GRM initiative include (non-exhaustive):
### 14.2.1. Role clarity and process ownership across national/county/municipal levels
Risk description and impact: Unclear ownership for triage, assignment, investigation, escalation, approvals, and closure can cause workflow stalling, inconsistent handling across counties/municipalities, and failure to meet grievance response timelines and reporting expectations.
Minimum mitigation measures:
Conduct an early RACI workshop (NPCT + CPCTs + municipal GRM focal points) to confirm who does what at each stage of the grievance lifecycle, including escalations and appeals, and to agree a practical escalation hierarchy that reflects KUSP2 operational realities.
Translate agreed roles into system configuration: queue/routing logic by geography and category, role-based permissions, approval steps (where required), and escalation triggers that route to the correct level when timelines are breached.
Produce and validate Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) and user job aids aligned to the configured workflows, ensuring users understand both the process and the system steps.
Indicative owner(s): Client (process ownership) with Supplier responsible for translating the agreed process into configuration and training outputs.
Early warning indicators / triggers:
High rates of reassignment, large volumes in “unassigned” queues, repeated status churn, or growing overdue backlog in early pilot/UAT.
Counties/municipalities reporting uncertainty about who is accountable for closure or escalation decisions.
### 14.2.2. Dependence on telecom services for toll-free hotline and SMS/USSD channels (approvals and lead times)
Risk description and impact: Delays in securing short codes/toll-free arrangements or unreliable telecom performance can reduce accessibility, cause missed acknowledgements/notifications, and weaken public trust in the system at go-live.
Minimum mitigation measures:
Treat telecom readiness as a managed dependency in the risk register and implementation plan, with clear assumptions and decision points (e.g., “If USSD is not ready by [milestone], proceed with web + assisted intake and add USSD as a controlled enhancement.”).
Implement channel integrations with fallback paths, including:
assisted intake workflows to ensure grievances can still be captured into the unified register;
alternate notification channels where consent allows (e.g., email if SMS fails); and
explicit tracking of delivery failures in logs/monitoring to avoid “silent failures.”
Ensure telecom integrations comply with applicable requirements for secure handling of phone numbers and compliant messaging, recognizing that commercial/regulatory arrangements are Client-led while technical integration is Supplier-led.
Indicative owner(s): Client (commercial/regulatory arrangements) and Supplier (technical integration and monitoring).
Early warning indicators / triggers:
Short code/toll-free parameters not available by the agreed integration build window.
High SMS delivery failure rates in staging/pilot testing; network-specific failures during field testing.
### 14.2.3. Data protection and privacy compliance risks, including sensitive grievances and breach risk
Risk description and impact: Non-compliance or a privacy breach can create legal and reputational consequences and may expose complainants—particularly for sensitive cases—undermining uptake and trust.
Minimum mitigation measures:
Implement privacy-by-design controls: consent capture, data minimization, role-based access control, secure storage/transmission, audit logging, and retention/archiving controls as configured features (not “policy-only” commitments).
Ensure sensitive grievance types (including SEA/SH) have restricted workflows, safe communications, and controlled visibility so downstream handling does not inadvertently expose identities or sensitive details.
Provide compliance evidence artifacts during Phase 2 (e.g., access control matrix, audit log export samples, security configuration summary, and documented data flows).
Establish incident response readiness for security/privacy events, including contacts, decision rights, and containment steps aligned with Client obligations and agreed procedures.
Indicative owner(s): Supplier (controls and evidence) and Client (governance and oversight).
Early warning indicators / triggers:
Audit logs show inappropriate access attempts, excessive privileged activity, or repeated permission exceptions.
Users request access outside their role due to unclear role design or operational pressure (a sign of misconfigured RBAC and/or unclear process).
### 14.2.4. Stakeholder adoption and inclusion risks (low uptake, exclusion of vulnerable groups, capacity gaps at local level)
Risk description and impact: Low adoption or uneven adoption across counties/municipalities leads to continued parallel/manual registers, poor data quality, weak reporting, and persistent barriers for vulnerable groups (including persons with disabilities or those without smartphones/internet).
Minimum mitigation measures:
Deliver strong change management within the three-month window: role-based training, practical job aids, and supervised early usage during pilot/hypercare to build confidence and correct errors early.
Support inclusion through:
multi-channel access (including assisted intake);
low-bandwidth design; and
accessible user interfaces and testing evidence (involving users with disabilities where feasible).
Monitor operational adoption indicators through dashboards (e.g., logins, queue volumes, backlog/overdue rates, data completeness error rates) and use these as triggers for targeted refresher training and support.
Indicative owner(s): Joint responsibility (Client + Supplier).
Early warning indicators / triggers:
Low grievance capture volumes despite active outreach, low login rates, or persistent use of informal/manual registers.
High rates of incomplete intake fields, misclassification, or repeated reopening of cases due to weak documentation.
### 14.2.5. Integration and reliability risks (channel integrations, performance under load)
Risk description and impact: Integration failures (email/SMS/USSD/hotline, web embedding) and poor performance (especially search and reporting) can block day-to-day operations and undermine trust in the unified system of record.
Minimum mitigation measures:
Design and test integrations in staging with defined end-to-end scenarios before pilot/UAT; include monitoring for delivery failures and clear operational procedures for retries and exception handling.
Execute proportionate performance testing focused on reporting and search, and prioritize remediation of bottlenecks before go-live; use phased rollout (pilot) to manage reliability risk in a controlled manner.
Maintain operational readiness controls (backup/restore, monitoring, and configuration snapshots) as part of go-live readiness and hypercare planning.
Indicative owner(s): Supplier (technical delivery) with NPCT ICT oversight/coordination where applicable.
Early warning indicators / triggers:
Recurrent integration errors, high failure rates for notifications, or unacceptable report generation times during UAT/pilot.
Increased downtime or instability during pilot as volume and concurrency increase.
### 14.2.6. Political or governance changes affecting continuity and resourcing
Risk description and impact: Leadership transitions or shifting priorities at national/county levels may reduce buy-in, delay decisions, or weaken resourcing for GRM operations; interference risk can undermine credibility of the grievance process and discourage use.
Minimum mitigation measures:
Embed e-GRM oversight in programme governance and maintain a clear line of sight from operational metrics (volumes, timeliness, overdue rates) to accountability, enabling issues to be escalated beyond individual offices when necessary.
Sustain stakeholder engagement through transparent, aggregate reporting and clear public guidance on channels and non-retaliation/ confidentiality principles (within legal and policy limits).
Ensure continuity through documentation, structured handover, and super-user capacity building so knowledge survives staff turnover.
Indicative owner(s): Client leadership structures (SDHUD/NPCT) with support from counties and programme governance partners, and Supplier support for adoption/enablement activities where requested.
Early warning indicators / triggers:
Delayed approvals at key gates (Implementation Plan, UAT sign-off, go-live readiness).
Loss of designated focal persons or prolonged vacancies in key operational roles.
## 14.3 Contingency and Escalation Protocols
The Supplier shall define practical, time-bound escalation thresholds and continuity procedures, recognizing the compressed three-month delivery window and the operational criticality of grievance records. At minimum, the following shall be defined and agreed during mobilization and refined through implementation:
### 14.3.1 Escalation thresholds
Escalation thresholds shall be specific enough to trigger action early, including:
Schedule / milestone slippage thresholds (e.g., any critical-path activity forecast to slip beyond the tolerance agreed in the Implementation Plan; failure to meet sprint exit criteria; delayed readiness of environments/integration parameters).
Quality thresholds (e.g., repeated failure of end-to-end workflow scenarios in demos/UAT; unacceptable defect backlog; inability to produce agreed reporting outputs).
Security/privacy thresholds (e.g., suspected unauthorized access, evidence of misconfigured permissions, or any incident with potential personal data exposure), triggering immediate incident response and Client notification per agreed procedures.
### 14.3.2 Fallback procedures for channel unavailability
The Supplier shall define channel fallback procedures that preserve a single unified grievance register and avoid “off-system” handling, including:
If SMS/USSD is delayed or experiences sustained outages, proceed with:
web portal intake (where available),
staff-assisted intake at municipal/county level, and
alternative acknowledgements/notifications (e.g., email or phone) where consent allows,
while ensuring all grievances are registered and tracked consistently in the e-GRM system.
If the toll-free hotline is delayed, implement interim phone intake scripts and staff-assisted entry processes, ensuring call notes are captured in the grievance record and linked to the reference number.
### 14.3.3 Manual continuity procedures for system downtime
The Supplier shall define a practical continuity procedure for temporary downtime, including:
Use of a temporary manual intake template (paper or spreadsheet) capturing the minimum standardized intake fields required for later entry.
A controlled method for assigning temporary reference numbers (or holding references) during downtime and reconciling them once service is restored.
A back-entry procedure (including QA checks) to ensure grievances received during downtime are entered into the system promptly and are not lost or duplicated.
Clear instructions on what can/cannot be recorded offline for sensitive grievances to preserve confidentiality and reduce risk.
### 14.3.4 Contingency actions for critical risks
For high-rated risks, the Supplier shall document contingency actions that can be executed rapidly, such as:
Phased rollout (pilot in selected municipalities first, then expand) to manage reliability and adoption risks while maintaining delivery confidence.
Scope sequencing within Phase 2 that protects Must requirements and compliance controls if external dependencies delay some channels, without losing the “single system of record” objective.
### 14.3.5 Escalation pathways and decision rights
The Supplier shall document and agree:
the escalation pathway from working team → NPCT focal points → management/steering level (as applicable);
decision rights for go/no-go items (e.g., channel enablement decisions, conditional go-live decisions with residual risk plans, and security incident containment measures); and
communication protocols for urgent incidents and major risks requiring rapid Client input.
# 15. SECURITY, PRIVACY & DATA MANAGEMENT
This section sets the minimum security, privacy, and data management requirements that the Supplier shall implement during Phase 2. Controls shall be proportionate to risk, consistent with privacy-by-design, and aligned to the approved Phase 1 compliance/risk inputs and the standardized intake/data collection requirements. The Supplier shall implement controls in a way that supports decentralized operations (national, county, and municipal users) while protecting complainants, preventing unauthorized access, and ensuring auditability and lawful processing of personal data.
## 15.1 Identity and Access Management
The Supplier shall implement role-based access control (RBAC) with least-privilege permissions for national, county and municipal users. The Supplier shall support segregation of duties and restricted access for sensitive grievances. The Supplier shall implement strong authentication and session controls and account lifecycle management (create, modify, disable).
To operationalize the above, the Supplier shall, at minimum:
a) RBAC model, scope, and segregation of duties
Implement a hierarchical but configurable access model that supports national oversight while limiting routine access to only the grievances and functions required by each user role (e.g., municipal handler, county supervisor, NPCT administrator, reporting-only user).
Separate privileged administration functions from day-to-day grievance handling functions, including (at minimum):
User/role administration
Configuration management (forms, workflows, templates, escalation rules)
Audit log access and export
Security settings (MFA enforcement, IP restrictions, retention settings)
Support segregation of duties for sensitive actions, where feasible, such as:
User provisioning vs. approval of privileged roles
Configuration change vs. configuration approval (or at minimum, configuration change logging plus Client review)
Case resolution vs. final closure approval for selected categories (where the GRM process requires supervisory closure).
b) Role and record-level access controls
Implement record-level controls that allow the Client to restrict grievance visibility and permissible actions based on parameters such as: location (county/municipality), category/help topic, assigned unit/team, escalation level, and sensitivity flags.
Provide field-level or section-level restrictions where required (e.g., mask or hide complainant identifiers and contact details from users who only need to see the issue details and resolution tasks).
Ensure attachments inherit the grievance’s access controls and cannot be accessed via “direct link” without authorization.
c) Sensitive grievance handling (“need-to-know” controls)
Implement restricted queues/workflows for sensitive grievances (including SEA/SH) with:
Limited visibility to explicitly authorized roles (designated focal persons and authorized supervisors)
Suppression or redaction of notifications that could inadvertently expose sensitive information
Additional access logging (including “view” events) and controlled export permissions
Optional “break-glass” access where the Client approves such a feature, with mandatory justification capture and heightened logging.
d) Authentication, session security, and hardening
Enforce unique user accounts for all staff users; shared accounts shall not be permitted.
Implement password policy controls (minimum length, complexity, reuse prevention, expiry policy if required by Client standards) and secure password reset processes.
Support multi-factor authentication (MFA) for administrative and other high-privilege roles, and provide the Client the ability to mandate MFA for all staff roles if desired.
Implement session controls including idle timeout, absolute session lifetime, secure cookie/token handling, protection against session fixation, and automatic logout on role removal/disablement.
Support account lockout / throttling for repeated failed login attempts and provide visibility of failed authentication events in system/security logs.
Where requested by the Client, support additional access restrictions such as IP allowlisting for staff and/or administrative interfaces.
e) Account lifecycle management and access governance
Provide controlled workflows or administrative procedures for user onboarding, role assignment, changes, and offboarding, including:
Defined authorization steps for granting privileged roles
Rapid disablement procedures for departing staff or reassigned roles
Periodic access review support (e.g., export of current users/roles for quarterly review by NPCT/CPCT)
Log all access-control and privilege changes (create/modify/disable accounts; role assignments; permission changes).
## 15.2 Encryption and Key Management
Encrypt data in transit using secure protocols. Encrypt stored data and sensitive attachments as appropriate; define key management approach.
The Supplier shall, at minimum:
a) Data in transit
Enforce secure transport (e.g., HTTPS/TLS) for all web-based access (public portal, staff console, administrative interfaces, and APIs).
Ensure secure configurations for integrations (email gateways, SMS/USSD aggregators, hotline/call-center tools where applicable), including authentication, integrity protection, and avoidance of transmitting sensitive details over insecure channels.
Ensure notifications sent via SMS/USSD or email are privacy-aware by design, and do not include unnecessary personal data or sensitive grievance content (use reference numbers and minimal status messaging).
b) Data at rest
Encrypt databases and file storage used to store grievance records and attachments, using encryption aligned to the hosting environment capabilities and risk level.
Apply stronger protection for particularly sensitive attachments where feasible (e.g., encryption plus strict access controls plus restricted export).
Ensure backups are encrypted and protected to the same (or higher) standard as production data.
c) Key management and secrets handling
Define and document a key management approach that covers: generation, storage, access controls, rotation, revocation, and recovery.
Restrict key access to the minimum administrative roles required, with audit logging of key-related administrative actions where supported by the platform.
Store credentials and secrets (API keys, database credentials, gateway credentials) in secure secret storage mechanisms appropriate to the chosen hosting model, and avoid hard-coding secrets in configuration files or source code.
Provide procedures for secure handover of credentials and secrets to the Client at go-live/handover, including a controlled method for credential rotation post-handover.
## 15.3 Audit Trails, Logs and Forensics
Immutable audit trail for key actions (create, view, edit, status change, assignment, communication). Administrative access logging and configuration change logging. Log retention and access procedures to support audits and investigations, consistent with privacy obligations.
The Supplier shall implement logging and auditability that is sufficient for oversight, accountability, and investigation of misuse while respecting data protection principles.
Minimum requirements include:
a) Audit trail scope (what must be captured)
Record auditable events for the full grievance lifecycle, including (at minimum):
Creation of grievances (by any channel)
Viewing/accessing a grievance record (especially for sensitive grievances)
Edits to core grievance fields (category, location, priority, due date, complainant info)
Status transitions (including reopen/appeal/escalation events)
Assignment/reassignment and routing rule actions
Creation/updates/completion of tasks linked to grievances (where implemented)
Outbound and inbound communications logged against the case (with channel metadata)
Attachment upload/download/view events (as feasible)
Record administrative events including: user creation/disablement; role assignment changes; permission changes; configuration changes to workflows/forms/templates; integration configuration changes; changes to retention/archiving settings; and enabling/disabling security controls such as MFA.
b) Audit record attributes and integrity controls
Each audit record shall include: actor identity, timestamp, action type, object/reference, and source context (e.g., IP address/device/session ID where feasible).
Implement controls to protect audit integrity (e.g., write-once/append-only configurations where possible; restricted delete permissions; administrative deletion—if permitted—must be itself auditable).
Ensure consistent time synchronization (system clocks) to support reliable correlation of events during audits and investigations.
c) Log access, retention, and privacy
Restrict access to audit logs and security logs to explicitly authorized roles and ensure access itself is logged.
Implement log retention settings aligned to the agreed policy and practical audit needs, with the ability to export logs for authorized internal reviews or lawful external requests.
Minimize exposure of personal data in logs (e.g., avoid logging full grievance narrative text in system error logs; avoid placing contact details in unprotected logs).
d) Forensic readiness
Provide procedures for evidence preservation and export for investigation (e.g., how to export a complete case file, audit history, and relevant logs while maintaining confidentiality).
Support the Client’s escalation and investigation workflows, including secure packaging of case files for authorized reviewers, and maintaining traceability of who exported what and when.
## 15.4 Data Classification, Retention and Disposal
The Supplier shall classify data according to sensitivity (personal data, sensitive grievance types, system metadata). The Supplier shall implement retention and disposal/anonymization rules for closed grievances in line with agreed policy and legal obligations. The Supplier shall provide administrative tools to support data subject requests (access, correction) and lawful deletion/anonymization where applicable.
Minimum requirements include:
a) Data classification model
Propose a practical classification scheme for e-GRM data, such as:
Public content (e.g., FAQs/process pages)
Operational content (case workflow metadata)
Personal data (complainant identifiers/contact details)
Sensitive grievance content (e.g., SEA/SH, integrity allegations, or other categories designated by the Client)
System/security metadata (audit logs, security events)
Apply classification through system configuration and access controls (e.g., restrict export fields, restrict visibility of contact details, isolate sensitive categories in restricted queues).
b) Retention and archiving
Implement configurable retention and archiving rules for:
Closed grievance records
Attachments and supporting evidence
Communications logs
Audit logs and system/security logs
Retention settings shall be aligned to the agreed policy and legal requirements, with the ability to implement different retention periods by grievance category/type if required (e.g., longer retention for audit-relevant categories, more restricted handling for sensitive categories).
Provide an “archive” state that preserves integrity and auditability while reducing routine visibility and operational load.
c) Disposal and anonymization
Implement controlled disposal processes at the end of retention periods, including:
Secure deletion or anonymization of personal data (as legally appropriate)
Controlled deletion of attachments, including ensuring that deleted files are not accessible via URLs or cached links
Clear administrative permissions and logging for disposal actions
Ensure disposal procedures address backups appropriately (e.g., define how deleted/anonymized records are handled in backup retention cycles).
d) Data subject rights and lawful requests
Provide administrative capabilities and documented procedures to support data subject requests and lawful requests, including:
Access and correction of personal data (where identity is verifiable)
Export of an individual case file for internal review or lawful external requests, subject to permissions and redaction rules
Where applicable, lawful deletion/anonymization workflows that preserve audit trails of the action taken (without retaining unnecessary personal data)
Define how requests will be handled for anonymous grievances (e.g., where identity cannot be verified, the system should still allow management of the grievance without collecting additional personal data).
e) Data quality and minimization
Reinforce data minimization by ensuring only necessary fields are collected for each channel and grievance type, and by limiting free-text capture for sensitive categories where agreed protocols require minimal data capture.
Implement validation rules, controlled vocabularies (reference data lists), and completeness checks in line with the standardized intake form to reduce inaccurate or incomplete records and to support consistent reporting.
## 15.5 Incident Response and Breach Notification
The Supplier shall define and document an incident response process, including containment, eradication, recovery and lessons learned. The Supplier shall support Client obligations for notification and reporting in the event of a reportable personal data breach.
Minimum requirements include:
a) Incident response process and governance
Define incident categories and severity levels (e.g., availability incident, suspected unauthorized access, confirmed personal data breach, integration compromise, malware/attachment-related incident).
Establish an escalation path and points of contact during hypercare and any warranty/support period, including a clear process for reporting suspected incidents to the Client without delay.
Document incident handling steps: detection, triage, containment, eradication, recovery, and post-incident review.
b) Evidence preservation and communications
Ensure incident procedures include evidence preservation measures (relevant logs, audit records, configuration snapshots) in a manner consistent with privacy obligations and confidentiality requirements.
Provide incident communication templates and decision-support information (what happened, what data may be affected, actions taken, residual risk, recommended next steps), suitable for Client governance and any regulatory reporting requirements.
c) Breach notification support
Support the Client in assessing whether an incident is reportable and in preparing the information needed for any required notifications.
Ensure the solution’s logging and audit capabilities can produce timely evidence for breach assessment (scope, affected records, time window, users/actions involved).
d) Testing and readiness
During Phase 2 (preferably prior to go-live or during hypercare), conduct at least one incident response readiness exercise (e.g., tabletop simulation) with NPCT technical and GRM leads to validate contacts, escalation, and evidence capture procedures.
## 15.6 Data Residency and Cross-Border Transfers
The Supplier shall propose a hosting approach that meets data residency expectations and avoids unauthorized cross-border transfers of personal data. Where any cross-border processing is proposed, the Supplier shall describe safeguards and approvals required.
Minimum requirements include:
a) Hosting and residency controls
Clearly document where production data (including databases, attachments, backups, and logs) will be hosted and processed, and ensure this location is approved by the Client and consistent with applicable Kenya requirements.
Ensure that monitoring, support tooling, and third-party services (including email/SMS/USSD gateways) do not inadvertently replicate or store personal data in unapproved locations without Client approval.
b) Cross-border access and support safeguards
Where Supplier support resources are outside Kenya or where remote access is required, implement safeguards such as:
Restricted and time-bound privileged access
MFA for privileged sessions
VPN or secure administrative access methods
Full logging of privileged access sessions and actions
Explicit Client authorization for any exceptional access to sensitive grievance categories
Maintain a register of subprocessors and third-party services that may process grievance-related data, with a description of what data is processed, where, and under what protections.
c) Data transfer approvals and contractual safeguards
If any cross-border transfer/processing is necessary, the Supplier shall identify:
The lawful basis and required approvals
The technical safeguards (encryption, minimization, access controls)
The contractual safeguards with third parties (data processing terms, confidentiality, breach reporting obligations)
How the Client retains practical control and auditability over these arrangements.
# 16. INTEROPERABILITY & INTEGRATION
## 16.1 Integration with KUSP2 Website and Channels
The Supplier shall embed or link the grievance submission interface within the KUSP2 web presence as directed by the Client. The Supplier shall provide public-facing status tracking functionality based on grievance reference number where required, without exposing personal data.
To ensure a coherent user experience and reduce access barriers, the Supplier shall design the web integration so that the eGRM is reachable through clear, low-friction user journeys from the KUSP2 website and related online channels. The integration approach shall be technology-neutral but must support common implementation patterns such as:
Embedded experience (e.g., embedded form or secure frame) to allow grievance submission without leaving the KUSP2 web domain, where feasible and approved by the Client.
Linked experience (e.g., a prominent “Submit a Grievance” button/URL redirect) where embedding is not feasible due to security, hosting, or governance constraints.
Mobile-first navigation, ensuring the grievance submission and status tracking pages render correctly on smartphones and low-end devices commonly used by the public.
The Supplier shall ensure that the KUSP2 website integration supports, at minimum, the following functional and operational expectations:
Public-facing pages and content alignment
Clear entry points for: (i) submitting a grievance, (ii) tracking a grievance, and (iii) accessing guidance/FAQs on how the GRM works.
Consistent branding and trusted identity cues (programme name, official logos, and consistent styling), subject to Client brand guidance.
A configurable “service offline/maintenance” notice and alternative channels (e.g., hotline, in-person, email) when the portal is unavailable.
Public status tracking with privacy protection
Status tracking shall be based on the unique grievance reference number and a secondary verifier (e.g., email address, phone number, or another agreed identifier), to prevent unauthorized disclosure.
The public tracking view shall display only the minimum necessary information to be useful (e.g., current status, date last updated, next step guidance), and shall not expose staff names, internal notes, sensitive categories, or personal identifiers.
Where a grievance is marked confidential or sensitive (e.g., SEA/SH), the public status view shall be appropriately restricted/redacted, and the portal shall provide safe guidance on follow-up without revealing sensitive details.
Secure web integration controls
All web integrations shall enforce secure transport (HTTPS/TLS), prevent unintended cross-site data leakage, and follow safe embedding/linking practices (e.g., protection against clickjacking and unsafe third-party script dependencies).
Where the eGRM is embedded, the Supplier shall align with the Client’s security posture for embedding permissions (e.g., allowed domains) and ensure no insecure mixed-content behavior is introduced.
Accessibility and language in the integrated web journey
Web journeys originating from the KUSP2 website shall maintain accessible navigation, readable content, and clear error messages.
User-facing pages shall support, at minimum, English and Kiswahili, with a mechanism to extend to additional languages if directed by the Client.
## 16.2 Telephony/USSD/IVR/SMS/Email Gateways
The Supplier shall integrate with SMS/USSD gateways and a toll-free hotline/IVR as available, ensuring unified grievance registration and consistent acknowledgements. The Supplier shall support email intake and email notifications with proper logging and confidentiality controls. The Supplier shall ensure integrations are testable across mobile networks and include monitoring for delivery failures.
To operationalize multi-channel access while maintaining one authoritative “system of record,” the Supplier shall implement channel integrations so that all enabled channels (digital or assisted) result in:
Creation (or update) of a grievance record in a single unified register;
Assignment of a unique reference number at registration, used consistently across all follow-up communications; and
A complete channel activity trail (timestamps, message/call metadata where applicable, delivery outcomes, and audit logs), subject to role-based access controls.
Minimum requirements by channel include:
SMS (notifications and, where enabled, intake/supporting messages)
Support outbound acknowledgements and status notifications via SMS where the complainant has provided a phone number and consented to SMS contact.
Ensure SMS templates are privacy-aware and do not include sensitive grievance content; messages should generally include only the reference number, high-level status, and next steps.
Support delivery reporting (where supported by the gateway) and implement retry/queue mechanisms for transient failures, with clear operational visibility of failed deliveries.
USSD (where enabled by Client arrangements)
Implement USSD flows that are short and structured, recognizing session time limits and user constraints on feature phones.
Capture the minimum dataset required for registration (as defined in the standardized intake requirements), while enabling follow-up through assisted channels when deeper details are required.
Provide multilingual prompts (English/Kiswahili at minimum) and clear confirmation of successful submission, including the reference number.
Toll-free hotline and IVR (where enabled by Client arrangements)
Support staff-assisted intake through an operator/call handler interface where call agents record grievances directly into the eGRM (or via an integrated call centre tool where available).
Where integration with telephony systems is feasible, capture call metadata (e.g., time, agent identifier, call disposition) linked to the grievance record; avoid storing unnecessary call content beyond what is required for handling.
Ensure callers can receive the reference number verbally during the call and optionally via SMS where consent and capability exist.
Email intake and email notifications
Support outbound notifications by email using approved sender identities and controlled templates (including English/Kiswahili variants as required).
Where enabled, support inbound email processing to (i) create new grievances from designated inboxes and/or (ii) append messages to existing grievances using reference numbers or routing rules.
Implement safeguards for email processing such as spam handling, attachment controls, and prevention of accidental disclosure in email threads (e.g., careful handling of CC/BCC, controlled forwarding rules, and protection of sensitive data).
Across all channel integrations, the Supplier shall:
Align dependencies and responsibilities: recognize that regulatory/commercial arrangements (e.g., USSD short codes, toll-free provisioning, SMS bundles) are Client-led, while the Supplier provides technical integration and testing support.
Provide end-to-end testing and monitoring: implement test plans and monitoring checks for channel reliability (message delivery, queue backlogs, gateway timeouts, email bounces), and provide an operational approach for resolving failures.
Define continuity fallback procedures: where a channel is delayed or unavailable, ensure alternative intake routes remain operational and that grievances can still be registered and tracked in the unified register (including manual-assisted entry where required).
## 16.3 API Management and Webhooks
The Supplier shall provide documented APIs for integration and data exchange, including secure authentication and authorization. The Supplier shall support event notifications/webhooks as needed for integrations (e.g., dashboards, external reporting).
To enable interoperability with present and future systems (including reporting platforms and approved government digital services), the Supplier shall provide integration interfaces designed around open standards and secure governance controls, including:
Documented APIs (minimum expectations)
Provide clear API documentation describing endpoints, request/response schemas, authentication methods, error codes, and versioning approach.
Support common integration use cases such as: creation of a grievance record from an external channel, status updates, retrieval of summarized case data for dashboards, and controlled export of aggregated data for programme reporting.
Ensure API responses and exports can be governed to prevent disclosure of personal or sensitive data unless explicitly authorized.
Security, authorization, and governance controls
Use secure authentication mechanisms (e.g., API keys/tokens and/or other standards-based methods) and enforce authorization boundaries consistent with role-based access control and least privilege.
Support controls such as IP allowlisting (where applicable), rate limiting, and logging of API access for auditability.
Ensure integrations can be enabled/disabled without redevelopment, to support operational risk management and incident response.
Webhooks / event notifications (where required)
Provide a webhook mechanism for defined events such as: grievance created, assigned, escalated, overdue, resolved/closed, and key workflow transitions (as agreed with the Client).
Implement webhook delivery reliability features (e.g., retries, failure visibility, and protection against duplicate events through idempotent design).
Secure webhook payloads (e.g., signed payloads/secret validation) and ensure webhook outputs are privacy-aware and minimize sensitive data.
Integration readiness support
Provide a “sandbox” or test configuration for integrations, where feasible, to allow Client or third parties to validate connectivity and payloads without affecting production data.
Provide integration runbooks that define who monitors what, how failures are detected, and how issues are escalated during hypercare and warranty.
## 16.4 Data Import/Export and ETL
The Supplier shall provide tools to export data in standard formats for reporting and analysis. Where the Client requires migration of existing grievance records, propose an approach to import historical data with appropriate data quality and privacy controls.
To support programme reporting, audits, and continuity of operations, the Supplier shall implement robust, practical data exchange mechanisms, including:
Exports for reporting and oversight
Provide on-demand and scheduled exports in common, non-proprietary formats (e.g., CSV, spreadsheet-compatible formats, and PDF for printable outputs) consistent with reporting requirements.
Support both operational exports (for case management and supervision) and aggregate/anonymized outputs (for wider reporting and disclosure where permitted), with role-based restrictions and audit logging of exports.
Support export of a complete case file (where permitted) including key metadata, action history, communication logs, and attachments (or secure references), for internal reviews or lawful external requests.
Data quality controls for exports
Ensure exports use consistent field definitions aligned to the standardized intake dataset and data dictionary.
Include consistent timestamps/time zones and stable identifiers to support trend analysis and reconciliation.
Historical data migration (where required by the Client)
Propose a structured migration approach covering: (i) mapping legacy fields to the standardized dataset, (ii) data cleansing rules, (iii) de-duplication logic, (iv) validation checks, and (v) secure handling of sensitive records.
Provide import templates and procedures suitable for typical legacy sources (e.g., spreadsheets, registers transcribed into electronic form), including controlled handling of attachments/evidence where available.
Ensure that any migrated data is flagged as migrated, retains traceability to its source where appropriate, and is subject to the same access controls and audit requirements as newly created records.
ETL and downstream analytics enablement (where required)
Where the Client requires automated extraction into external reporting tools, define an ETL approach that supports incremental updates and stable reporting datasets while preserving privacy-by-design controls (e.g., separating PII from operational analytics datasets where appropriate).
Ensure any automated data transfers are logged, governed, and can be paused if required by the Client (e.g., during incidents or audits).
## 16.5 Single Sign-On and Directory Services
If the Client requires integration with existing identity management/directory services, the Supplier shall propose an approach consistent with open standards.
To strengthen security, simplify user management, and support sustainable operations, the Supplier shall be prepared to integrate the eGRM with Client-approved identity services where available. The Supplier’s approach shall remain technology-neutral and standards-based, and shall include:
Standards-based SSO options
Support common open standards (e.g., SAML 2.0 and/or OpenID Connect) where required by the Client’s identity environment.
Ensure SSO integration does not compromise the required role-based access controls; authentication (SSO) and authorization (roles/permissions) must remain clearly governed.
Account lifecycle and access governance
Support onboarding, role assignment, and de-provisioning workflows aligned to government operational practices, including timely disabling of accounts for staff transfers or departures.
Provide mechanisms for emergency access management (e.g., break-glass admin access) under strict audit controls, if agreed by the Client.
Role mapping and least-privilege implementation
Define how directory groups/attributes (where used) map to eGRM roles (national/county/municipal) and any additional scoping rules (e.g., a county user sees only their county’s cases).
Ensure segregation of duties is supported (e.g., separation of administration, case handling, and reporting roles).
Fallback authentication (where needed)
Where SSO is not available or not approved for all user groups, support a secure fallback approach (e.g., local accounts) with strong authentication controls, session security, and alignment to Client policies (including optional multi-factor authentication for privileged roles).
# 17. INFRASTRUCTURE & HOSTING
The e-GRM will operate as a national system supporting decentralized use across counties and municipalities. Infrastructure and hosting arrangements must therefore prioritize: (i) reliable public access for grievance submission and status checking, (ii) secure access for authorized staff at national/county/municipal levels, (iii) strong data protection and auditability controls for personal and sensitive grievance data, and (iv) scalability as usage expands. This chapter sets the minimum expectations for the hosting model, environments, resilience, and operational readiness consistent with the ToR’s non-functional requirements and compliance obligations.
## 17.1 Hosting Model and Environments
The Supplier shall propose a hosting model that (a) complies with applicable Government of Kenya requirements and Client directions, (b) supports the required environments, and (c) enables secure operations for a public-facing service with sensitive case handling. The hosting model proposal shall clearly state what is provided/managed by the Client versus the Supplier versus any third-party hosting provider.
a) Hosting model options and approval pathway
The Supplier shall present a hosting approach suitable for Government delivery, such as (illustratively) hosting in a government data centre or a government-approved cloud environment, subject to Client approval and data residency expectations. The proposal shall include the decision assumptions, required approvals, and lead times (e.g., for procurement/activation, security reviews, and connectivity).
The Supplier’s hosting recommendation shall be technology-neutral, but must explicitly address:
Data residency and cross-border transfer controls, including how unauthorized cross-border transfers of personal data are prevented or governed.
Security-by-design expectations for public sector systems (e.g., strong access control, MFA for privileged users, audit logs).
Operational sustainability, including how the Client can administer the environment post-handover and what recurring dependencies exist (licenses, hosting subscriptions, certificates, gateways).
b) Environment structure and separation of duties
The Supplier shall support environments appropriate to a controlled implementation and release process, at minimum including test/staging/production (and development where required). Environments shall be configured to reflect security and stability needs, including:
Production environment: restricted access, least-privilege administration, strong authentication, hardened configuration, and monitoring enabled by default.
Staging/pre-production: as close as feasible to production (configuration, integrations, performance characteristics) to support realistic UAT and cutover rehearsals.
Test: used for functional/regression/integration testing and defect reproduction, with controlled test data.
The Supplier shall define how configuration and releases are promoted between environments, including version control and rollback approach, consistent with change control expectations in the ToR.
c) Data handling rules for non-production environments
To protect complainants, the Supplier shall ensure production personal data is not used in non-production environments unless explicitly authorized by the Client and protected by equivalent controls. Where realistic datasets are needed, the Supplier shall propose anonymized/synthetic test datasets and controlled refresh procedures.
d) Network, domain, and connectivity prerequisites
The Supplier shall identify the practical prerequisites required to operationalize the solution, including (as applicable): DNS and domain naming approach, TLS certificates, inbound/outbound firewall rules, IP allowlists for administrative access, and connectivity requirements to enable channels (email/SMS/USSD/hotline) and integrations. Where government infrastructure readiness is uncertain, the Supplier shall include mitigations and sequencing options to avoid timeline slippage.
## 17.2 Capacity, Performance and Scalability
The Supplier shall define sizing assumptions and a scalability approach for users, grievance volume, reporting workloads, and attachment storage, and shall demonstrate that performance remains acceptable under expected loads. This includes both (i) day-to-day operational use by staff and (ii) periodic reporting/analytics workloads.
a) Capacity planning assumptions (baseline and growth)
The Supplier shall explicitly document sizing assumptions for the national and decentralized footprint (including growth over time), covering at minimum:
Number of active staff users (NPCT, county, municipal) and expected concurrent users in peak periods.
Expected grievance volumes (daily/weekly/monthly), including potential surge scenarios (e.g., during major project works or publicity campaigns).
Attachment volume and storage growth, including the expected proportion of grievances with attachments and average attachment size.
Reporting demand, including frequency and complexity of dashboards and exports (especially when filtering across counties/municipalities and long date ranges).
These assumptions should be realistic for a program operating across many jurisdictions (the broader KUSP2 context includes multiple counties and municipalities) and should support scale-up without redesign.
b) Performance expectations (minimum benchmarks)
The Supplier shall ensure responsive performance for core journeys (public intake, staff triage, search, reporting). As a minimum, performance testing and tuning shall be aligned to the BRD’s non-functional expectations, including responsiveness for key pages and timely completion of search/filter operations under expected peak load.
c) Scalability approach (without vendor lock-in)
The Supplier shall describe how the hosting/infrastructure design supports scaling while preserving technology neutrality. This may include, for example:
Ability to scale application capacity and background processing capacity independently (to handle intake spikes and notification bursts).
Database/storage scaling approach to maintain search/report performance as data grows.
Archiving strategy (aligned to the retention policy) to keep operational datasets performant while preserving auditability.
The Supplier shall include how scaling actions are executed (manual vs automated), expected downtime (if any), and the cost implications of scaling events.
d) Low-bandwidth and distributed access considerations
The Supplier shall ensure the solution remains usable and responsive in low-bandwidth contexts, including efficient page payloads, pagination for lists, and optimized reporting queries, consistent with the ToR emphasis on decentralized use and accessibility.
## 17.3 High Availability and Resilience
The Supplier shall provide redundancy, failover and disaster recovery arrangements appropriate to the criticality of grievance records, and shall define recovery objectives as applicable. High availability must cover not only the core application, but also supporting components (database/storage, integrations, and authentication mechanisms).
a) Availability target and maintenance controls
The Supplier shall propose service availability targets and supporting design. At minimum, availability planning should align with the BRD expectation for high availability for a public service channel, including planned maintenance controls such as an offline/maintenance mode page for the public portal.
b) Redundancy and failover design
The Supplier shall describe how redundancy is achieved at each layer, proportionate to the approved hosting model, including:
Application/service redundancy (to avoid single points of failure).
Database resiliency and data integrity protections.
Attachment storage resiliency.
Failover approach (automatic vs manual), including any service degradation expectations during failover.
c) Disaster recovery (DR) planning and objectives
The Supplier shall define RTO/RPO targets and the DR approach (e.g., backup-based recovery, secondary site, or equivalent). The DR plan shall include:
The scope of DR (application + database + attachments + configuration + logs).
Backup frequency, retention, and protection measures.
A practical DR test or restore rehearsal approach, proportionate to risk and timeline, to validate that recovery procedures are functional.
The broader design considerations highlight the importance of local hosting or compliant data localization and a DR plan with periodic backups to a secondary location where applicable.
d) Resilience for dependency delays and channel availability
Where channels or hosting prerequisites are subject to external lead times (e.g., in-country hosting readiness, SMS/USSD/hotline arrangements), the Supplier shall propose pragmatic sequencing and fallbacks that preserve a unified system of record (e.g., enabling web and assisted intake first while integration dependencies complete). This aligns with identified implementation risks and mitigations.
## 17.4 Observability and Monitoring
The Supplier shall implement monitoring across application, database, and integrations, and shall configure alerting for critical failures. Monitoring shall support both operational continuity and compliance needs (e.g., detecting suspicious access patterns, integration delivery failures, or storage saturation).
a) Monitoring scope (minimum)
The monitoring approach shall cover, at minimum:
Application health: uptime/availability checks, response time/latency, error rates, queue/backlog indicators (where applicable).
Database health: connectivity, replication status (if used), storage growth, slow queries impacting search/reporting.
Storage health: attachment storage capacity and access errors.
Integration health: delivery success/failure rates and latency for enabled channels (email, SMS/USSD, hotline), including monitoring for delivery failures across mobile networks.
b) Logging and operational dashboards
The Supplier shall ensure logs are sufficiently detailed for troubleshooting and incident response while respecting privacy and access controls. Operational dashboards for administrators shall include at least: service status, critical component status, integration status, and alert history. The log approach should support configurable verbosity and retention policies aligned with the BRD.
c) Alerting and escalation
The Supplier shall define alert thresholds and escalation paths consistent with the support model (hypercare/warranty), including:
Immediate alerts for outages, severe degradation, or data integrity risks.
Alerts for integration failure spikes (e.g., SMS gateway down, email delivery failures).
Alerts for security-relevant events (e.g., repeated failed logins, abnormal access attempts), routed to authorized security/admin roles.
## 17.5 Compliance and Auditability
Hosting and operations shall support auditability and compliance requirements, including access logs, configuration change tracking, and secure backups. The infrastructure design must enable the Client to demonstrate due diligence in protection of personal data and integrity of grievance case handling.
a) Data residency and lawful processing controls
The Supplier shall ensure the hosting approach satisfies Client-approved data residency expectations and supports compliance with Kenya data protection requirements, including controls to prevent unauthorized cross-border transfers and to support local hosting where required. This is a known compliance risk area and must be addressed explicitly in the hosting proposal and as-built documentation.
b) Audit logging and traceability
The Supplier shall ensure auditability at infrastructure and application levels, including:
Audit logs capturing key actions with actor identity, timestamps, and source information (e.g., IP address) to support investigations and accountability.
Administrative and privileged action logging (including configuration changes and access to sensitive data areas).
Time synchronization controls (so audit timestamps are reliable across components).
c) Secure backup handling and evidence of recoverability
The Supplier shall ensure backups are protected (confidentiality, integrity, access control) and auditable. Evidence of backup configuration and at least one successful restore demonstration (as appropriate to the timeline and hosting model) shall be included in implementation evidence packs and the final handover materials.
d) Compliance-ready documentation (“as-built” hosting pack)
The Supplier shall provide documentation sufficient for Client review, operational continuity, and audits. At minimum this should include:
High-level hosting architecture description (components and responsibilities).
Environment definitions and access control approach.
DR/backup plan and restore procedures.
Monitoring and alerting configuration summary.
Log retention configuration and audit log export approach.
These artifacts support the broader ToR requirement for compliance readiness evidence and sustainable operation.
# 18. ETHICS, INCLUSION & SEA/SH
## 18.1 Code of Conduct and Ethics
Supplier personnel shall adhere to ethical conduct standards, confidentiality obligations, and integrity requirements. Zero tolerance for retaliation or misuse of grievance data; ensure staff handling sensitive grievances are trained on confidentiality.
To operationalize these requirements during Phase 2 implementation and commissioning, the Supplier shall:
Adopt and enforce a project-specific Code of Conduct (CoC) applicable to all Supplier staff, subcontractors, and any third parties engaged by the Supplier who may access the eGRM environments, data, or implementation stakeholders. The CoC shall, at minimum, cover:
Respectful and non-discriminatory engagement with complainants and stakeholders.
Prohibition of retaliation, intimidation, or any action that could deter grievance submission or follow-up.
Prohibition of requesting or accepting any fees, gifts, favors, or benefits related to grievance handling or system access.
Conflict-of-interest disclosure requirements (including relationships with county/municipal actors, telecom providers, or other connected parties).
Confidentiality, privacy and appropriate information sharing (need-to-know only).
Mandatory reporting obligations for suspected misuse of grievance data or unethical conduct.
Ensure CoC onboarding and acknowledgment by:
Requiring written acknowledgment/signature of the CoC prior to any system access (including development, staging, or production access).
Conducting mandatory induction for all personnel at mobilization and refresher briefings before UAT and go-live when access widens to more users and locations.
Maintaining a register of staff who have completed ethics and confidentiality induction, linked to role assignment and access provisioning.
Embed ethical safeguards into access and delivery practices, including:
Separation of duties within the Supplier team (e.g., configuration vs. security review vs. QA sign-off) to reduce the risk of unreviewed changes or inappropriate access.
Prohibition of using personal email accounts, personal cloud storage, or unapproved devices for transferring project data, system exports, test datasets, or screenshots.
Controlled use of test data: where real grievance data is unavoidable (e.g., for pilot verification), it must be minimized, protected, and handled only in approved environments under Client direction.
Implement a misconduct reporting and response pathway for Supplier personnel and stakeholders engaged during implementation activities (training, pilots, UAT), including:
A clear escalation route to Supplier management and to the Client focal point for any alleged misconduct.
Time-bound investigation and corrective action steps proportionate to severity.
Documentation of actions taken, while protecting complainant confidentiality and avoiding retaliation.
These measures reinforce the ToR’s requirements for integrity, confidentiality, and responsible handling of grievance information across decentralized operational contexts.
## 18.2 SEA/SH Sensitive Intake and Handling Protocols
The solution and implementation approach shall support survivor-centred handling of SEA/SH-related grievances. This includes confidential intake options, restricted access, and workflows that enable referral to specialized services while minimizing capture of sensitive details in the system where required by agreed protocols.
To translate this requirement into implementable system controls and operational procedures, the Supplier shall ensure that the eGRM supports the following minimum SEA/SH-sensitive design and handling features:
Survivor-centred handling principles embedded in workflow design, including:
Safety and confidentiality as primary considerations in intake, processing, and follow-up.
Informed consent for any follow-up contact or referral recording (where contact details exist).
Non-discrimination and respectful communication standards in all scripts, templates, and staff training.
Confidential intake options across enabled channels, such that:
Public-facing interfaces (web, and any enabled USSD/SMS flows) clearly present a confidential reporting option and explain what information is (and is not) required.
Assisted channels (hotline and in-person staff-assisted intake) include a clear intake script to avoid probing questions and to prioritize immediate referral where appropriate.
Anonymous submission remains available and does not prevent case logging or referral actions.
Restricted access controls for SEA/SH and other highly sensitive cases, including:
A dedicated “sensitive” classification/flag (e.g., SEA/SH) that automatically applies restricted visibility and routing.
A need-to-know access model (e.g., access limited to designated focal persons at municipal/county level and designated NPCT roles), with all access attempts and views recorded in audit logs.
Controlled attachments for sensitive cases (e.g., restrict who can upload/view attachments; apply stronger download controls).
Minimization of sensitive detail capture, ensuring that:
The system can record that a SEA/SH-related grievance exists and that a referral or survivor support action has been initiated without requiring detailed narrative in free-text fields.
Where structured tracking is needed (e.g., referral date, receiving service provider category, follow-up consent status), these are implemented via controlled fields rather than unstructured notes.
Templates and guidance text in the staff console discourage recording unnecessary sensitive details.
Referral workflow support, including:
Ability to route a SEA/SH grievance to a designated focal role/queue for immediate handling.
Ability to record referral actions (date/time, referral type, high-level outcome status) without exposing sensitive content broadly.
Configuration of closure/referred statuses aligned to agreed procedures (e.g., “Referred to service provider” / “Closed—Referral completed”), while maintaining auditability.
Notification and communication safeguards, ensuring that:
Automated notifications for sensitive cases are either disabled by default or strictly controlled to avoid disclosure through insecure channels.
Any outbound messages (SMS/email) contain only the minimum necessary information (e.g., reference number and generic next steps) and avoid sensitive content.
Internal notifications are restricted to authorized roles only.
Testing and readiness evidence for SEA/SH controls, including:
Verification during QA and UAT that restricted cases cannot be accessed by non-authorized roles (including through exports, dashboards, search results, or attachments).
Evidence that audit trails capture sensitive-case access and handling actions reliably.
Confirmation that training includes SEA/SH handling do’s and don’ts for intake staff, supervisors, and administrators.
These measures align with the ToR’s requirement for confidentiality, restricted access, and survivor-centred approaches for SEA/SH-related grievances, and are consistent with the broader safeguards expectations described in KUSP2 implementation materials.
## 18.3 Accessibility and Inclusive Design
The Supplier shall implement accessible user interfaces and content, compatible with assistive technologies and usable via keyboard navigation. The Supplier shall implement multiple channels to reduce barriers for people without smartphones or internet access. The Supplier shall support English and Kiswahili and provide culturally appropriate user guidance.
To ensure accessibility is implemented as a verifiable and operational requirement (not only a design intention), the Supplier shall:
Apply accessibility-by-design across both public-facing and staff-facing components, including:
Keyboard operability for all essential user journeys (submit grievance, upload attachments, check status, acknowledge/assign/close a case).
Compatibility with common assistive technologies (screen readers and magnifiers) through correct labels, headings, focus order, and error messaging.
Clear, consistent navigation and form behavior to reduce cognitive load (especially important for first-time users).
Design for low-bandwidth and low-spec device use, including:
Responsive layouts that work on common smartphones and older devices.
Efficient page weight and optimized loading for limited connectivity environments.
Progressive enhancement where feasible (core functions available even when advanced features load slowly).
Ensure multilingual and culturally appropriate interaction, including:
Provision of English and Kiswahili for all user-facing pages, key instructions, and standard notification templates (acknowledgements, requests for additional information, closure messages).
Clear language selection and consistent terminology for grievance types/help topics.
Plain-language guidance on what information is needed and why, including a clear privacy notice and non-retaliation assurances.
Operationalize multi-channel accessibility, ensuring that:
Assisted intake processes (municipal offices, hotline) can register grievances on behalf of users who cannot access digital channels.
Where SMS/USSD is enabled, user flows minimize complexity and session length and avoid requesting unnecessary personal data.
Conduct accessibility validation as part of testing and acceptance, including:
Accessibility test cases included in the overall test plan and executed before UAT sign-off.
Where feasible, inclusion of users with disabilities in UAT or structured walkthrough testing, with documented findings and remediation actions.
These requirements reinforce the ToR and BRD expectations for disability-inclusive design, multilingual support, and practical accessibility for stakeholders with diverse connectivity and capability constraints.
## 18.4 Vulnerable and Marginalized Groups Considerations
The Supplier shall ensure anonymity options, confidentiality controls, and clear non-retaliation messaging to encourage uptake among vulnerable groups. The Supplier shall support pilot and training in diverse contexts, including where refugees or other vulnerable groups may be present, as directed by the Client.
To strengthen inclusion and trust in the eGRM across all KUSP2 operational contexts, the Supplier shall ensure the system and implementation activities support the following:
Safe access and trust-building measures, including:
Prominent non-retaliation messaging on public-facing interfaces and in staff scripts, emphasizing that grievances can be submitted without fear of reprisal.
Clear options for anonymous submission and confidential handling, without blocking case registration or follow-up actions.
Clear “free-of-charge” messaging (especially relevant for hotline and assisted intake) to reduce fear of hidden costs or gatekeeping.
Privacy-aware support for representation, ensuring that:
The system can capture grievances submitted by representatives (e.g., a community leader, guardian, advocate, or authorized organization) while recording authorization/relationship appropriately.
Visibility and consent considerations are respected (e.g., avoid exposing complainant identity beyond those who need it to resolve the issue).
Inclusive rollout, training, and pilot participation, including:
Support to Client-selected pilots that represent diverse municipal contexts (e.g., large urban area, smaller municipality, and refugee-hosting setting), ensuring the system is usable in each context.
Training materials and examples that address scenarios commonly affecting vulnerable groups (e.g., barriers to access, language, fear of retaliation, concerns about confidentiality).
Collaboration with the Client’s stakeholder engagement approach to ensure outreach materials and user guidance are adapted to local realities (literacy levels, preferred channels, and community trusted points of contact).
Operational safeguards for sensitive populations, including:
Configuration that limits who can access personally identifying data and prevents unnecessary disclosure in reports, dashboards, exports, and notifications.
Practical guidance for municipal and county users on how to handle grievances involving vulnerable persons (e.g., ensuring privacy during assisted intake, using appropriate contact preferences, and avoiding unnecessary data capture).
These measures support the intended inclusive, confidential, and non-retaliatory design of the KUSP2 eGRM and strengthen the system’s credibility and usability across all participating counties and municipalities, including settings where vulnerability and protection risks may be elevated.

# 22. CONTRACTUAL & LEGAL PROVISIONS
This section sets out the minimum contractual and legal provisions expected to be reflected in the RFQ/RFP and the final contract for Phase 2 implementation of the KUSP2 eGRM.
Given the nature of the assignment (a national-facing digital system handling personal and potentially sensitive grievances), the contract shall include clear protections for integrity, confidentiality, data governance, continuity of service, and the Client’s long-term operational control. These provisions are intended to (i) safeguard complainants and programme stakeholders, (ii) protect the Client from operational, legal and reputational risks, and (iii) ensure deliverables can be sustained after handover without undue vendor dependence.
## 22.1 Fraud and Corruption Provisions
The Supplier shall comply with all fraud and corruption provisions, integrity requirements, and ethical conduct obligations applicable to the procurement and contract. These provisions shall apply to the Supplier, its affiliates, personnel, agents, and any subcontractors or third parties engaged for implementation (e.g., SMS gateway integrator, hosting support provider). At minimum, the RFQ/RFP and contract shall:
Define prohibited practices and standards of conduct, including (as applicable) corruption, fraud, collusion, coercion, and obstruction, and explicitly prohibit facilitation payments, kickbacks, or improper influence related to (i) procurement, (ii) implementation activities, or (iii) handling of grievance data and outcomes.
Require the Supplier to maintain adequate records and audit trails supporting invoices, milestone claims, subcontractor payments, and any cost elements where transparency is required, and to retain such records for a contractually defined period.
Provide audit and inspection rights for the Client (and any authorized oversight bodies as permitted in the RFQ/RFP/contract) to review relevant contract performance records, including testing evidence, configuration/workflow evidence, and deliverable acceptance artifacts.
Require prompt notification and cooperation in investigations if credible allegations of fraud, corruption, data misuse, or unethical handling of grievances arise (including misuse of privileged access).
Include remedies and sanctions for breach, which may include rejection of deliverables, suspension of payments, termination, and any other remedies provided under the RFQ/RFP, the contract, and applicable procurement rules.
Where integrity issues relate specifically to grievance handling (e.g., attempted retaliation, tampering with records, or unauthorized disclosure), the Supplier shall support evidence preservation and provide relevant logs consistent with the agreed security and privacy controls.
## 22.2 Conflict of Interest and Eligibility
Eligibility and conflict of interest requirements shall apply as specified in the RFQ/RFP and contract, and shall be treated as continuing obligations throughout the contract period. In addition to standard procurement eligibility requirements, the RFQ/RFP and contract should require that:
The Supplier submits full disclosure of any actual, potential, or perceived conflicts of interest, including relationships with public officials, programme stakeholders, telecom providers involved in integrations, or parties that may be subject to grievances within the system.
The Supplier warrants that it (and any subcontractors) is not subject to restrictions that would make it ineligible under the applicable procurement framework or any other binding eligibility rules stated in the RFQ/RFP.
The Supplier maintains independence and impartiality in implementing the eGRM, including neutrality in configuring categories, routing, and reporting, and avoiding any configuration that could unfairly influence grievance outcomes.
Conflicts that arise during implementation are subject to a defined mitigation and escalation process, including potential replacement of conflicted personnel, additional oversight controls, or other measures determined by the Client under the contract.
## 22.3 Confidentiality and Non-Disclosure
The Supplier shall treat all project information—including grievance data—as confidential, and shall comply with non-disclosure requirements and data protection obligations as defined in the RFQ/RFP and contract. Confidentiality provisions shall be structured to reflect the sensitivity of grievance information and the multi-level governance model (national/county/municipal). At minimum, the contract should:
Define confidential information broadly to include: grievance records (content, attachments, identity/contact details), user credentials, system configuration, security architecture, integration credentials (API keys, gateway tokens), and any non-public programme documents or operational data.
Require need-to-know access controls for Supplier personnel, including role-based access aligned to implementation responsibilities (e.g., segregation between developers, testers, and support personnel where feasible).
Require Supplier personnel and subcontractors to sign confidentiality undertakings and comply with the Client’s instructions on controlled handling of sensitive grievances (including SEA/SH-related cases and other restricted categories).
Specify secure handling requirements for all data and artifacts during implementation (e.g., secure file transfer, encrypted storage, controlled sharing of test datasets), including explicit restrictions on copying production data into non-approved environments and restrictions on use of real grievance data for demos or training unless explicitly authorized and anonymized where appropriate.
Define permitted exceptions to confidentiality (e.g., disclosure required by law) and the process for such disclosures (notice to Client, minimization, lawful basis, controlled disclosure).
Require return, deletion, or verified destruction of Client data and confidential information upon contract completion or termination, subject to any lawful retention obligations and the Client’s operational continuity needs.
## 22.4 Intellectual Property and Ownership
The contract shall define intellectual property (IP), licensing, and ownership arrangements in a manner that supports technology neutrality while preserving the Client’s ability to operate, maintain, and evolve the eGRM after handover. At minimum, the contract should provide that:
Ownership of grievance data and all programme-generated operational data remains with the Client at all times, including during hosting and while in transit through enabled channels and integrations.
The Client receives all necessary rights to use, administer, and maintain the solution, including perpetual (or contractually sufficient) licenses for any third-party components required for continued operation, subject to the agreed licensing model.
All implementation artifacts required for sustainability are handed over, including (as applicable): configuration workbooks, workflow definitions, forms and data dictionaries, templates, integration specifications, deployment scripts, environment configuration parameters, and administrator documentation.
For any custom development delivered under the contract (if applicable), the contract clearly defines whether source code ownership transfers to the Client or whether the Client receives an irrevocable license with rights to modify and maintain; in either case, the contract shall ensure the Client is not locked out of future maintenance or enhancement.
All third-party/open-source dependencies are disclosed in a license inventory, with confirmation that licenses permit the intended government use and do not impose restrictions that would hinder ongoing operation or lawful publication of required reports.
The Supplier provides a practical exit/transition posture, including complete export of data and configuration in open formats sufficient for transition to another provider or platform if required by the Client in future.

IP infringement indemnity (as applicable) protecting the Client against third-party claims that the delivered solution or its components infringe intellectual property rights, with clear obligations for defense, mitigation, and replacement where needed.

To ensure the above dataset is implemented as a usable national standard (not merely as a set of fields), the Supplier shall incorporate the following in configuration and documentation:
Data dictionary and mapping evidence
Produce and maintain a data dictionary / configuration workbook mapping each standardized field to the solution’s internal field name(s), data type, validation rule(s), visibility (public vs staff-only), and reporting availability. Channel-specific minimum capture rules
Ensure all channels create a single grievance record with the standardized dataset applied, while allowing practical channel adaptations:
Web: enforce all required fields at point of submission (including Consent to Personal Data Use); provide clear error messages and guidance text for accessibility and comprehension.
Assisted intake (in-person/phone): enable staff to complete mandatory fields and record when information is unavailable (e.g., anonymous, unreachable). Include a staff prompt/check to confirm details back to the complainant (where feasible) to improve data accuracy.
SMS/USSD (if enabled): implement a minimal viable capture flow aligned to mandatory fields and usability constraints (short sessions), while preserving core meaning (especially location, category, and description/summary). Ensure consent and privacy handling aligns to agreed procedures and channel limitations.
Conditional fields and validation logic
Configure conditional fields so they are enforced only when applicable:
Representative fields and Consent of Affected Person shall be required when “submitted by representative” is indicated.
Resolution/closure fields (e.g., Resolution Outcome/Summary, Date of Resolution, Complainant Informed of Outcome, Complainant Response) shall become mandatory at the appropriate workflow stage(s).
Configure validation rules for formats (email, phone), mandatory selection constraints (county/municipality lists), and controlled vocabularies (category list values).
Reference data governance
Configure and maintain controlled dropdown lists (e.g., county, municipality, categories) using administrative tools so they can be updated without redevelopment.
Maintain auditability for reference data changes (who changed what and when) to protect data consistency for longitudinal reporting.
Consent, privacy notice linkage, and confidentiality controls
Implement explicit consent capture (timestamped) and ensure it is linked to the relevant privacy notice/version presented at intake.
Ensure role-based controls restrict access to personal data, and enforce additional restrictions for sensitive categories (e.g., GBV/SEA/SH), consistent with agreed protocols (restricted visibility, limited free-text, safe reporting outputs).
Attachments and evidence handling
Configure accepted file types, size limits, and storage controls proportionate to security and bandwidth constraints.
Ensure attachments inherit the grievance record’s access restrictions and are included in audit logs (upload/view/download events, where supported).
Data quality controls for monitoring and reporting
Ensure required fields are available for reporting and disaggregation (channel, county, municipality, category, status, dates).
Provide configuration to reduce duplicate data entry (e.g., reference number search, duplicate detection prompts) and to standardize “unknown/not provided” handling for optional fields, to keep datasets analytically usable over time.
## 24.3 Appendix B – Illustrative Workflow Scenarios (Acceptance Scenarios)
The scenarios below are the minimum illustrative acceptance scenarios for demonstrations, testing, and UAT sign-off. The Supplier shall translate these scenarios into UAT scripts and test cases, and provide evidence (screenshots/logs/test results) showing that each scenario works end-to-end, including auditability and appropriate access controls.
Web grievance submission with consent, acknowledgement, assignment, resolution and closure
Expected flow: public submission → consent capture → system generates reference number → acknowledgement recorded/sent → routing/assignment to correct unit → action logging and communications → resolution summary recorded → closure with required fields completed.
Minimum evidence: reference number format demonstration; consent record; message log (acknowledgement and resolution communication); audit log entries for assignment/status changes; closure field validation.
Anonymous grievance submission (web or assisted), with reference number and status tracking without disclosure of identity
Expected flow: submission without identity/contact → reference number generated and provided → workflow proceeds internally → status tracking available via reference number (without exposing personal data) → closure marked with “N/A (anonymous)” where required.
Minimum evidence: system allows blank complainant name/contact; permissions prevent staff from adding identifying info without authorization; status tracking returns only permitted details; reporting aggregates do not expose identity.
Phone-assisted grievance intake logged by an officer, including read-back confirmation and acknowledgement
Expected flow: complainant calls → officer logs grievance using standardized dataset → officer confirms key details (read-back) → reference number provided verbally and/or via configured acknowledgement channel → routing and SLA countdown begins.
Minimum evidence: assisted intake form supports mandatory fields; submission channel captured as Phone Call; acknowledgement recorded; call notes stored in communications log (staff-only where appropriate).
Grievance requiring referral (e.g., corruption allegation) with controlled information sharing and status tracking
Expected flow: grievance captured and categorized appropriately → restricted routing/referral to designated unit → controlled access ensures only authorized roles can view full content → complainant receives safe updates (no sensitive detail disclosed) → referral outcome recorded and tracked to closure.
Minimum evidence: referral/escalation details captured; restricted visibility enforced; safe notification templates validated; audit log shows access and transfer actions.
SEA/SH-related grievance intake using a confidential pathway with restricted access and referral, with minimal sensitive data captured
Expected flow: confidential intake option selected → system applies sensitive classification and restricted access → referral workflow triggers per agreed protocol → minimal data captured in free text; structured fields used for tracking referrals/actions → reporting limited to aggregate outputs.
Minimum evidence: restricted roles/queues demonstrated; notification suppression/redaction demonstrated; audit logs capture access; exports/reporting show aggregation without personally identifying or highly sensitive narrative.
These scenarios shall be used not only to validate functional completeness, but also to validate privacy-by-design, role-based access, auditability, data quality rules, and multi-channel consistency (single record of truth with consistent reference numbering and status tracking).
## 24.4 Appendix C – System Requirements Specification (Software + Hardware)
This Appendix provides the consolidated System Requirements Specification (SRS) for the KUSP2 e-GRM. It translates hosting and environment expectations that are currently described across the ToR and BRD into an explicit, procurement-ready baseline so that quotations can be priced consistently and implementation can be accepted against measurable evidence.
The requirements below are technology-neutral and define minimum outcomes and minimum operational baselines. Where bidders propose alternative architectures (e.g., managed services, container platforms, or SaaS), the bidder shall demonstrate equivalence and provide the evidence items listed in Section 24.4.8 of this Appendix.
### 24.4.1 Purpose and scope of this System Requirements Specification
This Appendix shall be read together with the main ToR (especially Sections 5, 11–19 and 21) and is intended to clarify the system-level requirements that affect hosting, infrastructure, endpoint compatibility, software prerequisites, and acceptance evidence. It does not replace functional requirements; instead, it defines the minimum operating conditions under which the functional requirements must be delivered.
For clarity, this System Requirements Specification covers the following minimum areas:
Acceptable deployment/hosting models and bidder obligations (Section 24.4.2).
Responsibility split between the Client, Supplier, and third parties (Section 24.4.3).
Minimum environments and environment separation rules (Section 24.4.4).
Minimum infrastructure/hosting requirements (hardware) in a technology-neutral manner (Section 24.4.5).
Client endpoint requirements for staff and public users (Section 24.4.6).
Software dependencies and platform prerequisites, including integration prerequisites (Section 24.4.7).
Acceptance evidence checklist (Section 24.4.8).
Bidders shall use this Appendix as the baseline for their technical proposals and shall clearly state any assumptions, dependencies, or variances. Any variance from a mandatory baseline requirement shall be treated as a deviation and may render a quotation non-responsive, unless the RFQ/RFP explicitly allows and evaluates alternatives.
### 24.4.2 Deployment model statement (acceptable models and bidder obligations)
The e-GRM is a public-facing national system with sensitive case handling. The solution shall therefore be deployed in a manner that supports secure operations, data protection obligations, availability expectations, and sustainable administration after handover. For procurement clarity, the following deployment models are acceptable, subject to Client approval and compliance with applicable Government of Kenya requirements.
Model A – Government-Hosted (preferred): Hosting is provided in a Government data centre or Government-approved cloud environment. The Supplier implements, configures, and hands over the solution and all operational artifacts to the Client.
Model B – Supplier-Hosted SaaS (conditionally acceptable): The Supplier hosts the solution as a managed service, subject to data residency requirements, security/compliance controls, and evidence obligations that allow the Client to verify compliance and retain access to required data and logs.
Model C – On-premises hardware supplied (only if confirmed in the RFQ/RFP): The Supplier supplies on-premises hardware and deploys the solution on that hardware. This model shall only be proposed if the RFQ/RFP explicitly requests supply of hardware as part of the goods package or allows an on-premises option.
Bidders shall propose at least one deployment model and provide a clear architecture summary (logical and deployment views), including environment separation, how data residency is ensured, and how the Client will administer the solution post-handover. Where a bidder proposes Model B or Model C, the bidder shall clearly state all service dependencies, recurring costs, and any conditions required for compliance and acceptance.

### 24.4.4 Minimum environments and environment separation rules
The e-GRM shall be implemented and operated using a controlled release process that protects complainant data, supports realistic testing, and enables auditable promotion of changes. At minimum, the following environments shall be established and maintained for the duration of implementation and warranty/hypercare, unless the Client approves an equivalent controlled approach.
Development (Dev) – required where configuration/customization is needed; may be combined with Test only with Client approval and where controls remain adequate.
Test (QA) – used for functional, integration and regression testing, defect reproduction, and security/accessibility verification using controlled test data.
Staging / Pre-production (UAT) – configured as close as feasible to Production (including integrations and performance characteristics) to support UAT and cutover rehearsals.
Production (Prod) – live environment for public access and official case handling, with restricted access, hardened configuration, monitoring enabled by default, and controlled administrative operations.
Environment separation rules shall be applied consistently. Production personal data shall not be used in non-production environments unless explicitly authorized by the Client and protected by equivalent controls. Where realistic datasets are required, the Supplier shall use anonymized, masked or synthetic datasets and document the refresh procedure.
The Supplier shall define and implement a release promotion process (Dev → Test → Staging → Production) that includes: version control for configuration and any code artifacts, approval gates for promotion to Production, rollback procedures, and an auditable change log that can be reviewed during UAT and handover.
### 24.4.5 Minimum infrastructure / hosting requirements (hardware baseline)
Even where the procurement is primarily software-focused, a minimum infrastructure baseline is required so that the Client and bidders can plan and price consistently. The requirements below are expressed in a technology-neutral manner ("or equivalent"), and bidders may propose alternative sizing and architectures provided they can demonstrate that performance, availability, security and auditability requirements in the ToR are met.
a) Option A – Government-hosted (Client-provided hosting baseline)
Where the Client provides hosting (Government data centre or Government-approved cloud), the Supplier shall, at minimum, specify and validate the infrastructure sizing required to meet the ToR’s functional and non-functional requirements. The table below provides the minimum starting baseline for initial go-live; the Supplier shall refine sizing during mobilization based on agreed volumes and shall demonstrate adequacy through testing and/or architecture evidence.
| Component | Production (minimum starting baseline) | Test/Staging (minimum baseline) | Notes / evidence required |
| Application / web tier compute | >= 4 vCPU and >= 16 GB RAM (or equivalent scalable service) | >= 2 vCPU and >= 8 GB RAM (or equivalent) | Must support horizontal scaling; include sizing worksheet and scale-out approach. |
| Background processing / notifications | Included within app tier or separate worker capacity as needed | As per Test/Staging needs | If separate workers/queues are used, include capacity assumptions for notification bursts. |
| Database tier compute | >= 8 vCPU and >= 32 GB RAM (or equivalent managed DB service) | >= 4 vCPU and >= 16 GB RAM (or equivalent) | DB must support encryption at rest, backups, auditability, and performance tuning. |

TLS enforcement for all user and administrative access, including secure configuration and certificate renewal procedures.
Administrative access controls, such as IP allowlisting and/or VPN access for privileged administration, consistent with Government security requirements.
Reverse proxy and/or web application firewall (WAF) expectations (where available/required), including protection against common web threats and rate limiting as appropriate for a public service.
The Supplier shall confirm high availability and disaster recovery arrangements in the approved hosting environment, including backup frequency, retention, and restore testing. b) Option B – Supplier-hosted SaaS (minimum service requirements)
Where a Supplier-hosted SaaS model is proposed (subject to Client approval), the Supplier shall meet or exceed the following minimum service requirements. These requirements are intended to protect data, ensure continuity for a public service, and enable the Client to verify compliance and retain access to required data and audit evidence.
At minimum, the Supplier-hosted service shall provide:

Documented backup policy, including backup frequency, retention, encryption, and at least one successful restore test record prior to go-live.
Disaster recovery capability with documented RTO/RPO commitments and a proportionate DR rehearsal or recovery test approach.
Data residency and cross-border transfer controls consistent with applicable Government of Kenya requirements and Client directions, including explicit controls that prevent unauthorized cross-border transfer of personal data.
Vulnerability and patch management processes, including timely security patching and remediation of identified vulnerabilities.
Secure administrative access controls (least privilege, MFA for privileged roles, audit logs) and retention of audit logs for compliance review.

### 24.4.6 Client endpoint requirements (devices, browsers, and connectivity)
To reduce acceptance disputes and ensure equitable access, the minimum endpoint requirements below apply to both staff-facing and public-facing components of the e-GRM. The Supplier shall design, configure and test the solution against these baseline endpoints, and shall document any additional endpoint requirements introduced by the proposed solution.
At minimum, the solution shall support the following endpoint baseline:
Web browsers: the latest two major versions of Google Chrome, Microsoft Edge and Mozilla Firefox for staff and public portal access. Where Safari is required for iOS devices, the solution shall also support current supported Safari versions for the public portal.
Devices: common smartphones and standard desktops/laptops used in county/municipal offices. The public portal shall be responsive for mobile use; the staff console shall be usable on standard desktop/laptop screens and, where offered, on tablets.
Screen resolution: responsive design down to typical smartphone screen sizes, and full usability on standard office resolutions (minimum 1366x768 for staff console unless an equivalent baseline is agreed).
Connectivity: usable under low-bandwidth conditions (as required by the ToR), including efficient page payloads, pagination for long lists, and avoidance of unnecessary heavy assets.
No dependency on proprietary plugins: access shall not require browser plugins that are not commonly available in Government environments.
The Supplier shall confirm any special requirements for assisted intake stations (e.g., scanners for document intake, headsets for call handling where a call centre is used) only where such items are explicitly included in scope. End-user devices and connectivity remain Client-provided unless the RFQ/RFP explicitly includes them as part of the goods package.
### 24.4.7 Software dependencies and platform prerequisites (technology-neutral)
The e-GRM solution may be implemented using different technology stacks. However, to remain procurement-ready and to enable consistent acceptance, bidders shall meet the minimum software capability requirements below and shall declare any external dependencies introduced by their proposed solution. Where a bidder proposes a managed service alternative, the bidder shall demonstrate equivalence to these requirements.
At minimum, the proposed solution shall satisfy the following software prerequisites and capability requirements:
Server platform: compatible with commonly used Government hosting environments (Linux and/or Windows Server) or equivalent managed runtime services. Any required runtime components (e.g., application servers, container runtimes) shall be identified and included in the implementation scope.
Database capability: database layer must support encryption at rest, strong access control, backup/restore, audit logging, and scaling to meet the ToR’s reporting and search requirements. Technology choice may vary, but required capabilities are mandatory.
Identity and access management: role-based access control (RBAC) is mandatory, with multi-factor authentication (MFA) for privileged roles. If SSO is used, the solution shall support integration to an approved identity provider. Where SSO is not available, secure local authentication with strong controls shall be provided.
Logging and audit trail: the solution shall provide audit logs for key actions (case access, status changes, assignment, data exports, admin changes) with retention and exportability aligned to the ToR and compliance requirements.
Integration prerequisites: the solution shall support integration to (i) an SMTP email relay for email notifications, (ii) an SMS gateway/aggregator for SMS notifications and (where enabled) intake, (iii) a USSD aggregator for USSD flows (where enabled), and (iv) hotline/IVR interfaces where hotline integration is included. Where channel integrations are staged, the solution shall still maintain a single system of record and consistent reference numbering across channels.
Interoperability: the solution shall provide APIs and/or export mechanisms to support integration and reporting, using common secure standards (e.g., RESTful APIs over HTTPS) and non-proprietary export formats (e.g., CSV, XLSX, PDF) as required by the ToR.
Bidders shall explicitly state any licensing constraints, proprietary components, or third-party services that are required for the solution to operate (including costs and renewal conditions). The Client will not accept hidden dependencies that create operational lock-in or prevent the Client from meeting its compliance obligations.
### 24.4.8 Acceptance evidence checklist (minimum compliance and handover evidence)

The Client may request additional evidence proportionate to risk, but the items below are the minimum.
At minimum, the Supplier shall provide the following acceptance evidence artifacts:
Architecture diagrams: logical architecture and deployment architecture for the approved hosting model, including environment separation and key components (application, database, storage, integrations).
Environment inventory: list of environments (Dev/Test/Staging/Prod) with access controls, URLs/endpoints, and administrative account management approach (under controlled accounts).
Sizing worksheet: documented sizing assumptions and capacity plan (users, grievance volumes, storage growth) and how scaling is performed; include any performance testing results used to validate sizing.
Backup configuration summary and restore test evidence: backup schedule, retention, encryption, and at least one successful restore test record prior to go-live.
Disaster recovery procedure and test/rehearsal evidence (proportionate): documented DR approach, RTO/RPO commitments where applicable, and evidence of a DR rehearsal or recovery validation appropriate to the approved model.
Security configuration summary: hardening controls, RBAC/MFA implementation, audit logging configuration, and secure configuration of integrations (SMTP/SMS/USSD/hotline as applicable).
Vulnerability assessment results and remediation summary: scan/test results (as applicable), prioritized findings, and evidence that critical/high findings have been remediated or formally accepted by the Client.

The evidence pack shall be delivered in an organized and reviewable format (index and filenames), and shall be referenced in the Handover Dossier. Failure to provide the minimum evidence may delay acceptance and go-live approval until compliance is demonstrated.
